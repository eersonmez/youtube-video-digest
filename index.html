Medieval Europe struggled with reconciling the image of good emperors like Trajan with the concept that they might be in hell due to their paganism. This reflects how Medieval and Renaissance Europe selectively adopted elements from both pagan antiquity and Christianity to construct their idealized versions of the past.

Above the Cloud: Building Data Centers in Space - Richard Campbell - NDC Copenhagen 2026

Campbell treats space data centers as a tempting but mostly impractical idea. He dismisses huge orbital solar-panel compute farms as visually attractive but hard to assemble, protect, power, cool, and network. A narrower possibility is running inference, not model training, on rack-scale hardware in orbit, because building large multi-rack training clusters needs bandwidth and latency closer to terrestrial Infiniband than current orbital laser links can provide. The talk walks through mass, power, heat rejection, solar/cooling arrays, Starlink-like optical links, debris/Kessler risk, and launch economics. His bottom line is skeptical for Earth-serving low-Earth-orbit AI compute: power and land constraints on Earth are partly hype/investor narrative, orbital collision risk is real, and the workloads are limited. Space compute makes more sense when the users and data are already in space, such as around the Moon or future space infrastructure.

  • Training giant models in space is treated as unrealistic; inference is the only plausible near-term workload Campbell explores.
  • A single AI rack is framed as liftable but power-hungry and heat-heavy, requiring substantial solar and radiator/cooling mass.
  • Orbital networking is a mismatch for tightly coupled training: current optical links can be high throughput, but not terrestrial low-latency fabric.
  • Debris detection, traffic coordination, and Kessler syndrome are major system risks, especially as satellite counts rise.
  • He argues the AI data-center power panic is partly extrapolated hype: power plants and new data centers take years, and much current capacity was already planned.
  • The stronger long-term case is compute near off-Earth activity, especially lunar/space infrastructure, not low-Earth-orbit compute for ordinary Earth users.

What's the Best Blood Pressure for Older Adults?

Dr. Leonid Kim argues that blood-pressure targets for older adults should not be reduced to one universal number. He explains how the SPRINT trial drove enthusiasm for lower systolic targets by showing fewer cardiovascular events and a 27% reduction in all-cause mortality in the intensive-treatment group, but he emphasizes that the trial population was selected and relatively resilient. He also notes that SPRINT used unattended automated office measurements that can read about 7-10 mmHg lower than ordinary clinic readings, which is why a trial target below 120 may translate closer to a clinical target around 130. The practical framework weighs functional status, frailty, medication burden, fall risk, kidney injury, syncope, cognitive slowing, and diastolic pressure. For healthy active adults around 65-80, he starts the conversation near a systolic target of 130 and may go lower if tolerated; for frailer or older adults, he is more conservative. The repeated message is to discuss individualized targets with a doctor rather than self-adjust medications.

  • SPRINT supports lower targets for selected, healthier older adults, but it should not be applied mechanically to everyone.
  • Measurement method matters: SPRINT's unattended automated readings may correspond to higher real-world clinic readings.
  • Overly aggressive lowering can cause falls, hip fracture, kidney injury, passing out, medication interactions, or cognitive slowing.
  • The framework starts with function, frailty, medication load, symptoms, kidney status, and the full blood-pressure reading, not just systolic pressure.
  • Targets are presented as starting points for clinician discussion, not personal medical instructions.
