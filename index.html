Andrew Huberman interviews UC Davis psychologist Paul Eastwick about evidence from attraction, speed-dating, and relationship studies. The central theme is that dating-app and profile-based selection exaggerates consensus desirability: the same highly visible people get most swipes and messages, but once people spend time together face to face, idiosyncratic taste and shared experience matter more. Eastwick argues that many popular gender stories are weaker in real interactions than in questionnaires or online sorting. In his data, men and women both show some attraction to younger partners in early dates, and both value ambition or financial stability more similarly than stereotypes suggest. For long-term relationships, the episode emphasizes perceived similarity, feeling that a partner has irreplaceable qualities, reciprocal support, and sexual or physical intimacy. Attraction to other people is framed as normal; risk rises when attraction is repeatedly pursued through disclosure, private intimacy, and follow-through. Practical advice is conservative: create real contexts for interaction, treat apps as a starting point rather than a compatibility engine, notice whether a relationship helps both people become more stable and connected, and avoid fatalism when desire or compatibility feels imperfect but still workable.

How I use LLMs

His overall advice is to experiment across tools, choose stronger models for professional work when the cost is justified, provide concrete examples, and avoid blindly trusting generated tables, code, or claims.

  • Model selection matters: cheap/fast models are often enough, but professional coding and hard reasoning can justify paying for stronger models.
  • Use search when information is recent, obscure, or likely to change; use uploaded documents when the answer must reference specific context.
  • Treat LLM output as a first draft and check it, especially for tables, numbers, code, and factual claims.
  • Tool use extends models beyond next-token text: Python handles arithmetic/code execution, artifacts can create custom local apps, and coding agents can edit projects iteratively.
