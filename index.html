Above the Cloud: Building Data Centers in Space - Richard Campbell - NDC Copenhagen 2026

Campbell treats space data centers as a tempting but mostly impractical idea. He dismisses huge orbital solar-panel compute farms as visually attractive but hard to assemble, protect, power, cool, and network. A narrower possibility is running inference, not model training, on rack-scale hardware in orbit, because building large multi-rack training clusters needs bandwidth and latency closer to terrestrial Infiniband than current orbital laser links can provide. The talk walks through mass, power, heat rejection, solar/cooling arrays, Starlink-like optical links, debris/Kessler risk, and launch economics. His bottom line is skeptical for Earth-serving low-Earth-orbit AI compute: power and land constraints on Earth are partly hype/investor narrative, orbital collision risk is real, and the workloads are limited. Space compute makes more sense when the users and data are already in space, such as around the Moon or future space infrastructure.

  • Training giant models in space is treated as unrealistic; inference is the only plausible near-term workload Campbell explores.
  • A single AI rack is framed as liftable but power-hungry and heat-heavy, requiring substantial solar and radiator/cooling mass.
  • Orbital networking is a mismatch for tightly coupled training: current optical links can be high throughput, but not terrestrial low-latency fabric.
  • Debris detection, traffic coordination, and Kessler syndrome are major system risks, especially as satellite counts rise.
  • He argues the AI data-center power panic is partly extrapolated hype: power plants and new data centers take years, and much current capacity was already planned.
  • The stronger long-term case is compute near off-Earth activity, especially lunar/space infrastructure, not low-Earth-orbit compute for ordinary Earth users.
