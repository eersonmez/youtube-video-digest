Richard Campbell evaluates whether AI data centers in space are technically and economically plausible, especially in low Earth orbit.

NDC Conferences · 2026-06-19T11:14:37+00:00

Above the Cloud: Building Data Centers in Space - Richard Campbell - NDC Copenhagen 2026

Campbell walks through the engineering bill for putting AI compute in orbit and lands on a skeptical but nuanced conclusion. A single high-power GPU rack could be made plausible on paper, but only with large solar arrays, substantial radiator mass, optical links, careful orbit choice, and launch economics that make scaling hard. He argues the most realistic workload is inference, not training, because orbital links cannot behave like low-latency Infiniband between racks. Sun-synchronous orbit can avoid battery mass by staying in sunlight, but it narrows usable orbital space and worsens collision-management pressure. The talk's strongest warning is orbital carrying capacity: existing and planned communication constellations already push LEO toward congestion, and adding large AI satellites raises debris and Kessler-syndrome risk. He suspects some space-data-center hype is tied to SpaceX valuation narratives, while still expecting compute to matter later around the Moon or other off-Earth settlements where local processing makes sense.

  • Geostationary orbit is visually appealing but impractical for this use case because it is harder to reach, more exposed, and higher latency.
  • Low Earth orbit has useful latency, but inclination, station keeping, collision avoidance, and constant tracking become central constraints.
  • A 135 kW GPU payload implies large mass: roughly tons of solar panels and around twice that in radiator/cooling equipment, with a total satellite in the 12-15 metric ton range.
  • Communication is a bottleneck: Starlink-style optical mesh links around 100 Gbit/s are useful, but cannot substitute for sub-millisecond Infiniband between training racks.
  • The realistic compute model is inference near the network edge, not large-scale model training in orbit.
  • Sun-synchronous orbit can eliminate large batteries by keeping the satellite in sunlight, but constrains available orbital slots and makes collision management harder.
  • Campbell estimates large AI satellites would be expensive mostly because of launch: the vehicle might be tens of millions, while lift could be higher than the satellite cost.
  • Existing LEO growth is already intense: Starlink, Amazon Kuiper, and Chinese mega-constellations could consume much of the estimated carrying capacity.
  • Debris management is the hard safety problem: rocket bodies, defunct satellites, untracked small debris, and Kessler-syndrome cascades all become more serious with more traffic.
  • For Eren: treat space-AI-data-center claims as a systems-engineering and capital-markets story, not just a cool hardware story; ask what workload, latency, mass, launch cadence, debris policy, and downlink capacity actually support it.
