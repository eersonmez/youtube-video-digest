Why Italians Saw the Pope Very Differently - Ada Palmer

Ada Palmer argues that in this period the pope was not only a spiritual leader but also a temporal ruler who fought wars, even against other Catholics. She emphasizes that geography shaped how Catholics perceived him. For people in distant places like Denmark or Iceland, the pope appeared remote, majestic, and sacred, encountered mainly through impressive papal envoys, ceremonies, and public honors. For Italians, however, the pope was a familiar local political figure: someone whose youth, family, vices, rivalries, and personal history were widely known. Italians also directly experienced the patronage and disruption that followed a new pope’s rise, as his relatives and allies gained offices while others were displaced. This proximity made it easier for Italians to see the pope as a flawed, ambitious political actor rather than only as an exalted religious authority.

  • The pope held both spiritual and temporal power.
  • Catholics could find themselves fighting wars against the pope.
  • Distant Europeans often encountered the pope through ceremony and envoys.
  • Italians knew the pope as a local elite with personal flaws and family ties.
  • Papal elections reshaped Italian patronage networks and offices.

Who Actually Needs Magnesium? Which Type Is Right For You (Doctor Explains)

This educational video, not personal medical advice, explains that severe magnesium deficiency is uncommon but many people may fall into a “gray zone” of suboptimal magnesium that standard blood tests can miss because most magnesium is stored in cells, muscle, and bone.   • Severe deficiency is different from suboptimal magnesium status.

  • Diet, stress, alcohol, PPIs, diuretics, and diabetes can increase risk.
  • Glycinate is often favored for sleep, stress, and tolerability.
  • Citrate and oxide are more useful when constipation is a goal.
  • Evidence for malate, taurate, and L-threonate is promising but limited.
