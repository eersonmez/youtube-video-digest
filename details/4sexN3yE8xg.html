What This Video Is About

This video features biomedical engineer Derya Unutmaz, who discusses the critical role Artificial Intelligence (AI) plays in managing the overwhelming complexity inherent in biological systems, such as immunology and cancer research. Having witnessed the limitations of traditional scientific approaches to treating diseases, Unutmaz realized that AI was necessary to process the trillions of components and billions of reactions occurring within living organisms [1:26-1:32].

The discussion centers on how advanced models like Codex have enabled her to transition from complex biological theory into practical applications. She details using these tools not just for literature review, but for building highly specialized software—such as custom analysis tools for flow cytometry and simulators for cellular interactions—that would traditionally require months of dedicated software engineering work [8:56-9:10].

Ultimately, Unutmaz outlines a grand vision: the creation of "virtual cells" and eventually a "digital twin" of a complete human being. She posits that this level of simulation will revolutionize drug discovery and personalized medicine by allowing researchers to test treatments virtually before applying them to patients, potentially accelerating clinical trials from years to days [23:17-23:27].

Detailed Walkthrough

Derya Unutmaz first identifies the need for AI after graduating from medical school due to the sheer complexity of biological systems and the inability of existing methods to treat many diseases [0:59-1:36]. Her interest in AI began when she realized models could potentially simulate these massive datasets, accelerating her focus during the deep learning revolution [1:48-2:08].

A pivotal moment occurred around September 2024 when OpenAI approached her to test early reasoning models. She tested one on a complex immunology question by cross-referencing concepts from battle royale video games with the immune system, framing it as an immune system "battle royale against tumors" [3:17-4:05]. The response she received was deeply insightful and emotional compared to previous models like GPT 5.40 [4:11-4:29], marking a realization that AI's reasoning capabilities were relevant for science [5:20-5:31].

Currently, Unutmaz relies heavily on Codex in her daily routine, describing herself as a "Codex addict" [6:00-6:03]. She uses it to rapidly prototype ideas—whether simulations, apps, or games—that previously required weeks or months of coding knowledge [6:11-6:48].

She demonstrates the application of AI in analyzing flow cytometry data. Flow cytometry is described as a method using lasers and fluorescent markers to analyze thousands of cells (like immune cells) to determine their function [7:47-8:25]. Instead of relying on decades-old specialized software, she built custom applications using Codex. One example involves visualizing single cells where different fluorescent molecules label specific cell types; for instance, identifying T cells that are CD8 positive or CD4 positive and calculating percentages [9:31-10:33].

Further examples include building an app to select a desired cell type (e.g., naive T cell) which then suggests relevant markers for panel development [11:13-11:46]. She also detailed simulating the function of a single receptor, such as the T cell receptor. This simulation allows her to control inputs like ligand quality and dose to predict molecular activation, phosphorylation patterns of transcription factors, and pathway outcomes when inhibitory molecules are introduced [13:30-15:25].

Her vision extends to using generative AI tools. After ImageGen 2.0 allowed her to create a complex image based on an immune atlas published in Nature, she fed that image into Codex to generate an interactive website where users could click on cells (like memory or effector cells) and receive detailed information about them [16:06-17:39].

Finally, Unutmaz discussed applying AI to genomics. She built an application that takes a gene sequence, such as the CD4 gene, pulls its DNA from a database, identifies potential 20 to 22 nucleotide targets, and ranks them for selection [19:48-20:08]. Furthermore, she developed a feature allowing users to input multiple genes to automatically generate diverse CRISPR molecules for library design [20:32-20:51].

Regarding the "digital twin," Unutmaz explains that it requires simulating an entire biological system—including metabolites, gut bacteria, hormones, and genetics—to achieve complete personalization. She predicts this could lead to drugs with nearly 100% efficacy and 0% side effects, drastically reducing clinical trial times from years to days [23:11-23:27].
