The real return on investment (ROI) of using AI tokens in enterprise settings.

Alex Kantrowitz · 2026-07-09T16:30:21+00:00 · The Real ROI of AI Tokens — With Dallas Dolen

What This Video Is About

This video features Dallas Dolan, a TMT leader at PwC, discussing the practical realities and return on investment (ROI) when implementing Artificial Intelligence within large organizations. The discussion moves beyond abstract concepts like "token maxing"—the idea of simply using as many tokens as possible—to focus squarely on outcome maximization. Dolan argues that true success in enterprise AI adoption is defined by achieving tangible, valuable outcomes rather than merely generating high volumes of content or code.

The central theme revolves around shifting organizational strategy from maximizing token usage to rigorously measuring the value derived from AI outputs against business objectives and industry benchmarks. The conversation addresses cost sensitivity among large enterprises, the necessity of centralized governance for AI usage, and how organizations must define what constitutes a "better product" versus just a larger output volume.

Detailed Walkthrough

The discussion begins by addressing the media narrative around "token maxing," which Dolan suggests is often an oversimplification of reality [1:09]. While some problematic cost behaviors exist, he clarifies that the ultimate metric for success should be whether the AI output leads to genuinely better products or services [2:27].

Dolan introduces the concept of "token minimizing" as a counter-trend to token maxing, noting that companies like AT&T and Meta are becoming highly focused on cost control [1:56]. He posits that there is no inherent winner between token maximizers and minimizers; instead, the victor will be defined by who achieves outcome maximization [2:20].

A critical component of this strategy involves organizational planning. Dolan emphasizes the need to benchmark AI spending not just against other service companies, but also against different industries, such as tech companies with large engineering teams, to establish a relevant baseline for ROI [3:01-3:26]. He provides an example where AI can transform thousands of hours of work into a product in seven minutes, demonstrating high output value despite potentially high initial cost [3:47-3:58].

When discussing ROI measurement, Dolan references external studies (like one from MIT) suggesting that generative AI could replace about 23% of certain activities [4:44]. However, he stresses that in a business context, this must be measured by efficiency—are employees creating more output or higher quality products for the time spent? He strongly cautions against equating volume (more lines of code, longer decks) with value; investors and users ultimately want a better product they are willing to use [5:31-6:26].

To manage this complexity, centralized planning and control planes are becoming crucial. These systems allow enterprises to govern AI usage by dictating which models or interfaces should be used for specific tasks, preventing misuse (e.g., using an advanced model for a simple weather check) [7:06-8:13].

The conversation then shifts to cost sensitivity. When asked about willingness to pay more, the participants demonstrated that while many are satisfied with current pricing, there is significant price elasticity within large organizations. Dolan confirms that companies of his size (350,000 global people) are highly price sensitive due to sheer volume and will actively seek cheaper alternatives if offered [11:39-12:41].

Finally, the discussion touches upon AI agents. The limitations in deploying autonomous agents stem from three areas: risk tolerance (governance over data access or code changes), cost variance (when human performance is superior or cheaper), and organizational decision-making regarding acceptable levels of autonomy [13:19-14:30]. He concludes that the goal of AI should be augmentation—making existing employees better—rather than complete replacement, which fosters greater user comfort [20:28-20:53].

Key Takeaways

  • Success in enterprise AI adoption is defined by outcome maximization, not token maxing.
  • Enterprises must benchmark their AI spending against relevant industry standards to determine true ROI.
  • The focus should be on creating demonstrably better products or services, rather than simply increasing the volume of generated content (e.g., more lines of code).
  • Large organizations exhibit high price sensitivity regarding foundational models due to sheer usage volume across many employees.
  • Centralized planning and control planes are essential tools for governing AI usage by dictating model selection and access rights.
  • The limitations of autonomous agents are tied to risk tolerance, cost variance compared to human capability, and organizational acceptance of autonomy.
  • Augmentation—making existing staff more effective—is a key driver for user comfort with new technology, unlike full automation.

Practical Notes

  • ROI Measurement: ROI should be measured in terms of efficiency (output quality vs. time spent) rather than raw token consumption or volume metrics like line count.
  • Governance: Implement control planes to manage AI usage across the enterprise, specifying which models are appropriate for different tasks and controlling data access.
  • Cost Management: Be prepared for price wars among foundational model providers; large organizations actively seek cheaper alternatives if presented with a compelling cost reduction.
  • Agent Deployment: When deploying agents, define clear boundaries regarding their scope of action (e.g., what client data they can access or how much code they can modify) to manage risk tolerance.
