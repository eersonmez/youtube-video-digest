Big Technology tests AI narratives against Ramp spending data: Fable fallout, tokenmaxxing, DeepSeek, and SaaS.

Alex Kantrowitz · 2026-06-17T17:00:06+00:00 · AI Fact or Fiction: The Fable Ban, Tokenmaxxing, Saaspocolypse — With Ara Kharazian

What This Video Is About

This video presents an analysis of current narratives surrounding the Artificial Intelligence industry—such as market dominance, spending habits, and the viability of established Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) companies—by examining real-world purchasing data from Ramp. The discussion centers on separating hype from observable buyer behavior in the enterprise AI adoption landscape. Key questions addressed include whether major models like Anthropic are permanently damaged by regulatory scrutiny, how much money companies are actually spending per employee on AI, and if the "SaaS apocalypse" is a realistic threat to established software categories.

The central argument presented by Ramp Lead Economist Erik Razean is that while AI adoption is rapidly accelerating across businesses, many prevailing narratives—such as unrestrained token spending or the wholesale replacement of legacy SaaS—are overstated when viewed against granular transaction data. The analysis suggests that market dynamics are far more complex than simple winner-take-all scenarios, highlighting vendor stickiness based on product experience rather than just model capability, and pointing to overlooked players like Google.

Detailed Walkthrough

The discussion begins by addressing the potential impact of government export controls, specifically referencing a past instance where the Department of Defense labeled Anthropic a supply chain risk [0:00]. The analysis concludes that this labeling did not cause business adoption to cease; instead, it may have accelerated usage, potentially creating a "forbidden fruit effect" due to perceived power or scarcity [12:34].

The conversation then moves to the concept of vendor stickiness. Contrary to the idea that AI models are interchangeable commodities, Razean argues that user retention is driven by the overall product experience and integration into workflows, such as Claude Code's success in enabling multi-step tasks for engineers [5:00]. This suggests that while model improvements matter, the surrounding software tooling is critical.

Ramp data reveals a dynamic market where vendor shares shift rapidly. Historically, OpenAI was dominant (20–40% of US firms paying for it), but by late 2025, Anthropic had surpassed them in business adoption share [10:00]. The top 1% of AI spenders are highly diversified, using an average of eight vendors, whereas the median firm uses two.

Regarding spending trends, Razean refutes the narrative of unrestrained "token maxxing." While overall AI spend is growing rapidly—up 15x from January 2025 through May 2026 among already-spending firms—the actual level remains modest for most businesses [15:00]. The median firm spends only about $11 per employee per month, which equates to a single low-level subscription. Conversely, the top 1% of firms spend around $7,449 per employee monthly [20:00].

The data also shows that cost control is becoming a major concern for businesses. This has led to increased spending on open-source platforms and models like DeepSeek as firms seek ways to manage token costs, though this shift remains small (5% of platform users) [30:00]. Furthermore, Razean posits that Google is significantly underrated because its business model allows it to offer better cost control through diverse revenue streams, unlike OpenAI or Anthropic whose models incentivize high token usage [35:00].

Finally, the "SaaS apocalypse" narrative is examined. The data does not support a wholesale replacement of traditional SaaS categories by LLMs. While AI-native competitors are growing (e.g., Adept in CRM), legacy vendors like Salesforce and HubSpot maintain dominant market shares. Furthermore, subscription-based spend remains high for both traditional and AI companies; metered usage is still less than 5% across the board [40:00].

Key Takeaways

  • Ramp uses transaction data to provide a forward-looking view of AI adoption, though its user base skews toward tech-forward companies.
  • Regulatory actions against models like Anthropic may not deter adoption; in some cases, they might accelerate interest due to perceived scarcity or power.
  • AI vendor markets are fluid and dynamic, with sophisticated buyers often utilizing multiple LLM providers rather than standardizing on one.
  • Vendor stickiness is heavily influenced by the integrated product experience (e.g., workflow integration) more so than raw model capability alone.
  • While AI spend is growing rapidly overall, it remains relatively small for the median firm ($11/employee/month).
  • The top 1% of firms exhibit extremely high spending on AI, averaging around $7,500 per employee monthly.
  • Cost control is driving some migration toward open-source models and model orchestration layers to manage token spend.
  • Google is positioned as a potentially underrated winner due to its diverse revenue streams, allowing it to offer better cost discipline than pure LLM providers.
  • The "SaaS apocalypse" is overstated; traditional SaaS categories remain durable, though AI-native competitors are gaining share within them.

Practical Notes

  • Data Source Caveat: Ramp data reflects tech-forward businesses using the platform's payment tools and may not represent the average US firm.
  • Market Dynamics: Advanced AI users (top 1%) typically utilize an average of eight LLM vendors, suggesting that "trying both" is often the practical advice for effective adoption.
  • Cost Management Strategy: Firms seeking cost control are increasingly looking at open-source platforms or implementing model orchestration layers to route tasks to cheaper models.
  • Google's Advantage: Google's competitive edge lies in its broader ecosystem (e.g., Google Workspace) and revenue diversification, allowing it to offer better cost management options compared to token-dependent LLM providers.
