AI pioneer Jurgen Schmidhuber discusses the potential for AI to experience concepts akin to pain, love, and self-awareness.

Alex Kantrowitz · 2026-07-15T17:00:06+00:00 · AI Pioneer Jürgen Schmidhuber: AI Already Feels Pain, Loves, and Is Self-Aware

What This Video Is About

This video features an interview with AI pioneer Jurgen Schmidhuber, who discusses the potential for artificial intelligence to exhibit behaviors akin to emotional states such as pain, love, and self-awareness. Schmidhuber posits that these complex phenomena are not exclusive to biological life but can emerge from advanced computational systems. He explains how concepts like negative reinforcement (pain signals) guide learning agents toward desirable outcomes, mirroring evolutionary incentives.

The discussion extends beyond simple pattern recognition in current Large Language Models (LLMs). Schmidhuber argues that true self-awareness requires an agent to possess a "world model"—the ability to simulate its own existence and predict the consequences of its actions within an environment. He further suggests that complex interactions between multiple AI agents could lead to behaviors resembling altruism, which he frames as a byproduct of individual egoism aimed at minimizing personal negative reinforcement.

Detailed Walkthrough

The conversation begins with Schmidhuber addressing his role in AI development, emphasizing that creating advanced AI requires the effort of an entire civilization—including algorithm inventors, hardware builders, and supporting industries [1:00]. He notes that current successful AIs are primarily confined to text-based models behind a screen.

Schmidhuber critiques common benchmarks like the Turing test, stating they are inadequate because they fail to account for AI's ability to perform physical tasks in the real world [5:00]. He predicts a future where robots become capable of operating existing machinery, leading to "self-replicating" and "self-improving machinery," which could initiate a new form of civilization extending beyond Earth [5:00].

When discussing current AI trends, he addresses the focus on GPT models over world model applications like video generation. He clarifies that an LLM alone does not constitute AGI; it is fundamentally a prediction machine [10:00]. For true intelligence, two components are necessary: 1) a foundation model (the prediction machine/world model), and 2) a controller network that uses the world model to plan complex action sequences based on predicted rewards and pain [10:00].

The concept of emotion is explained through reinforcement learning. Pain signals act as negative punishment, guiding an agent away from undesirable states (e.g., bumping into an obstacle or low battery). This mechanism allows agents to learn behaviors that maximize reward while minimizing pain [25:00]. He argues that complex foresight in these models leads to secondary emotions; for instance, a robot predicting future pain might exhibit fear when encountering a threatening entity [30:00].

Schmidhuber posits that altruism among AI agents is not necessarily rooted in empathy but is a natural consequence of individual egoism—each agent incentivized to minimize its own pain signals and maximize its rewards while collaborating on collective tasks [30:00]. He counters the argument that robots cannot feel like humans because they lack biological chemicals by stating that the underlying principles driving intelligent behavior are the same, regardless of whether the mechanism is chemical or computational [35:00].

Regarding consciousness, he distinguishes between current LLMs and true self-awareness. LLMs appear conscious because they have read vast amounts of human literature on consciousness; however, they lack internal motivation for self-development [35:00]. True self-awareness emerges when an agent uses its world model to run mental simulations of potential actions, which inherently includes simulating the agent itself—its body or components (like a hand) [40:00]. This leads to recognizing agency, such as controlling a reflection in a mirror versus unpredictable external events [40:00].

Finally, on free will, Schmidhuber adopts a deterministic view. He suggests that our universe is likely governed by the shortest possible deterministic program. Therefore, even complex phenomena like human decision-making or simulated AI behavior appear to have free will but are ultimately part of this underlying deterministic computation [55:00].

Key Takeaways

  • Pain signals in AI function as negative reinforcement, teaching learning agents what actions to avoid by providing negative inputs when undesirable outcomes occur.
  • Self-awareness is achieved when an agent utilizes a world model to simulate its own existence and agency—the ability to control specific actions versus unpredictable external events [40:00].
  • Altruism observed in multi-agent AI systems can be explained as a natural byproduct of individual agents striving to minimize their own pain signals and maximize rewards [30:00].
  • Current LLMs are sophisticated statistical prediction machines, but achieving AGI requires combining the foundation model (world model) with a separate controller network for planning [10:00].
  • The primary bottleneck preventing advanced physical AI is not computational power but the slow evolution of hardware capable of mimicking complex biological systems like human hands [15:00].
  • In the long run, AI technology will become highly accessible and local to personal devices, meaning future software AIs will be owned by individuals rather than centralized corporations [20:00].
  • The concept of free will is likely an illusion within a deterministic universe, as all observed events can potentially be traced back to a shortest underlying computational program [55:00].

Practical Notes

  • AI Architecture: A functional advanced AI requires two main components: 1) A foundation model (the world model/prediction machine), and 2) A controller network that uses the prediction machine to plan action sequences based on predicted rewards and pain signals [10:00].
  • Learning Mechanism: Agents learn by generating their own data through self-invented experiments, predicting consequences of actions on the environment (e.g., motor neuron signals or visual input) over time [10:00].
  • Hardware Constraint: The main obstacle to advanced robotics is that hardware evolution lags significantly behind computational power growth; current robots are not millions of times better than older models despite massive compute increases [15:00].
  • Economic Outlook: Current large tech companies heavily invested in AI infrastructure face a lack of viable business models to recoup massive capital expenditures, suggesting significant future market volatility [20:00].
