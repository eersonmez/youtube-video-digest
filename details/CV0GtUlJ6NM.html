What relationship science actually says about attraction, dating apps, compatibility, and long-term romance

Andrew Huberman · 2026-06-22T12:00:17+00:00 · Science of Attraction, Compatibility & Romance | Dr. Paul Eastwick

What This Video Is About

Andrew Huberman talks with UC Davis psychologist Paul Eastwick about what attraction and relationship data show once you move past dating stereotypes. The episode pushes against profile-first thinking: apps amplify consensus desirability, but real relationships are shaped by repeated contact, idiosyncratic taste, reciprocal support, perceived similarity, unique shared moments, and physical intimacy.

Useful Substance

Eastwick's strongest point is that dating apps are not neutral relationship laboratories. They make everyone compete over easily ranked traits, so the most popular profiles get most right swipes and messages. In ordinary acquaintance, school, work, friend groups, or repeated in-person settings, people have more chances to develop specific attraction that is not captured by a profile.

The gender-stereotype section is also useful. Eastwick argues that when people evaluate real people they have met, the big simple stories weaken: men also like ambition in women, women also show some attraction to younger men in early-date data, and financial security matters as a couple-level stability issue more than as a one-directional provider script.

For compatibility, the key distinction is actual similarity versus perceived similarity. Matching people by objective traits or questionnaires often predicts chemistry poorly. What matters more is the felt sense that you have a lot in common, that your partner has qualities no one else can substitute for, and that the relationship gives both people support, trust, and room to become more stable.

The episode treats sexual desire as a serious relationship variable without making it fatalistic. Physical intimacy and desire predict stability, but attraction is not a simple permanent switch. Eastwick also separates ordinary attraction to other people from infidelity risk: the danger comes from repeatedly following that attraction into private disclosure, intimacy, and action.

Key Takeaways

  • 18:17: Apps create a winner-take-most attention market; repeated face-to-face contact reduces that effect.
  • 23:56: Dating as a trait interview misses the specific stories and reciprocal moments that make attraction feel unique.
  • 1:33:21: Ambition/status preferences are more similar across men and women in real-interaction data than stereotypes suggest.
  • 1:48:32: Early-date age-preference data do not support the simple claim that only men prefer younger partners.
  • 2:03:53: Perceived similarity matters more than objective similarity matching.
  • 2:15:43: Attraction outside the relationship is common; repeated follow-through is the risk pattern.
  • 2:35:19: Physical intimacy and sexual desire are important, but not always fixed or hopeless when they fluctuate.

Practical Notes

Treat apps as introductions, not compatibility engines. Prefer contexts that create real interaction, shared activity, and enough time for idiosyncratic attraction to emerge. In existing relationships, watch for whether both people feel uniquely valued, supported, physically wanted, and able to build a life together under stress.
