The discussion examines how physical AI can digitize supply-chain assets and automate operational decisions.

Alex Kantrowitz · 2026-07-20T15:04:34+00:00 · Inside The Rise of Physical AI — With Amir Khoshniyati

What This Video Is About

This sponsored interview between Alex Kantrowitz and Amir Khoshniyati, a Wiliot vice president, examines how AI can move beyond software interfaces and influence the physical movement of goods. The conversation starts with Amazon’s earlier “Hands Off the Wheel” program, which used historical purchase data to forecast fulfillment-center inventory and automate vendor negotiations. Khoshniyati argues that the next step is to give the physical products, pallets, cases, crates, and other assets their own continuously updated digital identities.

In this framing, physical AI is more than attaching a barcode or RFID tag to an object. A digitized asset can carry information about its location and traceability as well as its condition: temperature, humidity, light exposure, and movement. That data can be collected in the cloud, analyzed for patterns and exceptions, and used to trigger operational decisions such as moving, holding, discarding, replacing, or rerouting goods.

The practical importance is concentrated in ordinary supply-chain problems: refrigerated products dwelling too long at a loading dock, inventory that cannot be located, manual receiving and cycle counts, reusable pallets or crates that fail to return, and disputes over where a shipment became compromised. The interview’s central qualification is that AI decisions are only as trustworthy as the sensor data beneath them. Wiliot presents a staged deployment model using battery-free IoT Pixels, existing supply-chain technologies, and integrations with ERP or warehouse-management systems. Product capabilities, ROI figures, and future agentic use cases are presented by a company executive in a sponsored discussion rather than independently verified in the transcript.

Detailed Walkthrough

From Amazon’s digital decisions to physical operations

Kantrowitz opens with an example from Amazon’s mid-to-late-2010s use of AI and machine learning [0:24]. Amazon’s “Hands Off the Wheel” program reportedly examined historical purchase patterns, including what customers bought, where they lived, and the season in which purchases occurred. It used those patterns to predict what should be stocked in fulfillment centers, when it should be stocked, and at what price.

The system also included an AI-facing vendor portal that could negotiate with suppliers within predetermined price ranges. In effect, some of the vendor manager’s work moved from a human employee to software. This establishes the earlier model of supply-chain AI: algorithms make decisions from digital records, forecasts, and transaction history, while the underlying goods remain largely unobserved between scans.

The interview asks what changes when AI is connected directly to the physical objects moving through that system.

The progression from “Internet of Devices” to physical AI

Khoshniyati first separates physical AI from the earlier evolution of the Internet of Things [1:22]. He says that the early IoT label was used broadly before its meaning had stabilized. Much of what was called IoT was actually a transition from on-premises systems to cloud-connected devices. The emphasis was on “mobile first” and “cloud first,” with people using multiple phones, tablets, and other connected devices.

As those devices gained applications and began interacting with everyday objects, the category moved closer to the conventional idea of IoT. The COVID period accelerated contactless interactions and increased reliance on cloud services. QR codes, RFID and NFC-related technologies, contactless payment chips, and similar mechanisms allowed objects and users to exchange information without physical contact [2:21].

Khoshniyati’s distinction is that these connected objects were initially mechanisms for retrieving or summoning data, not necessarily intelligent assets. A QR code or contactless chip could identify something or initiate an interaction, but it generally did not provide a continuous record of the object’s condition.

What “physical AI” means in this discussion

At [3:42], Khoshniyati defines physical AI as the ability to give every physical asset a digital identity. The identity is not limited to knowing which tag is associated with which object. It also includes the state in which the object exists.

That state might include:

  • Current or historical location.
  • Temperature.
  • Humidity.
  • Exposure to light.
  • Movement or handling.
  • Traceability through different supply-chain stages.

For example, light detected through a package could suggest that the package was opened or tampered with. Movement can be inferred from an asset being detected in different places, including through triangulation. Once the asset’s signals are stored in the cloud, AI can examine the history, identify patterns, and make predictive judgments.

This is the key conceptual shift in the interview: physical AI is not simply an AI model controlling a robot. It is a data and decision layer built on top of digitally identifiable physical assets.

Replacing periodic spreadsheets with condition-aware inventory

Kantrowitz describes the conventional warehouse process as depending on spreadsheets, manual entries, and scans [4:18]. Khoshniyati agrees that physical AI can provide something closer to a real-time picture of inventory, but emphasizes that location alone is not enough.

A warehouse manager might need to know not just where a pallet is, but whether it has remained within its required temperature range, whether it was exposed to excessive humidity, or whether it has been moved unexpectedly. That information is valuable beyond the warehouse itself. Brands can potentially follow goods upstream to a manufacturer or packaging organization and downstream through distribution, receiving, and store operations.

The first implementation step is therefore to identify the asset and the part of the supply chain that needs visibility [6:01]. Wiliot might begin at a distribution center, start upstream with a manufacturer, or work with a packaging organization. The scope depends on what the customer is trying to track and trace.

Wiliot’s IoT Pixels

Khoshniyati describes Wiliot as a physical AI platform built around proprietary IoT Pixels [6:43]. He calls them reference designs that RFID manufacturers can produce. The tags are described as approximately the size of a postage stamp and battery-free.

Their operating model is:

  • The Pixel harvests energy from ambient waves.
  • A capacitor stores that energy, functioning like a sponge.
  • Once powered, the Pixel transmits a signal using Bluetooth Low Energy.
  • The signal is ingested into the cloud.
  • The platform turns the incoming data into operational insights.

According to the interview, the signals can represent location, temperature, humidity, light exposure, and movement. The Pixels can be attached at different levels of granularity: a pallet, case, crate, or, in some circumstances, an individual item [7:32].

The transcript does not specify the reader or gateway topology, installation requirements, transmission frequency, or cloud architecture. It does establish that Wiliot depends on consistent signal detection and an adequate read range to turn the physical tags into usable data.

Separating sensor data from the AI layer

When Kantrowitz asks where the AI begins, Khoshniyati draws a distinction between collecting data and reasoning over it [8:17]. Wiliot’s platform is described as a place where incoming signals are synthesized, unpacked, and made into a trusted source of information. The data may include repeated observations of location, temperature, humidity, and light, as well as changes that occur as an item moves through the supply chain.

Once that information has been normalized and made usable, the AI layer can answer questions or identify future outcomes. One example involves reusable assets. If a pallet or other asset leaves a facility, historical movement patterns may help predict when it will return.

Another example involves compliance. If a product is required to remain between 32 and 35 degrees Fahrenheit, the platform could identify a temperature excursion outside that range. In the example given, a two-degree variance could result in a warning that the pallet should not be moved to the front of the store because the goods may no longer comply with the customer’s requirements [9:28].

The emphasis is that AI is not expected to compensate for missing or unreliable observations. The system first needs a factual record of what happened to the asset.

Cold-chain workflows: dwell time, alerts, and item-level questions

Kantrowitz asks whether the system presents users with a chatbot or LLM-style interface [10:04]. Khoshniyati does not name a particular user interface. Instead, he describes two levels of operation: macro-level supply-chain monitoring and micro-level item questions.

At the macro level, he gives the example of separate pallets containing meat and vegetables. The pallets may require different refrigerated or frozen conditions as they travel from a distribution center, through a trailer, to a store’s loading dock. Once they arrive, they have a limited unloading window before they must be moved into refrigeration or a freezer.

Wiliot says it works with customers to calculate the acceptable dwell time for a particular process. The platform can then issue a proactive trigger, such as a warning that if the pallet remains at the dock for another five minutes, it will no longer be sellable [10:53]. The purpose is to intervene before the product becomes a confirmed loss.

At the micro level, the system could track an individual package of meat that was removed from a freezer several times. A user might ask an operational question equivalent to whether the product is safe to consume. In the hypothetical described, the platform would report that the item had gone out of variance and should not be consumed because of a potentially serious contamination risk [11:45].

The interview uses these examples to show why the economic value of AI may come from narrowly defined operational decisions rather than consumer-facing chat subscriptions. A single alert could prevent a customer from receiving compromised food, stop a store from selling it, or prevent an entire pallet from being distributed.

Traceability and proactive intervention

The benefit can exist at several levels. If a single item is compromised, item-level data may prevent that product from reaching a customer. If the problem is linked to a pallet, the organization can trace the event back and discard the affected group of goods rather than searching through the entire inventory [13:34].

The same record can also reduce disputes between food processors, distributors, and receivers. Instead of arguing about which party caused a compliance failure, the organizations can examine the asset’s recorded history and identify where the problem occurred.

Khoshniyati also agrees with a more agentic scenario: if a seasonal shipment is going to arrive too late to be useful, an AI system could flag it, stop the process, and arrange a replacement rather than allowing it to continue through the entire supply chain [14:10].

He illustrates this with strawberries. A shipment may encounter condensation and then repeated temperature changes, including a change that is too cold and produces freezing. Khoshniyati says that accumulating water and temperature cycling can create conditions associated with bacterial growth. In his proposed workflow, the system would flag the shipment early, stop or redirect it, and arrange replacement inventory before the store faces a larger stockout or customer-safety problem [14:50].

Why trustworthy physical AI starts with trustworthy data

The conversation then turns to a central risk: AI systems are probabilistic, while supply-chain and food-handling decisions can be mission-critical [15:30]. Kantrowitz points out that a system cannot simply “freelance” when the consequence of an incorrect decision is spoiled food or a missed shipment.

Khoshniyati responds that trust has to begin with the source of the data [16:13]. He uses an example of asking ChatGPT a question when he knows roughly 60 percent of the answer and receiving an incorrect response based on a weak or opinion-based source. His argument is that a physical AI system needs an equivalent source-of-truth discipline.

For Wiliot, that means:

  • Assets must be tagged consistently.
  • Signals must be detected within the intended read range.
  • Data must be factual and valid.
  • Repeated and varying observations must be synthesized into a usable record.
  • AI must be applied after the underlying data has been made reliable.

He describes Wiliot’s priority as visibility and readability first, with AI layered on afterward. The interview does not specify sensor calibration procedures, audit mechanisms, access controls, or how disagreements between sensor readings are resolved.

Proof of concept, pilot, and deployment

Wiliot describes a three-stage implementation process: proof of concept, pilot, and deployment [17:23]. The company’s stated preference is not to remove the infrastructure used during an early evaluation. Hardware installed for a proof of concept or pilot is intended to remain in place and expand as the organization increases volume and scale.

The goal is to validate the use case, prove that the tags are consistently detected, and confirm that the system produces reliable data before adding more AI-driven behavior. Khoshniyati characterizes this as making the process “bulletproof” through consistent scans and valid read ranges.

He argues that larger retailers, e-commerce companies, and third-party logistics providers could accelerate adoption across their supplier networks. If many suppliers already serve the same large organizations, a system adopted by a major retailer or logistics provider could eventually extend to other organizations in the same supply chain [18:27].

The tradeoff between automation and manual work

Kantrowitz raises a counterexample involving a reported Starbucks inventory effort in which employees took photographs of store shelves for an inventory system. He explicitly says that he may have the details wrong but uses it to raise a broader concern: AI deployments can fail when companies choose a solution quickly without selecting the right combination of computer vision, databases, retrieval systems, sensors, or other technologies [19:35].

Khoshniyati’s response is that every manual step adds friction and creates another opportunity for inconsistent data. Wiliot focuses on automating common workflows, including:

  • Recording goods as they leave a facility.
  • Recording and automating receiving when goods arrive.
  • Locating assets inside a warehouse.
  • Counting the contents of pallets, reusable plastic crates, or rolling cages.
  • Monitoring temperature and other condition signals.
  • Predicting the return of reusable assets owned by the organization.

The company’s recommended adoption pattern is to start with repeatable use cases, “rinse and repeat” the foundation, and then expand into more customized innovation [21:22]. Heavy customization at the beginning can make adoption harder because every new customer or site requires a completely different workflow.

The approach is additive rather than a wholesale replacement of existing systems. Barcodes, QR codes, machine vision, and line-of-sight workflows have been used for more than 25 years, according to Khoshniyati. RFID has been used for more than 15 years. Wiliot presents its technology as building on those established practices.

LLMs, multimodal systems, and shared supply-chain data

The interview briefly considers different AI-building strategies, including computer vision, multimodal AI, and RAG systems connected to databases [22:26]. Khoshniyati says that publicly trained LLMs provide a useful foundation, but they do not remove the need for reliable physical-world data [23:07].

An LLM can only reason effectively about a pallet or package if the system has a consistent record of that pallet or package. If tags are missing, read ranges are inadequate, or condition signals are inconsistent, more capable language models do not solve the underlying problem.

Khoshniyati also describes a possible future in which models benefit from patterns shared across organizations. Much supply-chain data is proprietary, particularly the handoffs between companies. However, a food processor may work with several distributors, and large brands such as Yum Brands may use multiple suppliers. If common patterns can be shared appropriately, models could gain more information about recurring supply-chain conditions and improve prediction [24:34].

The transcript does not specify how data rights, privacy, security, or commercial confidentiality would be handled. It presents cross-company data sharing as a potential source of broader predictive intelligence.

Measuring ROI from operational problems

When Kantrowitz asks about token usage and AI cost efficiency, Khoshniyati says Wiliot begins with the business problem rather than with the model [25:39]. The problem might be shrink, manual labor, or a combination of the two.

The process described is:

  • Identify the operational problem.
  • Quantify its current financial impact.
  • Map one or more Wiliot use cases to that problem.
  • Compare implementation cost and deployment complexity with the expected reduction in loss or labor.
  • Estimate how long it will take to recover the investment.

Khoshniyati cites a major retailer that calculated a loss whenever a pallet sat idle for more than 37 minutes. Multiplied across the retailer’s operations, that threshold reportedly represented more than 270 million dollars in annual losses [26:04]. He says the manual labor required to scan pallets was not included, meaning any labor reduction would be additional to that figure.

Wiliot’s stated typical ROI range is seven to thirteen months, with the cited retailer example reaching a thirteen-month payback [27:40]. He also says the actual result depends on the complexity of implementation and the time required to deploy across multiple sites.

On AI spending specifically, Khoshniyati says the focus is currently less on token costs and more on solving the operational problem. Wiliot concentrates on collecting and organizing the data, then relays it into ERP and warehouse-management systems where newer agentic AI systems can perform additional analysis [28:08].

The physical-world “data foundry”

The final concept is a “data foundry” for the physical world [29:12]. The metaphor describes raw real-world signals entering a shared pool, being synthesized into useful information, and producing actionable outputs.

At the organization level, the pool might contain all the data collected from a company’s tagged assets. At a larger level, the pool could include patterns across a supply chain. Companies generally do not publish all of their operational data, so Khoshniyati says the Wiliot platform currently focuses on interpreting the data available to each organization.

If the industry becomes more standardized, common patterns could emerge across food processors, distributors, retailers, and logistics providers. Publicly available AI agents could then analyze those patterns. Khoshniyati suggests that even smaller businesses could benefit by using broader supply-chain benchmarks while still tracking their own KPIs.

The proposed end state is not merely a warehouse database. It is a system in which physical assets generate structured observations, AI finds patterns in those observations, and agents or enterprise systems act on the resulting decisions.

Key Takeaways

  • Physical AI is defined in the interview as giving physical assets digital identities that include both identity and condition, not merely attaching a tag for location.
  • The progression described is from cloud-connected devices, to connected objects, to physical assets whose real-world state can be continuously analyzed.
  • Wiliot’s IoT Pixels are described as postage-stamp-sized, battery-free devices that harvest ambient energy and transmit signals over Bluetooth Low Energy.
  • The Pixels can be used at the pallet, case, crate, or individual-item level, depending on the tracking requirement.
  • Relevant signals include location, temperature, humidity, light exposure, and movement. Light exposure may indicate package tampering, while movement can help establish where an asset has traveled.
  • The sensor platform and the AI layer serve different functions: the platform creates a consistent record of physical events, while AI synthesizes that record, answers questions, predicts outcomes, and triggers actions.
  • Cold-chain value comes from intervening before goods become unsellable, such as warning that a pallet has exceeded its acceptable dwell time at a dock.
  • Item- and pallet-level traceability can reduce customer risk, support targeted disposal, and identify which party in a supply chain was responsible for a compliance failure.
  • Trustworthy agentic behavior depends on consistent tags, reliable reads, valid sensor data, and a defined source of truth. A larger language model cannot compensate for missing physical observations.
  • Wiliot recommends a proof of concept, pilot, and deployment sequence, with infrastructure intended to remain in place and scale rather than be discarded after testing.
  • Adoption is framed as additive: barcodes, QR codes, RFID, and machine vision can remain part of the workflow while physical AI adds continuous condition and location data.
  • Wiliot’s ROI method starts with a measurable problem such as shrink or manual labor. The interview cites more than 270 million dollars in annual idle-pallet losses at one retailer and claims typical payback of seven to thirteen months.
  • The interview presents fully agentic supply-chain management as a near-future possibility, but the currently described capabilities range from visibility and alerts to predictive analysis and selective intervention.
  • A physical-world data foundry would combine asset signals, organizational data, and eventually shared supply-chain patterns to produce operational decisions.

Practical Notes

Start with a measurable operational problem. Define the failure in business terms before selecting a model or interface. Examples from the interview include pallet dwell time, shrink, manual scanning, missed receiving events, temperature excursions, and reusable assets that do not return. Quantify the current cost and then determine which use case can address it.

Choose the asset scope and process boundary. Decide whether the first deployment concerns pallets, cases, crates, individual products, or reusable handling equipment. Decide whether the initial boundary is a distribution center, a manufacturer, a packaging operation, a store, or a handoff between companies. The video emphasizes that visibility can extend both upstream and downstream, but it does not prescribe one universal starting point.

Select the condition signals that matter. Location alone may be sufficient for some inventory workflows, while cold-chain products may require temperature and dwell-time monitoring. Other potential signals mentioned are humidity, light exposure, and movement. Thresholds should be defined for the specific product and customer process. The 32-to-35-degree-Fahrenheit example is an illustration from the discussion, not a universal food-handling standard.

Instrument the assets. Wiliot’s described product is the battery-free IoT Pixel. It can be placed on a pallet, case, crate, or item, harvests energy from ambient waves, and transmits over Bluetooth Low Energy. The transcript does not provide installation commands, API endpoints, reader specifications, sensor-calibration procedures, or a data schema.

Establish data quality before adding advanced AI. Confirm that tags are consistently detected, that assets fall within the intended read range, and that the resulting observations are factual enough to support decisions. The described architecture is: tagged asset, transmitted signal, cloud ingestion, data synthesis and normalization, AI analysis, then an alert, enterprise-system update, or agentic action. The interview treats this data-quality gate as a prerequisite rather than an optional refinement.

Encode operational rules and triggers. A customer can define an acceptable dwell time, temperature range, or compliance condition. The platform can then issue a proactive event when the condition is approaching or has been exceeded. The transcript describes warnings such as “another five minutes will make this pallet noncompliant,” but it does not explain the exact rule engine or how a customer configures it.

Integrate with existing enterprise systems. Khoshniyati says Wiliot can relay data into ERP and warehouse-management systems. Those systems may then provide additional analysis or agentic behavior. The interview does not name specific ERP or WMS products, so implementation details would have to be determined separately.

Use a staged rollout. The recommended sequence is proof of concept, pilot, and deployment. Validate the target use case and read consistency first, then expand volume and sites. Wiliot’s stated preference is to keep the hardware installed during this progression rather than rip and replace it.

Avoid unnecessary early customization. Begin with repeatable workflows such as shipping, receiving, location, cycle counts, condition monitoring, or reusable-asset tracking. The interview argues that excessive customization at the start creates friction and makes adoption harder. Existing barcodes, QR codes, RFID, and machine vision can remain in the architecture.

Treat autonomous action as a higher-risk layer. The transcript moves from visibility, to questions, to proactive alerts, and then to agents that could stop or redirect shipments. It does not describe approval workflows, human sign-off, audit logs, model governance, or fail-safe behavior. Those controls would be important for any deployment involving food, compliance, or irreversible disposal decisions.

Measure ROI against the original baseline. Compare the cost of tags, readers or other required infrastructure, cloud processing, integration, and rollout with the quantified cost of shrink, idle time, manual work, or stockouts. The seven-to-thirteen-month payback range and the more-than-270-million-dollar idle-pallet figure are claims made in the interview and should not be treated as guarantees for other organizations.

Keep the food examples in context. The meat, strawberries, contamination, and “safe to eat” scenarios are educational operational examples, not personal medical advice or a substitute for applicable food-safety regulations and qualified professional judgment.
