Michelle “MishManners” Duke explains how to build engaging WhatsApp messaging experiences with Twilio, RCS, and AI-generated media.

NDC Conferences · 2026-06-23T14:01:14+00:00 · Building fun and creative messaging experiences on WhatsApp - Michelle "MishManners" Duke

What This Video Is About

Michelle “Mish” Duke presents a developer-focused talk about creating more engaging business messaging experiences, especially on WhatsApp, using Twilio. The talk starts from a broad premise: communication channels shape how people understand, respond to, and act on messages. As phones and messaging platforms have evolved from SMS to MMS, RCS, and WhatsApp, the expectations around business communication have changed too.

The core argument is that businesses increasingly need to meet customers in the messaging channels they already use, with short, relevant, trusted, and visually rich interactions. Duke contrasts overloaded email inboxes and low click-through rates with the immediacy of text messaging, while also warning that plain business SMS is beginning to inherit the same problem as email: lots of generic, uninspiring content.

The practical center of the talk is a live demo of a WhatsApp experience built with Twilio. A user sends a selfie over WhatsApp; Twilio receives it through the WhatsApp gateway; a webhook downloads the temporarily hosted media; OpenAI transforms the photo into a selected anime style; the Sharp image-processing library overlays a consistent branded frame; and Twilio sends the final generated image back to the user. The example is intentionally fun, but Duke frames it as a pattern for building many kinds of customer experiences: event activations, shopping flows, transit notifications, appointment confirmations, surveys, and branded calls to action.

Detailed Walkthrough

Duke begins by introducing the talk as a session about creating “really fun experiences with WhatsApp,” while also covering why fun messaging matters for customer engagement [0:00]. She identifies herself as Michelle or Mish, a developer evangelist at Twilio, and explains that Twilio is still widely known for SMS, but now covers a broader communications platform: SMS, email, phone calls, AI, voice calling, call centers, and other messaging capabilities [0:00].

She lays out the structure of the session: communication, the evolution of messaging, business messaging, WhatsApp business messaging, a live demo, and thoughts about the future of communication platforms [0:00]. The framing is not just technical. The talk is about how communication works, why format matters, and how businesses can use richer channels to get people to act.

The first conceptual section focuses on why clear communication matters. Duke uses developer documentation as an example: if documentation communicates poorly, people may implement something incorrectly or give up on using it altogether [5:00]. She then connects this to broader communication history. Written communication moved from manuscripts and books to phones, and as the medium changed, the style of communication changed with it. Early mobile texting required tapping through physical phone keypads; later phones enabled more characters, faster input, emojis, photos, videos, and richer interactions [5:00].

She highlights a pattern: when communication platforms change, communication culture changes too. Emojis, for example, are now normal not just in personal messaging but also in developer documentation, pull requests, and open-source project culture [5:00]. Her point is that businesses and developers need to keep up with the ways people actually communicate, because if they do not meet users where they are, users will not read or act on the message.

Duke then gives a compressed history of digital messaging. The first SMS was sent in 1992 and reportedly said “Merry Christmas” [5:00]. A reply came the next day. In 2002, MMS changed mobile messaging by enabling multimedia such as photos, videos, and GIFs [5:00]. Instead of sending only short text like “I’m okay,” users could send images and richer context.

The talk then turns to RCS, or Rich Communication Services. Duke says Twilio announced API support for RCS the previous year, enabling richer business messaging with features like embedded URLs [10:00]. However, she stresses that RCS availability is uneven. It depends on country, device platform, and carrier support. In Australia, for example, she says she can use RCS on Android through Telstra, but iPhone users in Australia cannot use it in the same way at the time of the talk [10:00]. Her practical advice is to check the countries and channels where customers actually receive messages before investing in a specific messaging format.

The motivation for richer messaging is partly that email has become ineffective for many kinds of customer engagement. Duke asks how many people read every email in their inbox and describes the flood of spam and marketing messages that leads many users to delete most messages without reading them [10:00]. She gives the example of her husband creating a separate Gmail address specifically for spam and marketing signups [10:00]. Even when emails are opened, they may be poorly formatted, overly long, or not relevant.

She compares email with messaging channels. According to figures she cites in the talk, a good email campaign might have about a 30% open rate, and only about 1% to 3% of those opened emails lead to a click [15:00]. By contrast, she says consumers increasingly want short, relevant messages rather than long emails that require searching through an inbox or loading complex templates [15:00]. She also notes that businesses send roughly six billion text messages to customers every day globally [15:00]. That scale shows adoption, but it also creates a risk: business messaging can become as generic and ignored as email if companies use it badly.

To illustrate the problem, Duke describes actual business SMS messages from her own inbox. They are shorter than email, which is good, but often uninspiring and poorly personalized [15:00]. A clothing promotion might advertise t-shirts, shorts, and polos even though the sender likely has enough customer data to know that those are not relevant to her. A gym clothing mystery box message similarly fails to match her likely interests, despite the company having data [15:00]. Her criticism is not just that businesses lack data, but that they often fail to use it well.

RCS is presented as one way to improve business messaging. Duke cites Twilio research suggesting about a 12% increase in click rate for RCS compared with SMS, while noting that other research has shown increases as high as 30% [20:00]. She also mentions higher conversion rates and says some companies have seen conversion rates up to 40% with RCS [20:00]. Richer messages can also increase average order value because users see individualized information, images, and previews that make purchasing more compelling [20:00].

She then describes what an RCS message can contain. In a demo example for a fictional transit company called “Twill Transit,” the message includes branded messaging, cards or carousels, QR codes, and quick links to services such as Google [20:00]. Branded messaging is especially important because SMS and messaging scams are common. Duke gives examples of fraudulent messages pretending to be from banks, insurance companies, or postal services, asking users to click links and verify personal details [20:00]. If a business can verify its identity and show trusted branding, users are more likely to trust the message and click legitimate links.

This leads into WhatsApp. Duke explains that WhatsApp business senders also require verification with Meta to send business messages, and verified businesses can display a trusted tick [20:00]. She uses FedEx as an example: if a FedEx message arrives with branding, a tracking number, and a verified profile, she is more confident that the link is safe [20:00]. This matters because skepticism around links is one reason click-through rates can fall.

The talk then surveys WhatsApp-style rich messaging features. Duke mentions cards, suggested replies, and structured feedback options such as “average,” “great,” or “poor” [20:00]. She describes rich content cards with an image, a link or button, a URL, a time-saving action, and a short description [25:00]. In one Twilio coffee example, WhatsApp lists allow users to choose a type of coffee from a menu [25:00]. URL previews can show a small preview when a message links to something like GitHub [25:00].

The underlying business reason for all of this is action. Duke says companies send messages because they want recipients to do something: buy an NDC ticket, confirm a doctor’s appointment, get a boarding pass, click through to a store, or purchase products [25:00]. Rich messaging helps because it can make that action easier and more obvious. Instead of scrolling through a large email or deciphering a plain SMS, the user receives a concise, interactive, visually meaningful prompt.

The demo section introduces a WhatsApp chatbot-style experience used at events. A user sends a photo and receives back an anime-style generated image with event or brand framing [25:00]. Duke shows examples from events such as Programmable, Her Tech Circle, and API Days, describing how attendees often share the original photo next to the AI-generated version on social media [25:00]. The generated images become a form of brand activation: they promote Twilio, the conference, and the capabilities of AI-powered messaging.

She also describes an additional event use case: after photos are generated, they can be shown in a carousel on a screen at a booth [25:00]. Attendees can see their generated images appear and then encourage others to try it. This turns a technical messaging workflow into an interactive conference experience.

Duke explains that the same architecture can support many kinds of applications, not only anime portraits. Developers could build transit applications, shopping flows, or other WhatsApp-based services, using Twilio as the communication layer [30:00]. In this demo, OpenAI is used for image generation because the team has OpenAI API keys, but she explicitly says alternatives could include Gemini, open-source models, or locally hosted models [30:00].

The code walkthrough begins with image upload and timeout handling. The app includes a timeout function so slow internet or service delays do not cause the operation to hang indefinitely [30:00]. WhatsApp-hosted media requires special handling: when a user sends an image, the media is temporarily hosted at a URL in the cloud. The app must download that image before sending it to OpenAI for transformation [30:00].

A key implementation decision is the use of Sharp, a JavaScript image-processing library, to overlay the final branded frame. Duke says an earlier version sent both the user photo and the frame to OpenAI, asking the model to generate the full composite [30:00]. That caused two problems. First, it used more tokens than necessary. Second, because AI image generation is nondeterministic, the frame would be recreated slightly differently each time [30:00]. By using Sharp to overlay a fixed frame on top of the generated image, the branding remains consistent and the AI model only does the part that actually requires generation.

This becomes one of the talk’s broader engineering lessons: do not “throw AI at absolutely everything” [30:00]. If a deterministic tool solves part of the problem better, use that. In this case, AI was making the frame worse, while Sharp handled the task predictably.

The main WhatsApp webhook uses TwiML, Twilio’s markup language, to interpret and respond to messages coming through Twilio [30:00]. The server logs incoming events and errors to the console so Duke can trace failures. The webhook checks whether the incoming message includes media; if it does, the app downloads the image, processes it, and returns a generated response. If no image is detected, the app sends a message telling the user to send a photo [45:00].

Duke points out optional code for saving original uploaded images [35:00]. She has it commented out for the demo because she does not need the original images and does not want the privacy burden of storing them. She says this could be useful for personal projects, corporate offsites, or birthday parties where participants have given permission and the organizer wants to keep the originals instead of sending a separate Google Drive or OneDrive link later [35:00]. The caveat is explicit: only save originals when the context and permissions make that appropriate.

The OpenAI prompt is another important part of the implementation. Duke’s prompt asks for a “Joseph Moy anime style image” of the person or persons in the photo [35:00]. She explains that simply saying “anime” is too broad, just as saying “cartoon” is too broad. There are many different anime and cartoon styles, so naming a particular style gives more consistent outputs [35:00]. She contrasts the selected style with Studio Ghibli style, manga style, and Shonen Jump style.

The prompt also includes constraints based on observed failure modes. It tells the model not to add new people, not to add text if it was not present, not to add extra objects, and to avoid generating large blank spaces when the source selfie only includes part of someone’s face [35:00]. Duke emphasizes that even with careful prompting, AI is nondeterministic and will not follow instructions perfectly every time [35:00].

Model selection is discussed in practical terms. An earlier version used a “4o mini” style model, which sometimes produced images that looked more like a child’s drawing than anime and produced more errors [40:00]. Some errors came from landscape images, because the generated output did not match the dimensions required by the Sharp overlay. Other errors occurred when the model could not detect a person or face, such as a black dog on a black bed or an object like a chair [40:00].

The newer OpenAI image models allow Duke to specify output size directly, reducing the need to force portrait orientation through the prompt [40:00]. She says she tried multiple models, including 1, 1.5, 2, and 2.5. She chose 1.5 because it produced the style she liked and returned results in a reasonable amount of time [40:00]. Version 2.5 looked too hyperrealistic for the desired anime effect and took longer to generate images [40:00]. Her advice is to test different models instead of choosing the newest or most popular one by default.

After OpenAI generates the image, the server saves the generated file, applies the Sharp overlay, outputs the final image as a PNG, cleans up the temporary generated image, and sends a WhatsApp message containing the final image [40:00]. Because WhatsApp images are handled through hosted media URLs, the final image also needs to be available in a way Twilio can access and send back to the user [40:00].

Error handling is built for the user as well as the developer. Duke adds a user-facing error message so that if image generation fails, the user is told that something went wrong and can resend the image [45:00]. She explains that failures may happen if the image contains copyrighted characters or protected media. In testing with an earlier model, a selfie with Pikachu on her shirt would not generate; attempts to work around it by referring to “a popular yellow Pokemon” did not solve the issue [45:00]. She also mentions other examples such as Squirtle and Chewbacca causing copyright or safety-related refusals [45:00].

The live demo setup uses a local server running on port 3001 [45:00]. Because Twilio cannot send webhook requests directly to a private local server, Duke uses ngrok to expose the local server through a temporary public URL [45:00]. She copies the ngrok URL and adds the `/message` path for the webhook endpoint.

In the Twilio Console, she navigates to WhatsApp configuration. She notes that WhatsApp is under “Senders” rather than the general messaging area because WhatsApp sending requires a verified sender setup [50:00]. Unlike buying a normal Twilio number, a WhatsApp sender cannot simply be purchased and used immediately; it must go through Meta’s verification process [50:00]. For the demo, she configures the incoming message webhook URL, sets it to use POST, and notes that fallback URLs and voice configuration can also be configured [50:00].

On her phone, Duke opens a WhatsApp conversation with “Twilio AI artist” [50:00]. The profile includes information about Twilio, a privacy policy, a URL, a business account category such as arts and entertainment, and other business profile details [50:00]. She shows that if she sends something that is not an image, the bot responds with a message saying it cannot detect an image and asks her to send a photo [50:00]. She then takes and sends a selfie, and the bot replies that the image is being processed [50:00].

She then explains the architecture step by step [55:00]. A person takes a selfie and sends it through WhatsApp. The message goes through the Twilio API and WhatsApp gateway using the Twilio phone number. Twilio converts the incoming WhatsApp message into an HTTP POST request to the webhook server. The server downloads the original image from the temporary cloud-hosted media URL. It sends the image to OpenAI’s API, receives the generated anime-style image, then uses Sharp to add the branded overlay. The final image is statically hosted or otherwise made accessible, and Twilio sends it back over WhatsApp to the user [55:00].

Duke also shows a dashboard built around the activation [55:00]. It logs some information from the messaging workflow so she can monitor failed images without relying only on console output, which can scroll away quickly during high-volume use [55:00]. The dashboard can show where users are coming from, though she says it is not tied to names. It can also select users and send a message, for example telling participants that the experience will reopen the next day [55:00]. Another feature randomly picks a winner by number, sends them a message saying they won, and asks them to come to the booth to claim a prize [55:00].

Near the end, Duke invites attendees to try the WhatsApp experience by scanning a code [55:00]. She says she tested load by uploading around 10 images and mentions an anecdote where someone uploaded an entire holiday album of about 55 images in one go and the system still worked [55:00]. She ends by reiterating the larger lesson: if you build something fun and engaging, people are more likely to do the action you want, whether that is buying a service, hiring a consultant, buying a ticket, purchasing a product, or participating in an event [1:00:00].

Key Takeaways

  • Rich messaging is about action, not decoration. Businesses send messages because they want users to confirm, buy, click, respond, attend, or otherwise act. Rich WhatsApp and RCS features can reduce friction around that call to action.
  • Email engagement is weak for many customer interactions. Duke cites a good email campaign as having around a 30% open rate, with only about 1% to 3% of opened emails producing clicks [15:00]. This motivates the shift toward messaging channels.
  • Plain SMS is also becoming crowded and generic. Businesses send around six billion customer text messages per day globally, according to the talk [15:00]. Without relevance and creativity, text messaging can fall into the same trap as email.
  • RCS and WhatsApp support richer interaction patterns than SMS. Examples include branded senders, verified profiles, cards, carousels, QR codes, quick links, suggested replies, lists, buttons, and URL previews [20:00], [25:00].
  • Verification and branding matter because scams reduce trust. Users are less likely to click links if they cannot tell whether a message is legitimate. RCS branding and WhatsApp/Meta verification help establish trust [20:00].
  • Twilio can act as the communications layer for WhatsApp workflows. In the demo, Twilio receives WhatsApp messages, converts them into HTTP POST requests, routes them to a webhook, and sends media responses back to the user [55:00].
  • WhatsApp media must be downloaded before processing. Incoming images are temporarily hosted at a URL, so the server needs to fetch that media before sending it to OpenAI or any other image-processing system [30:00].
  • Use AI only where it adds value. Duke replaced AI-based frame compositing with Sharp because AI produced inconsistent branded frames and used unnecessary tokens [30:00]. Deterministic image processing was better for the overlay step.
  • Prompt specificity improves consistency. Asking for a specific anime style, rather than generic “anime,” produced more consistent outputs. The prompt also includes constraints to avoid extra people, extra objects, unwanted text, and excessive whitespace [35:00].
  • Model choice should be tested empirically. Duke tried multiple OpenAI image models and chose 1.5 because it balanced style and speed. The newer or larger model was not automatically better for her use case [40:00].
  • Privacy decisions should be intentional. The demo does not save original user images because Duke does not need them and wants to avoid privacy risk. She only recommends saving originals when participants have clearly permitted it [35:00].
  • User-facing error handling is necessary. It is not enough for failures to appear in server logs. The app sends users a message when image generation fails so they know to retry [45:00].

Practical Notes

The demo architecture is a WhatsApp-to-webhook-to-AI workflow. A user sends a selfie to a WhatsApp business sender. Twilio receives the message through the WhatsApp gateway, converts it into an HTTP POST, and sends it to a configured webhook endpoint. The server checks whether the incoming message contains media. If it does, it downloads the temporary media URL, sends the image to OpenAI for transformation, saves the generated result, overlays a branded frame with Sharp, hosts or exposes the final image, and sends it back through Twilio over WhatsApp [55:00].

For local development, Duke runs the server locally on port 3001 and uses ngrok to expose it publicly [45:00]. Twilio needs a publicly reachable webhook URL, so the local endpoint is mapped to an ngrok URL. The webhook path used in the demo is `/message`, and the Twilio configuration is set to POST [50:00].

Twilio Console setup for WhatsApp involves sender configuration. Duke notes that WhatsApp is found under “Senders,” because WhatsApp requires sender verification rather than simply buying a phone number and sending messages immediately [50:00]. Production WhatsApp sending requires going through Meta verification. The business profile can include a display name, profile photo, privacy policy, website URL, business category, and related metadata [50:00].

The image generation implementation uses OpenAI in the demo, but Duke explicitly says other model providers could be used: Gemini, open-source models, or locally hosted models [30:00]. The model should be selected by testing output quality, latency, reliability, and style fit, not by assuming the latest model is best [40:00].

Sharp is used for image compositing. The talk specifically mentions overlaying a branded mask/frame “over” the generated image and outputting a PNG [40:00]. The reason for using Sharp is consistency: OpenAI recreated the frame differently each time when asked to generate the whole final image, while Sharp can apply the same overlay deterministically [30:00].

The prompt includes style and behavioral constraints. Duke uses a specific “Joseph Moy anime style” prompt and adds instructions not to add new people, text, or extra objects [35:00]. She adjusted prompts after seeing issues such as blank space from partial-face selfies and unwanted additions from the model. However, she repeatedly cautions that AI outputs are nondeterministic and prompts cannot guarantee perfect compliance [35:00].

Image orientation and dimensions matter. Earlier model behavior sometimes returned landscape images, which caused errors when the Sharp overlay dimensions did not match [40:00]. Newer image models with explicit size parameters helped reduce this problem. This is an implementation decision point: ensure the generated image size matches the overlay and delivery format before compositing.

Error cases include missing media, no detectable person, object-only images, difficult image content, copyright-protected characters, and model refusals [40:00], [45:00]. Duke’s examples include a black dog in a black bed, a chair, Pikachu on a shirt, Squirtle, and Chewbacca. The app handles non-image input by asking the user to send a photo, and handles processing failure with a retry-oriented error message [45:00].

Privacy is a significant caveat. The code includes optional lines to save original uploaded images, but they are commented out in the demo [35:00]. Duke recommends only enabling original-image storage for contexts where it is appropriate and consented to, such as a personal event, corporate offsite, or birthday party where participants understand that originals will be retained.

The dashboard extension logs activation activity, tracks failed images, can send follow-up messages, and can randomly select a winner by phone number [55:00]. Duke emphasizes that this is useful when console logs are insufficient during heavy message volume.
