The application of AI in motorsports to improve team performance and data utilization.

OpenAI · 2026-07-16T17:00:16+00:00 · What racing reveals about working with AI — the OpenAI Podcast Ep. 22

What This Video Is About

This video explores the rapidly evolving intersection of artificial intelligence and motorsports, framing the current landscape as a "data wars" era [0:35]. The central theme is how high-bandwidth time-series data from racing—combined with qualitative information like driver notes—can be leveraged by AI to provide significant advantages in team performance, logistics, and decision-making.

The discussion features insights from OpenAI researcher Joyce Ruffell, who collaborated with Chip Ganassi Racing on applying AI across various aspects of their operation, and Chase Holden of RaceTek Systems, who builds bespoke racing intelligence tools using models like ChatGPT and Codex. The conversation emphasizes that while AI offers a massive information advantage, the ultimate success still hinges on human creativity, instinct, and the ability to organize complex workflows effectively [0:26].

Detailed Walkthrough

The discussion begins by establishing the premise that data is paramount in modern racing, leading to what is termed the "data wars" [0:35]. Joyce Ruffell details her research focus at OpenAI on data problems, including sources, quality, pipelines, and efficiency [0:48], noting a collaboration with Chip Ganassi Racing in IndyCar [1:10]. The nature of motorsports data is described as high-bandwidth time-series data that needs to be processed both retroactively and in real time at the track for quick decision-making [1:34].

Chase Holden describes RaceTek Systems' function: building "racing intelligence systems" on demand, including an all-in-one system designed to consolidate various operational details into one place [2:47, 3:09]. The application of AI extends beyond the track; it is being used to optimize logistics and operational processes for smaller teams to compete more effectively [19:34].

A key finding from the collaboration with Chip Ganassi Racing is the importance of education regarding AI. Holden stresses that understanding how the models work—that they are machines under the hood, not magic—is crucial for effective utilization, which takes the form of prompt engineering [11:43, 12:09]. The onus is placed on the user to determine the correct input to achieve the desired output [12:37].

On the driver side, AI can help break down communication barriers. For drivers who may not be engineers, AI allows complex analysis—which might take hours of study—to be delivered in a readable and digestible format for immediate use during practice or race preparation [14:04, 14:29].

The discussion highlights the challenge that much critical knowledge in motorsports is often trapped in unstructured formats, specifically spreadsheets [15:39, 21:51]. RaceTek's work involves helping teams convert this data into AI-native formats so models can process it effectively. This effort aims to allow smaller or mid-level teams to achieve operational capabilities closer to those of larger "juggernauts" by filling knowledge gaps that might otherwise require a large team of specialized engineers [19:23, 19:46].

Furthermore, AI is being applied across the entire organization, including logistics (parts tracking for broken components) and administrative tasks like sponsorship outreach and scheduling, helping individuals manage multiple roles more efficiently [20:47, 40:58]. The overall philosophy shared by both speakers is that the future of motorsports involves a human-centric co-working environment with AI systems [35:00].

Key Takeaways

  • Motorsports are currently defined by "data wars" due to the massive volume of available telemetry and operational data [0:35, 26:09].
  • AI is uniquely positioned to bridge the gap between hard quantitative telemetry (from ECUs) and softer qualitative data (like driver notes or engineer observations) [17:57].
  • Effective use of AI requires significant education for teams on how models function, specifically through prompt engineering, as input quality dictates output quality [12:09, 12:37].
  • Tools like RaceTek Systems utilize ChatGPT and Codex to build custom racing intelligence systems that enhance decision-making at the track [2:47].
  • AI can democratize competitive advantage by allowing smaller or mid-level teams to access analytical capabilities previously reserved for large, well-resourced organizations [19:34, 19:46].
  • A major hurdle in motorsports is that much critical operational knowledge resides in complex, unstructured formats like spreadsheets [21:51].
  • AI's role is not to replace human expertise but to act as an extension of ideas, allowing teams to rapidly prototype and workshop concepts before needing specialized personnel [38:47, 39:00].
  • The ultimate competitive edge in the future will belong to those who can best communicate their intent and build superior workflows with AI models [36:39, 37:19].

Practical Notes

  • Data Formats: Motorsports data often exists in complex Excel sheets; converting this into an "AI-native format" is a critical step for downstream analysis [21:51, 23:12].
  • Tools Mentioned: ChatGPT and Codex are used by RaceTek Systems to build racing intelligence tools.
  • Implementation Focus (Chip Ganassi Racing): Applications span logistics (parts tracking), driver performance enhancement, and strategic pre-race preparation [20:47, 13:09].
  • Process Improvement: Teams should continuously test their models every three months to ensure they are using the state of the art and can ingest data upstream with less manual legwork [23:27].
