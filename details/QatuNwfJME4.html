Mike Krieger discusses why breakthrough consumer AI startups may be difficult to build in today’s consolidated app market.

Alex Kantrowitz · 2026-06-21T16:23:19+00:00 · Mike Krieger on Why Most AI Startups Will Struggle

What This Video Is About

This short excerpt is about the difficulty of building a breakout consumer AI startup in today’s app ecosystem. Mike Krieger contrasts the environment in which Instagram launched with the current market for consumer apps, arguing that the conditions that allowed small teams to rapidly gain attention are much less available now.

The core point is that consumer attention has become highly consolidated. In Instagram’s early era, the app market still felt like a “wild west,” where two relatively unknown founders could release an app and quickly reach the top of a category. Today, according to Krieger, much of users’ time is concentrated in dominant platforms such as TikTok and Reels, making it harder for a new consumer product to break through.

The discussion matters for AI startups because “AI-powered” improvements alone may not be enough to change user behavior. Krieger gives the example of a document editor that is twice as good because of AI: even if the product is materially better, users may still hesitate to move their existing work and workflows into a new tool.

Detailed Walkthrough

Krieger begins by framing the question around “consumer AI breakouts” and whether the market has actually seen many yet [0:00]. The phrase suggests the discussion is not about enterprise AI adoption or infrastructure, but about consumer-facing AI products that could become widely used mainstream apps.

He then compares the present moment with the period when Instagram was released. At that time, he says the app environment still felt “a little bit wild west” [0:05]. The important point is that discovery and growth were less locked up by incumbent platforms. He describes Instagram as being released by “two kind of random people” who were able to reach “number one in photos and video within 3 months” [0:08]. The example is used to show that a small founding team could once launch a consumer app and rapidly gain category-level prominence.

Krieger argues that this kind of trajectory is “much harder now” [0:17]. The reason he gives is consolidation at the top of the app ecosystem. He refers to “how consolidated the top 10 is” and how much user time is spent inside major attention platforms, specifically naming “the TikToks and Reels of the world” [0:17]. In other words, the barrier is not only building a good product; it is breaking through entrenched user habits and attention patterns.

He then states the central conclusion: getting a “breakthrough consumer experience” is “really really hard” [0:25]. This is especially relevant to AI startups because many AI products may be framed as improvements to existing workflows rather than entirely new consumer behaviors. If the improvement is not dramatic enough, or if it requires users to migrate data and habits, it may fail to overcome switching costs.

The concrete example he gives is an “AI powered doc editor” that is “2x better” [0:28]. Even in that case, he asks whether users are going to “move all your stuff” into it, and answers: “Maybe, probably not” [0:32]. The implication is that consumer AI products need more than incremental quality improvements. They may need a compelling new behavior, a distribution advantage, or a much stronger reason for users to abandon existing tools.

Because the transcript is very short, it does not provide a full framework for evaluating AI startups, nor does it describe specific companies, product strategies, technical architectures, or investment advice. The supported substance is narrowly focused on market structure, attention consolidation, switching costs, and the challenge of consumer AI product adoption.

Practical Notes

For founders or product teams, the transcript supports a few practical decision points.

A consumer AI product should be evaluated not only by how much better it is than existing tools, but by whether that improvement is enough to overcome switching costs. Krieger’s document-editor example is the clearest case: even a “2x better” AI-powered editor may not persuade users to migrate all of their existing work [0:28].

Distribution is a central risk. The early Instagram example suggests that app-store discovery and category ranking once offered a path for small teams to break out quickly [0:08]. Krieger’s contrast with today’s consolidated top apps implies that new consumer AI startups need to think carefully about how they will reach users whose attention is already absorbed by large platforms.

Product teams should distinguish between a feature improvement and a breakthrough experience. The transcript does not define what a breakthrough consumer AI experience would look like, but it does clearly argue that incremental utility may not be enough in a market dominated by TikTok, Reels, and other top apps [0:17].

A key caveat is that the transcript is sparse. It does not include implementation steps, commands, model choices, pricing strategies, go-to-market tactics, or detailed examples beyond Instagram, TikTok, Reels, and an AI-powered document editor. Any broader strategy would go beyond the available evidence.
