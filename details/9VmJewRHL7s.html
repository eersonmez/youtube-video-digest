Ada Palmer explains why Italians viewed the pope differently from distant Catholics because of the papacy’s local political power.

What This Video Is About

This video explains how Catholics’ perceptions of the pope could differ sharply depending on geography. Ada Palmer’s central point is that, in the period being discussed, the pope was not only a spiritual leader but also a temporal political power. That meant the pope could participate in ordinary power politics, including warfare, patronage, rivalries, and the promotion of relatives and allies.

The key contrast is between distant Catholics and Italians. For Catholics in places such as Denmark or Iceland, the pope was far away and encountered mainly through formal religious-political spectacle: impressive papal representatives, public ceremonies, and honors given to the visiting envoy. From that distance, the papacy could appear majestic, sacred, and worthy of deference.

For Italians, the pope was much more familiar and much less abstract. Italians might know the pope’s youth, family, past misconduct, personal rivals, and political record. They also directly experienced the consequences of a papal rise to power: the pope’s relatives and allies being promoted, and others being pushed out. This proximity made it easier for Italians to see the pope as a flawed and ambitious political figure, not merely as the exalted head of the Church.

Detailed Walkthrough

The clip opens by establishing the pope’s dual role: “not just a spiritual leader, but a temporal power” [0:00]. This distinction is essential. A purely spiritual leader might be imagined as standing above ordinary politics, but a temporal ruler governs territory, commands resources, makes alliances, and fights wars. Palmer immediately emphasizes the tension this creates: the pope and his son are “literally fighting wars against other Catholics,” while those Catholics fight back [0:04]. The question is not simply military but conceptual: what does it mean to be a Catholic in armed conflict with the pope?

Palmer answers by stressing geography: “geographic proximity is everything” [0:13]. Distance changes the way religious authority is experienced. If someone lives in Denmark or Iceland, the pope is far away, and ordinary contact with him is indirect. The pope appears through an “incredibly impressive” papal legate or envoy, accompanied by “vast pomp and circumstance” [0:20]. The local city might even rename a street to honor the fact that someone sent by the pope has visited [0:28]. Under those circumstances, the papacy is mediated through spectacle and ceremony. The pope is not encountered as an ordinary political operator but as the distant source of a grand and sacred authority.

From that remote perspective, Palmer says, “it’s easy to have a lot of respect for that pope” [0:28]. The respect is not necessarily irrational; it is shaped by the limited kind of evidence people receive. They see ceremonial grandeur, formal honors, and representatives of papal authority. They do not regularly see the pope’s local bargains, family networks, or personal failings.

The Italian perspective is different because the pope is local. Palmer gives a blunt example: in Italy, the pope may be “that [person] who went to college with your brother and beat him up” [0:36]. Instead of being a distant sacred figure, he is someone whose biography and reputation are socially known. People may know that he was “drunken and irresponsible in middle age” [0:45]. They may have negotiated with him when he held other offices [0:45]. In short, Italians may know him as a human being embedded in elite networks, with flaws, grudges, ambitions, and a history.

This local knowledge extends beyond the individual pope to his family and rivals. Palmer says Italians know “his family,” “the other jerks who are also competitors,” and “all of his dirty laundry” [0:50]. The papacy, from this vantage point, is not only an institution of spiritual leadership but also the prize at the center of a competitive political field. The people around the pope matter because they stand to gain when he gains power.

The final point concerns patronage. When a pope “moves into power,” everyone related to him is likely to be promoted within Italy, while those outside his network are likely to be removed [0:59]. This gives Italians a direct material stake in papal politics. A new pope does not merely change the spiritual atmosphere; he reshapes offices, influence, access, careers, and local balances of power. For those living near the machinery of papal government, the pope’s rise can mean advancement for one faction and displacement for another.

The transcript ends mid-sentence, but the implication is clear: proximity made it much easier for Italians to see the pope in political and personal terms [1:07]. Distant Catholics encountered the papacy as ceremony and authority; Italians encountered it as family networks, rivalries, patronage, and sometimes war.
