The concept of freedom as understood by Machiavelli, contrasting it with arbitrary power.

Dwarkesh Patel · 2026-06-27T21:51:54+00:00 · What Machiavelli thought freedom actually was - Ada Palmer

What This Video Is About

This video explores Niccolò Machiavelli's specific definition of freedom, contrasting it sharply with mere personal safety. The central argument is that true liberty is not simply the absence of a single person who could summarily execute an individual on the street. Instead, for Machiavelli and the Florentines, freedom was intrinsically linked to the existence of a structured system—one requiring due process, public examination, and established procedures before any punitive action against a citizen could be taken.

The concept held profound cultural weight among the Florentine people. They deeply valued this tradition of liberty, which was symbolized by their banner, 'libertas.' This sense of freedom was so fundamental to their identity that it represented something distinct from the mere protection offered by even the most benevolent ruler; the institutional framework itself mattered more than the current governing power.

Detailed Walkthrough

The discussion begins with a clear demarcation of what Machiavelli considered unfree [0:00]. If an individual lives in a state where someone can walk past them on the street, point at them, and order their summary execution without recourse, that person is not free. Conversely, liberty exists within a system that mandates a trial, requires a process to be followed, and demands public examination before any action against a citizen is taken [0:16].

The example provided illustrates this concept through the hypothetical conquest of Florence by Valentino. If Valentino took over, the established system would cease to exist, meaning there would be a man capable of walking down the street, pointing at a Florentine citizen, and having that person killed [0:24]. This lack of process was highly significant to Machiavelli and the populace.

The Florentines demonstrated their commitment to this tradition by constantly being willing to risk their lives while flying the banner proclaiming 'libertas' [0:40]. The concept of liberty, symbolized by the banner 'libertas,' served as the code of arms for the Senate, which was comprised of the top one percent of the elite [0:48]. Despite this deep cultural attachment to this tradition of liberty—the requirement of process over arbitrary power—it was understood that even the most benevolent tyrant could not entirely erase this ingrained understanding from the Florentine culture [1:11].

Key Takeaways

  • True freedom, according to Machiavelli's perspective, is defined by systemic processes rather than just personal safety.
  • The absence of a system allowing for due process means an individual can be subject to arbitrary execution on the street.
  • Liberty requires a structured mechanism involving trials and public examination before any punitive action against a citizen.
  • The Florentines held 'libertas' as a deeply ingrained cultural value, symbolized by their banner.
  • 'Libertas' was associated with the Senate, which represented the top one percent of the elite in Florence.
  • Even a benevolent tyrant could not completely eradicate the deep-seated tradition of liberty within the Florentine mindset.
