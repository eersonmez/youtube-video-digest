Alex Kantrowitz and Ranjan Roy discuss OpenAI’s new enterprise superapp, Meta’s AI pricing strategy, and AI cheating at Brown University.

Alex Kantrowitz · 2026-07-13T17:00:06+00:00 · OpenAI Finally Ships Its Superapp, Meta’s AI Price War, ChatGPT Cheating At Brown

What This Video Is About

This episode of the Big Technology Podcast Friday edition features Alex Kantrowitz and Ranjan Roy discussing three connected AI stories: OpenAI’s reported enterprise “superapp” and ChatGPT Work product, Meta’s aggressive AI model pricing and possible AI cloud business, and a Brown University cheating controversy involving students apparently using ChatGPT on a take-home economics exam.

The central thread is that AI products are converging. OpenAI, Anthropic, Writer, Notion, Cursor, and others are all moving toward tools that let users ask AI systems to do work across applications, data sources, documents, code, spreadsheets, presentations, research, and business workflows. The hosts debate whether the long-term value will come from the underlying AI models themselves or from the product scaffolding, integrations, domain expertise, and services that make those models useful inside real organizations.

The Meta discussion broadens this into a business-model question. If frontier model capability becomes more interchangeable and companies such as Meta offer high-performing models at much lower prices, OpenAI and Anthropic may face pressure on margins and differentiation. Meta’s strategy is framed as both defensive and offensive: it can push down AI costs for the industry, weaken potential competitors, improve its own consumer AI ambitions, and possibly rent out compute if demand for AI infrastructure stays high.

The final segment shifts from enterprise and platform strategy to education. The hosts discuss a Brown University professor who moved a midterm exam to a take-home format after a campus shooting, then saw unusually high scores that he believed were likely caused by AI-assisted cheating. When the final exam returned to an in-person format, many students dropped, skipped, or failed the course. The discussion treats this not only as a cheating case, but as evidence that education and assessment need to adapt to a world where AI tools are widely available.

Detailed Walkthrough

The episode opens by framing the three main stories: OpenAI’s “super app,” Meta undercutting frontier AI labs on pricing, and Ivy League students using AI to cheat [0:00]. Alex Kantrowitz introduces OpenAI’s reported launch of ChatGPT Work, described as a new enterprise agent that can tap into corporate data to automate spreadsheets, presentations, financial forecasts, research, and more complex business tasks. He also says OpenAI unveiled a desktop “super app” that combines ChatGPT, Codex, and ChatGPT Work.

Kantrowitz presents this as OpenAI’s answer to Anthropic’s Claude Co-work product. He notes that OpenAI appears to have realized Codex is better than ChatGPT for long-running, multi-step tasks that use external tools. The larger question is whether all AI applications are converging on the same basic purpose: not just chatting, but getting work done for users.

Ranjan Roy responds from the perspective of someone working in enterprise AI. He describes the category as “natural language driven agentic workflow building,” meaning systems where business users can build or trigger workflows through conversation rather than conventional programming. He says this resembles what Codex does in a command-line context, but moved into a more chat-based business interface. He also says he tried ChatGPT Work and found it underwhelming and rushed compared with Claude Co-work, which he describes as more gradually built out and now integrated into Claude as a separate button.

The hosts then discuss how difficult differentiation becomes when every AI booth, pitch, and product starts to sound like “enterprise agentic AI.” Roy argues that differentiation will come less from saying “we have agents” and more from the surrounding infrastructure that makes those agents useful in real companies. For example, he describes a marketing organization that needs more than a tone-of-voice document. It needs a foundation of brand knowledge, guidelines, product knowledge, connectors, and workflows so that agents can do correct work out of the box [5:00].

Kantrowitz pushes back with his own experience organizing the Big Technology Summit using off-the-shelf AI tools. He says Claude Code built the website, embedded Luma, Claude Co-work helped create a prospectus for speakers and sponsors, ChatGPT helped design stage graphics, and ChatGPT handled scheduling through a single chat thread. His argument is that bespoke enterprise software was not necessary for many real tasks; the AI tools worked once given references and instructions.

Roy counters by scaling up the scenario. A small event can be handled with general-purpose AI tools, but a large enterprise may have 221,000 websites, 17 regulatory agencies, six major brands, multiple CRM systems, and complex rules about communication, compliance, and product knowledge. In that environment, the “agent” is only useful if the system has reliable access to the right data, connectors, policies, and expertise. This is where he sees the competitive battleground.

The conversation then turns to Palantir and Alex Karp’s “forward deployed engineer” model [10:00]. Kantrowitz asks whether OpenAI and Anthropic are effectively moving toward versions of Palantir-style consultants who help organizations put AI into action. Roy agrees that this kind of high-touch, human service layer is part of the current reality. He says the industry may combine forward deployed engineers, domain experts, or “AI sommeliers” with increasingly productized expertise. The companies that win may be those that encode expertise for specific kinds of work into their platforms.

Roy resists the term “verticalized,” because in traditional SaaS it usually means software built for a specific industry. He thinks the AI version may be more about the type of work being done rather than the type of company. For example, sales and marketing workers may use different AI systems than researchers, not merely because they are in different industries, but because their workflows, knowledge needs, and outputs differ.

Kantrowitz then revives a recurring debate: is the durable value in AI products or in AI models? From the perspective of the big AI labs, specialized consulting and product scaffolding may only be temporary bridges. If the model becomes intelligent enough, it may be able to crawl a company’s information, understand the brand voice, ingest the relevant context, generate a plan, and execute at scale with far less human setup [15:00].

Roy agrees that this remains the central unresolved debate. He says labs are likely betting that more capable models will eventually subsume much of the scaffolding required to make agents work today. He gives the example of parsing PDFs: two years earlier, developers might have needed to define tools or upload scripts; now models handle much of that automatically. Model progress can make previously necessary product architecture disappear. But he still sees scaffolding, integrations, and domain setup as crucial in the near term.

The hosts then consider a hypothetical future three years out, in which several companies have highly capable AGI-like systems built into products such as Codex, ChatGPT, Anthropic’s apps, Meta’s products, and perhaps Apple’s iPhone. If multiple companies can do roughly the same thing, Kantrowitz asks, does everything become a race to the bottom on price?

This leads into the economics of AI usage. Kantrowitz references Sam Altman saying a future GPT model was significantly more token-efficient than a previous model, and he argues that lower input costs improve enterprise return on investment. Roy responds that he is more focused on the “R” in ROI, meaning the return, while Kantrowitz is focused on the “I,” the investment. Roy says cost has suddenly become a much bigger topic among enterprise leaders. Six months earlier, executives were not talking as much about token economics; now they are.

Kantrowitz argues that the cost discussion is becoming real because agents are much more token-intensive than earlier generative AI use cases [20:00]. As AI usage inside companies grows, bills rise sharply, and leadership begins asking whether productivity gains justify the expense. He mentions Anthropic’s revenue run-rate growth as an example of how quickly enterprise AI spending may be expanding, while noting that ARR can be an imperfect measurement.

The next major topic is Meta’s MSpark 1.1 model and aggressive API pricing. Kantrowitz reads from Bloomberg reporting that Meta introduced a new model with pricing around 25% of what OpenAI and Anthropic charge for other top models. Mark Zuckerberg is quoted saying this is the first time Meta is doing a serious API for a non-open-source model and that pricing will be attractive and aggressive.

Roy sees this as a major headwind for OpenAI and Anthropic. If models become more interchangeable and developers can route workloads to cheaper models, the economics change. He describes model interoperability and “the right model for the right task” as increasingly important. In a normal technology cycle, expensive new technologies often become cheaper over time. The problem for OpenAI and Anthropic, he says, is that their valuations, fundraising, and possible IPO paths may depend on maintaining more favorable economics.

Kantrowitz argues that Zuckerberg may be deliberately playing spoiler [25:00]. Meta has historically responded aggressively to threats by copying or neutralizing competitors, such as Stories from Snapchat or Reels from TikTok. AI is a threat because users may spend more time with ChatGPT-like assistants and less time on social media. By pushing down model prices, Meta can weaken companies that might threaten its attention-based business while also supporting its own ambition to build consumer AI or “personal superintelligence.”

The hosts also discuss Meta’s distribution advantage. Roy notes that billions of people use Meta products monthly, giving the company a massive front door to consumer AI. If AI becomes cheap enough, Meta can integrate it deeply across Instagram, Facebook, Messenger, and other products. Kantrowitz adds that Meta may also use lower AI costs as leverage when negotiating with OpenAI, Anthropic, or Google for model access.

Kantrowitz then cites analysis suggesting Meta’s model strategy may support a broader AI cloud business. Meta could make money by offering model APIs, selling or renting compute, and positioning itself as a lower-cost alternative. Roy says this shows that companies are starting to map out where they fit in the future AI economy rather than simply assuming overall AI demand will explode and lift everyone [30:00].

The discussion then returns to the business prospects of OpenAI and Anthropic. Roy says it is hard to see how a world of cheaper, interoperable models is good for them unless their models remain so superior that they absorb specialized products and dominate user behavior. Kantrowitz suggests one analogy may be laptop makers: multiple companies can make valuable products, but they often compete on cost, differentiation, and ecosystem rather than enjoying unlimited pricing power. He also notes that OpenAI and Anthropic have built compelling products, such as ChatGPT, Claude Code, and Claude Co-work, which may continue to drive growth even if pricing power declines.

Kantrowitz and Roy debate whether eventual public filings, such as S-1 documents, would clarify the picture [35:00]. Kantrowitz argues that traditional filings are backward-looking and may not reveal much because the real battle is only just beginning. The business these companies have run so far may not be the same business they run going forward. Roy counters that bad financials could still affect stock price, employee morale, researcher retention, and the ability to keep competing.

After a break, the episode turns to reports that Meta may rent out AI compute through a cloud business. Kantrowitz cites Bloomberg reporting that Zuckerberg said Meta needs all the compute it can get, but that offers for compute are so high it might make sense in some cases to rent it out. Zuckerberg also said the potential for a cloud business is there anytime Meta wants to build it.

Kantrowitz responds to a listener comment about opportunity cost. The listener argued that if demand for compute exceeds supply, Meta should rent out excess compute and profit rather than struggle to use it internally. Kantrowitz rejects this as too narrow. If Anthropic is willing to pay high prices for compute, it is because Anthropic believes it has a product that can mark up that compute into a valuable business. In tech, companies often invest early in products that may become more profitable later rather than simply renting out resources for immediate returns.

Roy connects this back to the commoditization discussion [40:00]. If Anthropic can charge a lot for its product, it can afford expensive compute. But if a price war forces Anthropic to use cheaper models or lower prices, it may no longer be able to pay as much for compute. Meta, meanwhile, can take both sides: rent compute when prices are high, and push model prices down when that helps weaken competitors or improve Meta’s own consumer AI.

The hosts then briefly discuss AI-generated video. They contrast earlier crude examples, such as Will Smith eating pasta, with newer AI videos they find more compelling and shareable. They discuss examples like AI-generated Erling Haaland memes around the World Cup and “Peptide Seinfeld,” described as a Seinfeld-like AI video in which George goes on peptides and becomes better-looking and more muscular. Roy argues that when AI-generated media is not pretending to be real, it may simply be a new form of content rather than “slop.”

This leads into Meta’s Muse image tool and privacy concerns [45:00]. Kantrowitz cites Guardian reporting that Instagram’s AI image generator alarmed privacy experts because it can use public Instagram profiles by default. Users of Meta’s Muse AI image tool can tag public Instagram profiles and generate pictures that pull from faces of people featured in social posts, according to the reporting he cites. Instagram users are not notified when their posts are integrated into Meta’s image generation system.

Kantrowitz gives a practical opt-out instruction: on Instagram, tap the hamburger menu in the top right, go to “sharing and reuse,” and toggle off the button that allows other people to reuse your content. He says he disabled it on his personal profile but left it on for the Big Technology Podcast account because remixes might be beneficial for the show. The hosts frame the issue as typical Meta behavior: strong AI momentum mixed with privacy-insensitive defaults.

The final segment covers the Brown University cheating case [48:00]. Kantrowitz cites Inside Higher Ed reporting about Brown economics professor Roberto Serrano, who taught welfare economics and social choice theory. After a December mass shooting at Brown, some students were anxious about being in a classroom, so Serrano gave a take-home midterm for the first time in nearly two decades of teaching the course.

According to the account Kantrowitz reads, Serrano later suspected dozens of students had used AI to cheat, because many earned perfect or near-perfect scores on the take-home midterm. He then made the final exam in-person. The transcript says 18 students dropped the class, nine stayed enrolled but did not show up for the final, three got a zero, and the average final score was 48.6%, compared with a 96% average on the midterm. Historically, Kantrowitz says, the final average had never dropped below 68%.

Kantrowitz quotes Serrano saying society cannot afford for a significant fraction of its best young minds to think cheating is okay, because that leads to a declining or failed society and “we cannot choose to become idiots” [50:00]. Roy is less sympathetic to the professor’s framing. He argues that education should fundamentally change based on the tools now available. If students have access to AI, exams should test who can produce the best work with those tools, or should focus more on understanding, insight, and reasoning rather than information retrieval and calculation.

The hosts agree that assessment should adapt. Kantrowitz compares AI use to outsourcing lower-level tasks so that the human can focus on harder problems. He says that since giving “lesser activities” to AI, he feels he is thinking through tougher questions and that his brain may be getting stronger rather than weaker [55:00]. Roy gives the analogy of navigation: he can drive around where he grew up without a map, but depends on Google Maps in the New York area because that part of cognition has been outsourced. Similarly, he says he no longer makes slides himself, which frees him to think more about what should go in them.

The episode closes with the contrast between students who maintained high performance on both the take-home midterm and in-person final, and others whose scores collapsed. The hosts do not deny that cheating may have happened, but they treat the larger lesson as institutional: universities need to redesign teaching and evaluation for an AI-saturated environment rather than relying on old exam formats and then being surprised when students use new tools.

Key Takeaways

  • OpenAI’s reported ChatGPT Work product is aimed at enterprise automation. It is described as an agent that can use corporate data to create spreadsheets, presentations, financial forecasts, research, and other complex work outputs.
  • OpenAI’s desktop “superapp” is framed as a merger of ChatGPT, Codex, and ChatGPT Work. The hosts interpret this as recognition that Codex is strong at long-running, multi-step tasks involving tools, while ChatGPT remains the broader conversational interface.
  • AI products are converging on “getting work done,” not just chat. Anthropic’s Claude Co-work, OpenAI’s ChatGPT Work, Codex, Writer, Notion, Cursor, and other tools are all moving toward agentic workflows controlled through natural language.
  • Enterprise differentiation may come from scaffolding, not just models. Roy argues that large companies need connectors, policy knowledge, brand rules, regulatory context, product knowledge, CRM integration, and domain expertise before AI agents can reliably create value.
  • The model-versus-product debate remains unresolved. The labs may believe better models will eventually eliminate much of today’s scaffolding, just as models have already made previously complex tasks like PDF parsing easier. But current enterprise deployments still require significant setup.
  • Agentic AI makes cost more important. Because agents perform multi-step work and use many more tokens, companies that previously ignored token costs are now asking whether AI spending produces measurable productivity gains.
  • Meta’s MSpark 1.1 pricing could intensify an AI price war. Zuckerberg’s reported API pricing, around 25% of competitors’ top-model pricing, is interpreted as a deliberate attempt to commoditize the model layer and pressure OpenAI and Anthropic.
  • Meta may benefit whether AI compute stays expensive or gets cheaper. If compute demand remains high, Meta can rent infrastructure. If model pricing collapses, Meta can weaken competitors and make AI cheaper for its own consumer products.
  • OpenAI and Anthropic face business-model pressure if models become interchangeable. Their path is strongest if their products and models remain clearly superior; it is weaker if enterprises can route tasks among cheaper models with similar performance.
  • Meta’s Muse image tool raises privacy and consent concerns. The transcript says public Instagram content can be reused by default in AI image generation unless users opt out through Instagram’s sharing and reuse settings.
  • The Brown University case shows how AI disrupts assessment. A take-home midterm produced unusually high scores, while the in-person final produced drops, absences, zeros, and a historically low average, leading the professor to suspect AI-enabled cheating.
  • The hosts argue education should adapt rather than simply ban tools. They suggest assessments should test understanding, judgment, and higher-order reasoning in a world where AI can handle calculations, summaries, drafts, slides, and other lower-level tasks.

Practical Notes

For enterprise AI adoption, the transcript supports a workflow distinction between small-team use and large-organization deployment. A small event or project can be managed with general-purpose AI tools: Claude Code can build a website, Luma can be embedded for event registration, Claude Co-work can help draft a sponsor or speaker prospectus, ChatGPT can help design graphics, and a single ChatGPT thread can help coordinate schedules. But this workflow does not automatically scale to enterprises with many websites, brands, regulatory obligations, data systems, and customer relationship management tools.

For larger organizations, the decision point is whether to rely on general-purpose models and prompting or invest in systems with deeper integrations. Useful layers mentioned in the conversation include brand knowledge, tone and messaging guidelines, product knowledge ingestion, CRM connectors, event tools such as Luma, regulatory constraints, and prebuilt workflows for specific business functions. Roy argues that without this foundation, “agents” may not produce correct or valuable work.

For AI vendors, the practical architecture choice is whether to compete primarily on model capability, product experience, domain expertise, service layers, or cost. The transcript describes a likely mix: forward deployed engineers or consultants for high-touch implementation, productized expertise for repeatable workflows, and increasingly intelligent models that reduce the need for manual scaffolding over time.

For companies managing AI costs, the transcript supports model interoperability as a practical strategy. Organizations may route different tasks to different models, use cheaper models where possible, and reserve expensive frontier models for tasks where they produce enough value to justify the cost. This becomes more important as agentic workflows increase token usage.

For Meta’s AI strategy, the transcript identifies several possible moves: offer a lower-cost API for MSpark 1.1, use lower pricing to pressure OpenAI and Anthropic, rent out AI compute when market prices are attractive, integrate AI into consumer products with massive distribution, and use cheaper models to support “personal superintelligence” ambitions.

For Instagram users concerned about AI reuse, the transcript gives an opt-out path: open Instagram, tap the hamburger menu in the top right, go to “sharing and reuse,” and toggle off the setting that allows other people to reuse your content. The transcript does not provide more detailed menu screenshots or confirm every jurisdiction’s settings, but it clearly states this as the suggested privacy step.

For educators, the Brown case highlights several implementation choices. Take-home exams are now vulnerable to AI use unless explicitly designed around it. In-person exams can still test unaided understanding, but may expose large gaps between AI-assisted output and individual mastery. A middle path would be to redesign assignments so that students are allowed to use AI but must demonstrate reasoning, interpretation, critique, and original insight.

Risks and caveats mentioned or implied by the discussion include: enterprise AI tools may look similar at the surface but differ greatly in implementation quality; price wars may undermine high-valuation AI business models; S-1 filings may be backward-looking and fail to capture the current competitive transition; AI-generated content can become misleading if presented as real; Meta’s default reuse of public Instagram material may create privacy concerns; and students may learn to produce polished work without developing underlying competence if assessments are poorly designed.
