Dr. Chinasa T. Okolo discusses AI governance, equity, and the challenges facing the global majority.

Scott Hanselman · 2026-07-17T16:33:09+00:00 · Who is left behind when AI moves fast? with Dr. Chinasa T. Okolo

What This Video Is About

This video features a discussion with Dr. Chinasa T. Okolo, an internationally recognized researcher, strategist, and policy advisor on AI governance and safety for the global majority. The central theme revolves around the risks associated with the rapid advancement of Artificial Intelligence and how these technologies impact populations in the Global South and the broader "global majority."

Dr. Okolo cautions against a purely optimistic view of AI's trajectory, highlighting that policymakers often lack the necessary technical expertise to govern complex systems effectively. A significant concern raised is the danger of "technosolutionist theater," where superficial AI deployments mask deeper systemic issues such as inadequate infrastructure, poverty, or corruption. The discussion stresses that meaningful progress requires addressing fundamental societal needs—like reliable electricity and fair wages—before applying advanced AI solutions.

Furthermore, the conversation advocates for a shift toward local autonomy in AI development. Instead of simply importing technological frameworks from Western nations, true sovereignty demands building local capacity, curating indigenous data, and establishing independent governance structures that prioritize the needs of frontline workers over mere productivity gains dictated by large tech companies.

Detailed Walkthrough

The discussion begins with an exploration of public perception regarding AI, noting that while some fear job replacement, Dr. Okolo remains cautiously optimistic, emphasizing that humans control how AI is shaped and deployed [1:48]. She clarifies the common misconception that "AI" only refers to large language models like ChatGPT; in reality, many governments have used basic machine learning systems for decades in areas like predicting economic issues or delivering government services [2:33, 3:03].

Dr. Okolo's expertise is cross-cutting, spanning computer science and ethnographic fieldwork focused on the Global South and healthcare [4:18]. She argues that AI cannot be treated as a purely technical problem; it intersects deeply with infrastructure, safety, and healthcare [4:43]. A key concern she raises is that policymakers often possess only a limited understanding of these complex systems, which are further complicated by AI integration [5:44].

The concept of Explainable AI (XAI) is discussed. Initially, the focus was on explaining the system to the user; however, Dr. Okolo suggests a more critical question: should the AI have been imposed on the user at all? Her field work in India with community healthcare workers showed that when these workers were pressured to adopt AI tools without understanding them, they risked deferring to potentially false decisions made by the computer [8:16]. XAI can help maintain autonomy if users understand *how* a decision was reached, but this is contingent on their ability to refuse the technology in the first place [9:31].

The conversation then shifts to the skills of AI researchers and students. Dr. Okolo notes that over-reliance on Large Language Models (LLMs) can cause a decline in critical thinking skills because fundamental tasks like searching, reading literature, interpreting nuances, and synthesizing information are outsourced to chatbots [12:04]. This reliance risks devaluing the research process itself [12:29].

A major critique is leveled against "technosolutionist theater." This occurs when external entities deploy polished AI solutions—like an Arduino setup in a remote village—without addressing underlying issues like lack of stable electricity, poor infrastructure, or insufficient funding for long-term support [23:41, 25:06]. The problem is often not the technology itself but where the money is going and whether it addresses fundamental needs [21:33].

Regarding AI sovereignty, Dr. Okolo posits that true autonomy requires more than just local compute or models. It necessitates the power to independently develop capacity, curate one's own data, compensate labor equitably for this curation, and possess independent infrastructure like AI clusters [27:55]. While global connectivity is unavoidable due to shared supply chains (chips, subsea cables), sovereignty must be pursued at a higher philosophical level of self-determination.

Finally, when addressing what big tech companies should demand beyond funding, Dr. Okolo insists on true independence in governance framework development. She advocates for the inclusion of neutral external stakeholders to prevent undue influence from corporate funders or affiliated groups [31:22]. A potential success story involves modular infrastructure solutions, such as micro data centers built in shipping containers by companies like Aman in Kenya and Barbados, coupled with greater electrification and renewable energy adoption across regions [33:17].

Key Takeaways

  • AI deployment must address underlying systemic issues—such as electricity access, wages, and corruption—before being considered a viable solution for the global majority.
  • There is a significant risk of "technosolutionist theater," where superficial technological fixes are used to mask deeper societal problems.
  • True sovereignty in AI requires local capacity building, including the ability to curate proprietary data and develop indigenous models.
  • Over-reliance on LLMs can lead to the erosion of critical thinking skills among researchers by outsourcing fundamental cognitive tasks like literature synthesis.
  • Explainability (XAI) is valuable for maintaining user autonomy, but it is insufficient if the user lacks the practical power or ability to refuse the technology being imposed upon them.
  • The concept of "global majority" emphasizes that marginalized populations constitute a significant portion of the world's population, demanding consideration beyond traditional economic indicators.
  • Sustainable AI solutions in low-resource areas require long-term support structures, including cloud maintenance and community engagement, rather than short-term deployments.
  • Big tech companies must ensure true independence when advising on national AI strategies, preventing corporate influence from shaping governance frameworks.

Practical Notes

  • Infrastructure Prerequisites: Before deploying AI in regions with limited resources, foundational needs like stable electricity and internet connectivity must be met [22:41].
  • Sovereignty Components: Achieving AI sovereignty involves local compute power, the development of local language models, independent data curation, and establishing self-governance over AI deployment.
  • Design Principle: Solutions should move from a "push technology" model (imposing solutions) to a "pulled" model where the community defines the problem first [26:17].
  • Corporate Engagement Demand: Policy experts demand true independence in governance development, requiring neutral external stakeholders to advise against undue influence by large tech funders or affiliated groups.
  • Example of Local Infrastructure: Aman (based in Kenya) is cited for developing modular micro data centers using shipping containers in partnership with Barbados [33:17].
