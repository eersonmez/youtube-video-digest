Seven dietary additions that can help lower blood sugar by improving insulin sensitivity.

Leonid Kim MD · 2026-06-29T16:00:36+00:00 · These 7 Foods Lower Blood Sugar QUICKLY (Backed by Science)

What This Video Is About

This video provides an educational overview of seven specific dietary additions—including certain foods and drinks—that may assist in lowering blood sugar levels and improving insulin sensitivity. The central premise is that achieving better blood sugar control does not require eliminating carbohydrates or severely restricting food groups; rather, it involves strategically adding beneficial items to the diet.

The speaker emphasizes that the most significant metabolic improvements are achieved by "stacking" a few of these recommended items together consistently over time, as opposed to relying on any single intervention. The discussion delves into the underlying biological mechanisms for each item, such as slowing glucose conversion or improving cellular energy use, while also providing practical advice and necessary precautions for implementation.

It is crucial to understand that this content is presented as educational information only and does not constitute personal medical advice; viewers are strongly advised to consult with a healthcare professional before making any changes to their health regimen.

Detailed Walkthrough

The video introduces seven components, detailing the science behind each one:

1. Vinegar (specifically acidic acid): This component works on two levels when consuming carbohydrates. First, the acidic acid partially deactivates alpha amylase, an enzyme responsible for turning starch into glucose, thereby slowing down how fast carbs are converted to blood sugar [1:13-1:29]. Second, it activates AMPK (AMP-activated protein kinase), which acts as a cellular energy sensor. When activated, cells shift from storing glucose to burning fat for fuel [1:36-1:59]. A meta-analysis of 25 clinical trials found that apple cider vinegar consumption lowered A1C by nearly a full percentage point (0.9%) [2:11-2:24]. The recommended practice is one tablespoon per day, diluted in at least 8 to 10 ounces of water, consumed right before a meal, especially a carb-heavy one, using a straw for dental protection [2:42-3:06].

2. Fenugreek Seeds: This seed contains four hydroxyisoleucine, which sharpens the insulin response by nudging pancreatic beta cells to release more insulin only when blood sugar is high [3:25-3:38]. Furthermore, fenugreek possesses galactomannan fiber, a gel-forming fiber that slows down sugar absorption and lowers postmeal glucose spikes [3:42-3:49]. A 2024 meta-analysis of 19 randomized controlled trials showed that fenugreek supplementation significantly lowered A1C by about 0.5% and also improved cholesterol markers [3:56-4:17]. The suggested workflow is to soak one teaspoon of seeds overnight, drink the water, and chew the seeds in the morning [4:20-4:24].

3. Extra Virgin Olive Oil: This oil is rich in polyphenols like hydroxytyrosol and oleuropein, which reduce inflammation, allowing cells to respond better to insulin [5:10-5:24]. Additionally, its main fat, oleic acid, slows gastric emptying, ensuring carbohydrates from a meal arrive gently rather than all at once, preventing sharp glucose spikes [5:26-5:38]. The Predime trial in Spain indicated that those on a Mediterranean diet plus extra virgin olive oil were 40% less likely to develop Type 2 diabetes compared to the control group, without changes to calorie restriction or activity levels [5:42-6:01].

4. Grass-Fed Beef: This specific type of red meat contains Conjugated Linoleic Acid (CLA), which improves insulin sensitivity by targeting the PP gamma receptor—the same target for drugs like thiazolidinediones [7:04-7:18]. It also provides L-carnitine, directing cells to burn excess calories as fuel instead of storing them as fat in mitochondria [7:18-7:28]. Grass-fed beef is also rich in micronutrients such as zinc, iron, and Vitamin B12 necessary for metabolism [7:30-7:39].

5. Protein Intake (General Principle): While not a single food item, the video stresses that adequate protein intake is essential for reversing insulin resistance. Specifically, consuming 30 grams of protein at breakfast has been shown to lower daily hunger and quiet brain reward centers, leading to less snacking [8:17-8:31]. A general target mentioned is 1.2 to 1.6 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight, though basing goals on lean tissue mass is suggested for greater accuracy [8:39-8:50].

6. Green Tea: The beneficial compound in green tea is EGCG (epigallocatechin gallate), which helps cells respond more effectively to insulin and reduces inflammation [11:17-11:28]. Studies showed average A1C reductions of about 2% in people with Type 2 diabetes when adding green tea, but the main benefit cited is stacking it with other interventions and reducing visceral fat long term [11:36-11:50].

7. Cinnamon: This spice contains cinnamaldehyde, which targets the pathway cells use to respond to insulin, helping them pull sugar out of the bloodstream [13:05-13:21]. A meta-analysis found that cinnamon reduced postmeal glucose by almost 40 milligrams per deciliter and showed an average A1C reduction of 0.5% [13:25-13:37].

Long-Term/Alternative: Fatty fish (sardines, mackerel, salmon) are highlighted as a "long game" food because they contain omega-3s like EPA and DHA, which target visceral fat and inflammation—the root causes of insulin resistance over years [14:45-15:02]. Chia seeds offer an ALA source of omega-3s, but supplementation with microalgae oil may be necessary to obtain sufficient EPA and DHA.

Key Takeaways

  • The fastest way to lower blood sugar involves adding beneficial foods rather than eliminating entire food groups like carbohydrates [0:06-0:12].
  • Metabolic improvement is best achieved by "stacking" two or three of these dietary additions consistently, not by relying on any single item [0:44-0:46; 15:58-16:03].
  • Vinegar slows glucose absorption in the gut and activates AMPK to promote fat burning at the cellular level [1:29-1:59].
  • Fenugreek seeds contain galactomannan fiber that physically slows sugar absorption, reducing postmeal spikes [3:42-3:49].
  • Extra virgin olive oil improves insulin response by lowering inflammation and slowing gastric emptying to prevent sharp glucose spikes [5:20-5:38].
  • Grass-fed beef provides CLA and L-carnitine, which enhance insulin sensitivity and direct cells to burn fat for fuel [7:04-7:28].
  • Green tea's EGCG helps improve cellular response to insulin and reduces inflammation over time [11:17-11:30].
  • Cinnamon works by targeting the pathway that allows cells to respond to insulin, helping pull sugar from the bloodstream [13:05-13:21].

Practical Notes

Vinegar:

  • Dosage: One tablespoon per day or no more than 15 milliliters.
  • Method: Always dilute in at least 8 to 10 ounces of water.
  • Timing: Best consumed right before a meal, especially one high in carbohydrates.
  • Caveat: Use a straw to protect tooth enamel.

Fenugreek Seeds:

  • Usage: Soak about one teaspoon overnight, then drink the water and chew the seeds in the morning.
  • Precautions: Start slowly due to potential gastrointestinal side effects; avoid if pregnant or breastfeeding. Cross-reactivity with peanut or soybean allergies is possible. Must be discussed with a doctor, especially if on diabetes medications, to prevent unsafe glucose drops.

Extra Virgin Olive Oil:

  • Quality Check: Look for a dark bottle with an early harvest date.
  • Usage: Best used cold or to finish dishes rather than frying.

Grass-Fed Beef:

  • Guidelines: Must be grass-fed and not processed; should always be paired with fiber and consumed in moderation.
  • Protein Goal: Aim for roughly 1.2 to 1.6 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight, though basing goals on lean tissue mass is more accurate.

Green Tea:

  • Warning: Only consume the actual green tea drink; avoid extracts or EGCG supplements due to risks of liver damage from high dosing.
  • Dosing Guideline: Limit intake to 2 to 3 cups per day, as one cup typically contains about 50 to 150 milligrams of catechins.
  • Interaction: Can interfere with iron absorption; avoid consuming it near iron-rich foods or supplements.

Cinnamon:

  • Type Preference: Prefer Ceylon cinnamon over Cassia cinnamon because Cassia can contain coumarin, which is potentially toxic to the liver at high doses.
  • Caution: Cinnamon can interact with medications, particularly blood thinners.
