The future of AI training paradigms suggests a shift from pre-training dominance to continuous real-world learning.

Dwarkesh Patel · 2026-06-26T16:56:10+00:00 · What does the next training paradigm look like?

What This Video Is About

The video explores potential future shifts in Artificial Intelligence (AI) training paradigms, moving beyond current reliance on massive pre-training towards continuous real-world learning. The central hypothesis discussed is that by training AI agents across millions of verifiable tasks within diverse Reinforcement Learning (RL) environments, AGI—an agent capable of handling open-ended tasks with ambiguity and errors over long periods—could be achieved [0:02]. Optimists suggest that scaling compute alone might overcome current limitations like data inefficiency and the lack of continual learning.

However, the discussion pivots to highlight significant hurdles in this vision. While progress is strong in domains like coding due to the availability of deterministic simulators, applying these methods to complex, unstructured real-world skills—such as running a business or practicing law—is far more challenging because those environments are not easily replayable or parallelizable [3:12]. The core challenge identified is that true human-like skill acquisition demands sample efficiency when dealing with scarce, ambiguous data from genuine real-world interactions.

Detailed Walkthrough

The speaker begins by outlining the major research bet: training AIs on millions of verifiable tasks across thousands of diverse RL environments to create a problem-solving agent capable of handling ambiguity [0:02]. Optimists argue that scaling compute can overcome current deficits, such as data inefficiency and lack of continual learning, similar to how LLMs improved with more compute [0:35].

The speaker then contrasts the training phase (a one-time cost) with the operational session where a model's intelligence is truly tested. Current RL training has shown that AI agents can solve increasingly ambitious problems over longer time spans [1:17]. Some argue that continual learning, which updates weights based on deployment experience, might be unnecessary if in-context learning becomes powerful enough to simulate months of job experience within the context window [1:24].

A critical detour addresses why progress in "computer use" is slower than in coding or math. The speaker posits that verifiability alone is insufficient; a domain must also be "grindable," meaning it requires parallel rollouts against a deterministic and replayable simulator [3:16]. For example, while one can create containers with missing features for AI to code solutions against, this does not work trivially for using real websites like Amazon because bots would be detected [3:41].

The speaker argues that for skills requiring interaction with the real world—like building a business or winning court cases—the outer-loop verification might take months or years of actual action, making parallel rollouts impossible [5:08]. This emphasizes the need for sample efficiency when data is sparse and unstructured.

To address this, two main avenues are proposed:

  • On-Policy Self-Distillation (OPSD): This technique aims to distill knowledge learned during a long session back into the base model's weights [13:09]. OPSD encourages the base model to match the predictions of a "teacher model" that has accumulated experience within the context window. It is superior to naive RL because it doesn't require an outer-loop verifiable reward, and it offers denser supervision by training on per-token probability discrepancy between teacher and student [13:29].
  • Dreaming: This highly speculative idea suggests AIs could build simulations of reality internally to rehearse new skills, allowing them to experience vastly more simulated samples than in real time [15:34]. This would be analogous to EfficientZero, where the model plays many internal games for every one step in the actual game.

The long-term vision involves an RLVR agent that is competent enough to be deployed broadly. After initial deployment, its intelligence grows not just from prior training but from accumulating experience across all users globally [19:22]. This continuous improvement relies on a functional continual learning recipe that allows knowledge gained in one session to update the weights effectively.

Key Takeaways

  • The leading research bet is that training AIs on millions of verifiable tasks in diverse RL environments will lead to Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) by creating robust problem-solving agents [0:02].
  • A major bottleneck for AI progress is the requirement for domains to be "grindable," meaning they must allow for parallel rollouts against deterministic and replayable simulators, which is difficult for real-world tasks like business creation [3:16].
  • For skills requiring interaction with scarce, unstructured real-world data (e.g., law, business), sample efficiency during training is paramount because the relevant information may not exist elsewhere [5:49].
  • On-Policy Self-Distillation (OPSD) is a proposed method to distill knowledge from long sessions back into model weights by making the base model mimic a context-accumulated "teacher" model [13:09].
  • OPSD offers denser supervision than naive RL and avoids the need for an outer-loop verifiable reward, focusing instead on per-token probability discrepancy [13:29].
  • The concept of "dreaming" suggests future LLMs might simulate vast amounts of experience internally by building their own environments to rehearse skills before real-world deployment [15:34].
  • A key limitation noted is that short-horizon RL training may not generalize well to long-horizon performance, which affects the ability of agents trained on white-collar tasks to build a business from scratch [7:29].
  • Current online learning methods are limited because they often require predicting the same objective across millions of users (e.g., Cursor Tab model), failing to capture unique, job-specific knowledge needed for true continual learning [10:43].

Practical Notes

  • OPSD Implementation: The core idea is to train a base model to match the predictions made by a teacher model that has processed a long session's context. This requires focusing on the per-token probability discrepancy between the two models rather than relying solely on a single trajectory reward [13:54].
  • Architectural Needs: While sparse attention and KV cache compaction are mentioned as existing architectural optimizations for memory, the speaker suggests architecture may not be the fundamental bottleneck for continual learning; the loss function approach is more critical [12:37].
  • Real-World Application Example (Non-AI): The speaker uses Mercury.com as an example of successful automation where an email address automatically scans and extracts data from incoming invoices to create draft payments, demonstrating a form of automated workflow processing [11:06].
