Dr. Leonid Kim explains who may benefit from magnesium supplementation and how to choose among magnesium forms.

Leonid Kim MD · 2026-06-23T14:30:10+00:00 · Who Actually Needs Magnesium? Which Type Is Right For You (Doctor Explains)

What This Video Is About

This educational video from Dr. Leonid Kim, a double board-certified physician, explains how to think about magnesium supplementation without assuming that everyone needs it or that all magnesium products work the same way. He frames the main issue as a “magnesium gray zone”: many people may not have a severe deficiency that shows up clearly on a standard blood test, but may still have suboptimal magnesium status that affects sleep, stress tolerance, fatigue, muscle symptoms, blood pressure, insulin resistance, migraines, or long-term bone health [0:39].

The video matters because magnesium is involved in more than 300 enzyme reactions and sits at the intersection of cardiovascular function, nervous system regulation, and metabolic health [2:38]. Dr. Kim argues that modern diets and lifestyles can simultaneously reduce magnesium intake and increase magnesium losses. Processed foods tend to contain less magnesium than whole-food counterparts, while stress, alcohol, certain medications, diabetes, and gastrointestinal issues can increase magnesium demand or loss [5:10].

A central practical point is that “magnesium” is not one uniform supplement. Different forms behave differently. Some are better absorbed, some stay in the digestive tract and help constipation, and some are marketed for brain, muscle, or cardiovascular effects despite limited human evidence [10:18]. The video walks through magnesium glycinate, citrate, oxide, malate, taurate, and L-threonate, including what each may be best suited for and where the evidence is weaker.

This is educational content, not personal medical advice. Dr. Kim explicitly says that although he is a physician, he is not the viewer’s physician, and viewers should talk to their doctor before changing their health regimen [1:06].

Detailed Walkthrough

Dr. Kim opens by naming common symptoms that often drive people toward magnesium: unexplained tiredness, brain fog, trouble sleeping, and feeling “off” [0:00]. He acknowledges that online advice often tells people to take magnesium, but says that advice is sometimes correct and sometimes overhyped [0:14]. The problem, in his framing, is that most discussions do not help people decide whether magnesium is actually the answer for them, or which formulation makes sense if they do supplement [0:23].

He then introduces the “magnesium gray zone” [0:39]. The distinction is between severe magnesium deficiency and suboptimal magnesium status. Severe deficiency is the kind that shows up on a blood test and may require urgent medical attention, but he says this situation is not very common in people with a reasonably varied diet and without specific risk factors [1:26]. Risk factors he names include type 2 diabetes, gastrointestinal issues, heavy alcohol use, genetic predisposition, and certain medications, especially diuretics and proton pump inhibitors such as Prilosec, Nexium, and Prevacid [1:49].

The important distinction is that not being severely deficient is not the same thing as being optimal [2:22]. Dr. Kim says many symptoms live in that gap. Magnesium participates in over 300 enzyme reactions, and enzymes are needed for the chemical reactions that keep cellular processes running [2:38]. Because magnesium affects cardiovascular function, the nervous system, and metabolic health, even a mildly suboptimal state can show up in diffuse ways: fatigue, feeling wound up, poor sleep, muscle cramps, or blood pressure running slightly high [3:00]. These symptoms may not point to one clean diagnosis because magnesium’s role is distributed across many systems [3:17].

A major reason this gray zone can be missed is that standard blood magnesium testing reflects only a tiny fraction of total body magnesium. Dr. Kim states that less than 1% of the body’s magnesium is in the blood; more than 99% is inside cells, muscle tissue, and bones [3:57]. Blood magnesium is tightly regulated by the kidneys and bones, so the body can keep blood levels normal even while tissue stores are being depleted [4:06]. He compares skeletal magnesium storage to a savings account: if daily intake does not meet daily demand, the body pulls from bone stores to keep blood levels stable [4:31]. Short-term, that compensation may work, but he says years or decades of drawing from bone magnesium stores may carry long-term costs. He cites research linking chronic suboptimal magnesium levels with lower bone density and higher fracture risk over time [4:47].

The video then shifts to why modern life may push people into this suboptimal zone. Dr. Kim describes a mismatch between declining magnesium intake and rising magnesium demand [5:10]. On the intake side, modern food processing strips magnesium from foods. Processed breads, processed cereals, and ultra-processed foods contain less magnesium than whole-food versions [5:28]. Meanwhile, magnesium-rich foods—nuts, seeds, legumes, leafy greens, and whole grains—are often reduced or missing in a typical standard American diet [5:42].

On the demand or loss side, stress is a major factor. Dr. Kim says stress depletes magnesium, and low magnesium then makes it harder for the nervous system to handle stress, creating a two-way cycle [5:58]. Alcohol can increase magnesium losses [6:18]. Certain medications can interfere with absorption or increase losses, including proton pump inhibitors and diuretics, also called water pills. He names Lasix and hydrochlorothiazide as examples of diuretics [6:26]. He emphasizes that there are additional medications that can strip magnesium, so people concerned about low or suboptimal magnesium should discuss it with their doctor [6:38].

The practical question becomes who should consider supplementation. Dr. Kim first points to people who simply are not getting enough magnesium from their diet, especially if their diet is high in ultra-processed foods and low in leafy greens, legumes, nuts, seeds, and whole grains [7:03]. He says food-first is ideal when possible, but several situations may justify discussing supplementation with a clinician [7:18].

Poor sleep is one such situation, especially trouble winding down at night or waking in the middle of the night with a racing mind [7:25]. Dr. Kim explains that magnesium helps regulate GABA, the brain’s main inhibitory neurotransmitter, which helps tell the nervous system to slow down [7:42]. He also says magnesium helps regulate cortisol, the main stress hormone. If cortisol remains high in the evening, the body can stay in a low-grade fight-or-flight state even without obvious conscious stress [7:51].

Stress itself is another scenario [8:07]. The pattern is cyclical: stress increases magnesium losses through urine, but magnesium is needed to regulate the stress response. More stress can mean more magnesium loss; lower magnesium can then make stress harder to handle [8:16].

Frequent headaches or migraines are another potential use case [8:33]. Dr. Kim says magnesium can block the NMDA receptor, which is involved in pathways that can trigger migraines [8:33]. He references a meta-analysis of 21 randomized controlled trials showing that both IV and oral magnesium can reduce migraine attack severity and lower both frequency and intensity [8:41].

Insulin resistance and type 2 diabetes are also highlighted. Dr. Kim states that low magnesium is 10 times more common in people with type 2 diabetes than in the healthy population [8:58]. Insulin resistance changes how the kidneys handle magnesium, causing more urinary loss [9:12]. Higher blood sugar also pulls more water into urine, carrying minerals such as magnesium out with it [9:20]. This can become another vicious cycle because low magnesium can worsen insulin resistance, not merely result from it [9:37].

High blood pressure is the last major scenario before the supplement-form discussion [9:45]. Low magnesium is associated with impaired blood vessel function and increased arterial constriction, both of which can contribute to higher blood pressure [9:53]. Dr. Kim cites a meta-analysis of 38 randomized controlled trials showing that magnesium supplementation can produce modest but meaningful blood pressure reductions, especially in people already taking blood pressure medications [10:01].

The formulation section begins with the idea that magnesium should be treated as a family of supplements rather than a single supplement [10:18]. Different forms have different absorption profiles and different likelihoods of staying in the digestive tract [10:25]. The right form depends on the goal: sleep, stress, constipation, migraines, muscle symptoms, cardiovascular/metabolic support, or possible cognitive effects.

Magnesium glycinate is presented as a common choice for poor sleep, stress, and feeling wound up [10:49]. It is described as well absorbed and less likely to cause gastrointestinal side effects such as diarrhea [11:02]. In this form, magnesium is bound to glycine, an amino acid that may have calming effects of its own [11:06]. Dr. Kim cautions that strong human data proving glycinate is superior for sleep or stress are limited; much of the evidence comes from animal studies. Still, he says the biological rationale makes sense because it is well absorbed, generally well tolerated, and glycine may be calming [11:14].

For constipation, Dr. Kim shifts to magnesium citrate and magnesium oxide [11:31]. Magnesium citrate is described as fairly well absorbed, able to boost magnesium stores, and useful for constipation at higher doses [11:40]. He notes that magnesium citrate is sometimes used as bowel preparation for colonoscopies [11:48]. Magnesium oxide is poorly absorbed, which is precisely why it can help constipation: because much of it remains in the digestive tract, it pulls water into the intestines and supports bowel movements [11:56]. He states that gastroenterology guidelines include magnesium oxide as a first-line option for chronic constipation [12:05].

Magnesium oxide also appears in migraine discussions. Dr. Kim says several headache guidelines include magnesium oxide as a reasonable option because it may reduce migraine frequency [12:14].

Magnesium malate is discussed as a form with theoretical relevance for muscle-related issues [12:23]. In preclinical studies, it appears well absorbed and may achieve good levels in muscle and brain tissue [12:31]. Dr. Kim says this is the form that could theoretically help with muscle fatigue, cramps, or exercise recovery, and that it is usually well tolerated from a gastrointestinal standpoint [12:38]. However, he stresses that much of this is based on animal studies and that there is not much human data [12:47].

Magnesium taurate is framed around cardiovascular and metabolic possibilities [12:56]. Taurate itself has been studied in blood pressure regulation, insulin sensitivity, and heart stability, while magnesium has overlapping effects in those same areas [13:03]. Combining magnesium and taurine may therefore have theoretical synergy for people with high blood pressure, diabetes, or heart issues [13:11]. Dr. Kim references the WHO-CARDIAC study, which found that higher urinary magnesium and taurine levels correlated with lower cardiovascular risk [13:26]. But he cautions that this is not based on robust human randomized controlled trials; the evidence is mostly animal studies and observational data [13:34].

Finally, he discusses magnesium L-threonate, a form popular in longevity circles [13:43]. Its appeal is that it appears capable of raising magnesium levels inside the brain and crossing the blood-brain barrier [13:51]. However, there are major caveats. First, its elemental magnesium dose is low, usually about 7% to 8%, so it is not a good choice for replenishing magnesium stores when someone is deficient [13:59]. Second, evidence for cognitive benefits is still limited. Dr. Kim mentions a recent small randomized controlled trial in which magnesium L-threonate taken for six weeks improved overall cognition, cognitive age, and working memory [14:16]. But the study used a branded form called Magtein and was funded by the company that manufactures it [14:31]. He says that does not prove the findings are wrong, but it does mean potential bias should be recognized, and he wants to see more peer-reviewed data [14:39].

Key Takeaways

Severe deficiency may show up on blood tests and require urgent treatment, but the video focuses heavily on the “gray zone” where blood levels may look normal while symptoms or tissue depletion still matter [1:26].
  • Standard blood magnesium testing can miss the bigger picture. Dr. Kim states that less than 1% of total body magnesium is in the blood, while more than 99% is stored inside cells, muscle, and bones [3:57].
  • The body can keep blood magnesium normal by drawing from bone stores. This compensation may hide low tissue stores, but chronic reliance on skeletal magnesium may be linked to lower bone density and fracture risk over time [4:23].
  • Modern diets may reduce magnesium intake. Processed breads, cereals, and ultra-processed foods contain less magnesium than whole-food counterparts, while magnesium-rich foods such as leafy greens, legumes, nuts, seeds, and whole grains are often under-consumed [5:28].
  • Stress can create a magnesium-depletion loop. Stress increases magnesium loss through urine, while low magnesium makes it harder to regulate the stress response, potentially reinforcing a cycle of stress sensitivity [6:06], [8:07].
  • Alcohol, proton pump inhibitors, diuretics, diabetes, and gastrointestinal issues can increase risk. Dr. Kim specifically names PPIs such as Prilosec, Nexium, and Prevacid, and diuretics such as Lasix and hydrochlorothiazide [2:08], [6:26].
  • Magnesium may be worth discussing with a clinician for poor sleep, high stress, migraines, insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, or high blood pressure. The video links each of these scenarios to mechanisms or studies suggesting possible benefit [7:25]-[10:18].
  • Magnesium glycinate is presented as a sleep-and-stress-oriented form. It is described as well absorbed, generally well tolerated, and less likely to cause diarrhea, though Dr. Kim says strong human evidence proving superiority for sleep or stress is limited [10:49].
  • Magnesium citrate and magnesium oxide are more relevant when constipation is a goal. Citrate is fairly well absorbed and can help constipation at higher doses; oxide is poorly absorbed, stays in the gut, pulls water into the intestines, and is included in gastroenterology guidelines as a first-line option for chronic constipation [11:31].
  • Magnesium oxide also has migraine relevance. Dr. Kim says headache guidelines include it as a reasonable option for reducing migraine frequency [12:14].
  • Magnesium malate and taurate have plausible but less-proven use cases. Malate is discussed for muscle fatigue, cramps, and exercise recovery; taurate is discussed for cardiovascular, blood pressure, insulin-sensitivity, and heart-stability contexts. In both cases, Dr. Kim emphasizes limited robust human trial evidence [12:23]-[13:43].
  • Magnesium L-threonate may reach the brain, but it is not ideal for repletion. Its elemental magnesium content is low, around 7% to 8%, and cognitive-benefit evidence remains limited, with at least one small trial involving a company-funded branded product, Magtein [13:43].

Practical Notes

Magnesium decisions should start with the goal and the risk profile, not with the generic question “Should I take magnesium?” Dr. Kim’s practical framework is to first consider whether intake is low, whether demand or losses are high, and what symptom or condition is being targeted.

A food-first approach is preferred when possible. The magnesium-rich foods named in the transcript are leafy greens, legumes, nuts, seeds, and whole grains [7:10]. Diets high in ultra-processed foods and low in those foods are presented as a reason to consider whether magnesium intake may be inadequate [7:03].

People with medication-related risk should involve a clinician. The video specifically flags proton pump inhibitors, including Prilosec, Nexium, and Prevacid, and diuretics, including Lasix and hydrochlorothiazide [2:08], [6:30]. Dr. Kim also says many other medications can strip magnesium, so medication review is a decision point [6:38].

For poor sleep or stress, magnesium glycinate is the form Dr. Kim emphasizes. The rationale is absorption, tolerability, minimal gastrointestinal side effects, and the glycine component’s possible calming effect [10:49]. The caveat is that strong human data proving it is superior for sleep or stress are limited [11:14].

For constipation, magnesium citrate or magnesium oxide may be more appropriate. Citrate is fairly well absorbed and can help constipation at higher doses; oxide is poorly absorbed and therefore pulls water into the intestines [11:40]. Dr. Kim notes that magnesium oxide appears in gastroenterology guidelines as a first-line option for chronic constipation [12:05].

For migraines, magnesium oxide is specifically mentioned as a reasonable option in headache guidelines, and magnesium more broadly is supported by a meta-analysis of 21 randomized controlled trials showing reductions in migraine severity, frequency, and intensity with IV and oral magnesium [8:41], [12:14].

For insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, and high blood pressure, magnesium status may deserve attention because these conditions can be involved in feedback loops. In type 2 diabetes, low magnesium is described as much more common, partly due to urinary loss from kidney handling changes and higher blood sugar [8:58]. In high blood pressure, magnesium is linked to blood vessel function and arterial constriction, with trial evidence suggesting modest but meaningful reductions in blood pressure [9:45].

For muscle fatigue, cramps, or exercise recovery, magnesium malate is presented as theoretically relevant, but not strongly proven in human studies [12:23]. This is a “promising rationale, limited data” category rather than a firm recommendation.

For cardiovascular or metabolic support, magnesium taurate is presented as theoretically synergistic because both taurine and magnesium have been studied in overlapping areas such as blood pressure, insulin sensitivity, and heart stability [12:56]. The caveat is that the evidence base discussed is observational and animal-based rather than robust randomized human trials [13:34].

For cognitive or brain-targeted purposes, magnesium L-threonate is the form discussed because it may cross the blood-brain barrier [13:43]. But it is not a good choice if the main purpose is replenishing magnesium stores, because its elemental magnesium content is low, around 7% to 8% [13:59]. Evidence for cognition is not yet strong enough for confident claims, and Dr. Kim flags funding bias in a small trial using Magtein [14:16].
