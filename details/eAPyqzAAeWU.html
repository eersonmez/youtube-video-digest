Anthropic Labs, led by co-founder of Instagram Mike Krieger, discusses building AI-native products using advanced models like Claude.

Alex Kantrowitz · 2026-06-25T17:45:41+00:00 · Anthropic's Labs Lead On Fable's Capabilities + Building AI-Native Products — With Mike Krieger

What This Video Is About

This video features Mike Krieger, co-founder of Instagram and lead of Anthropic Labs, discussing the evolution of Anthropic from a model builder to a massive product builder centered around advanced AI capabilities like Claude Code and Co-work. The central theme is that true understanding of new models requires extensive real-world application beyond initial hype or short-term reactions.

Krieger outlines how Anthropic Labs operates by focusing on two guiding questions: first, visualizing the gap between current model capabilities and typical user usage to close it; and second, anticipating future needs six months out to ensure products are ready for upcoming advancements. He provides concrete examples of how AI is being used to handle complex tasks that were previously difficult or time-consuming for humans, such as large-scale code conversion.

The discussion also touches upon the company's approach to safety, its dual existence as both a platform and a product creator, and the shift in operational philosophy required when moving from an executive role to a builder role while leveraging powerful AI tools.

Detailed Walkthrough

Mike Krieger details Anthropic Labs' function within Anthropic, noting that the organization is focused on building "the next level of frontier products with AI at the center" [0:00]. He emphasizes that they are moving beyond just being a model builder to becoming a product builder with offerings like Claude Code and Co-work.

Krieger shares his personal experience regarding early reactions to new models, stating that he has learned "to not really trust day of or even week of model reactions" because real work is needed to put them through their paces [1:20]. He contrasts this with the initial hype around Fable, noting that sustained use reveals true capability.

A key aspect of Anthropic's internal process involves assessing risk and uplift in domains like biology by comparing a layperson using the model versus an expert or layperson just using the internet, as detailed in their model cards [5:00]. Regarding external scrutiny, he addresses concerns about specific models being singled out, suggesting that the overall trajectory of capability is more significant than any single instance.

The function of Labs has evolved over time. Initially, when Krieger joined, the goal was to ensure products didn't fall behind the rapidly improving models [14:00]. The two guiding thought exercises for Labs are: 1) visualizing and closing the gap between current model capabilities and common user usage; and 2) imagining what models will be bad at in six months and preparing a product for that future state [15:00].

He describes the shift from using models as an executive—where you delegate chunks of work like "please fix this bug"—to using them in a builder role, where delegation is more comprehensive. For instance, he recounts delegating a full language conversion of hundreds of thousands of lines of code from Python to TypeScript [9:30]. This task involved the AI planning, executing, verifying, and double-verifying the work, completing it in an hour—significantly faster and more accurate than traditional methods.

Krieger highlights that advanced models exhibit a "theory of project," meaning they consider disparate parts of a system and anticipate future changes, unlike earlier iterations which might just make isolated changes [10:45]. He also mentions the development of Claude Code Artifacts, allowing the model to generate illustrations or drawings rather than just text responses, reflecting a shift in what users expect from intelligent systems [16:30].

Looking ahead, he outlines two major themes for future development. The first is giving Claude "more agency and self-knowledge" within its operational environment. Currently, if you create a file with Claude, it might instruct you to manually download and drag it into the project; the goal is for it to integrate directly [25:00]. This includes allowing secure access to a customer's source code while running in an agent loop so that when issues arise, the model can reference maintainers or existing code context. The second theme is closing the gap between how people understand their work and the actual day-to-day execution of it. He cites an example where automating eight manual steps of moving a ticket through a task tracker made a 30-year veteran feel like they were experiencing something new [28:15].

Finally, he discusses the industry's economic metrics, noting that there is no strong correlation between token usage and actual productivity among users. He suggests looking at model intelligence combined with effort and token efficiency as key levers for improvement [30:45]. For future product direction, he points to Claude managed agents, which offer both a standard task-based mode and an outcome-based mode where the user defines what "good looks like" via a rubric [32:15].

Key Takeaways

  • Anthropic Labs functions as a dedicated unit within Anthropic focused on building frontier products that showcase advanced AI capabilities.
  • The core philosophy of Labs is to bridge the gap between current model potential and how typical users actually apply them, while also anticipating future user needs six months out.
  • Advanced models can handle complex, multi-stage tasks—such as converting hundreds of thousands of lines of code from one language (Python) to another (TypeScript)—with increased speed and accuracy compared to traditional methods.
  • A significant shift in AI interaction is moving from using models as an executive for simple delegation to leveraging them as a builder capable of complex, end-to-end project execution with greater "theory of project."
  • Future development heavily emphasizes giving Claude more agency and self-knowledge by allowing it deeper integration into its operational environment (e.g., secure access to source code).
  • Anthropic is exploring outcome-based pricing models for agents, where success is measured against a defined rubric rather than just token consumption.
  • The company views itself as both a platform provider and a product builder, aiming to push industry boundaries in areas like agentic coding.

Practical Notes

  • Labs Guiding Questions: 1) Visualize the gap between current model capabilities and typical user usage; 2) Imagine what models will be bad at now that they'll be good at in six months and build a product for it.
  • Product Examples: Claude Code, Co-work, Claude Code Artifacts (enabling drawing/illustrations).
  • Workflow Improvement Example: Automating the process of moving a ticket through eight manual steps across different queues using Labs projects to streamline workflows for non-technical users.
  • Agent Capabilities: Anthropic is developing "Claude managed agents" with two modes: standard task execution and an outcome-based mode defined by a user-provided rubric.
  • Risk Mitigation: The company prioritizes front-loading ethical considerations and impact analysis, which has become more normalized within the organization compared to previous social media companies.
