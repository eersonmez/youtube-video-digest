The relationship between human energy, patience, and dog training principles.

Andrew Huberman · 2026-07-06T12:00:30+00:00 · Raising a Dog & Mastering Calm Assertive Energy | Cesar Millan

What This Video Is About

This video features Caesar Millan discussing the profound relationship between human internal states—such as patience, calmness, confidence, love, and joy—and how these states influence animal behavior, specifically dogs. Millan argues that a dog reacts directly to the energy sent by its owner; therefore, effective training is less about commanding obedience and more about cultivating a positive, stable state of mind in oneself. This approach applies not only to dog ownership but also offers insights into human psychology and improving interactions with other people across all relationships.

The central theme is that dogs are pack animals whose instincts must be respected rather than overridden by human expectations. By focusing on internal mastery—achieving silence (patience), calmness (trust), confidence (knowledge), love, and joy—one can create an environment where the dog feels safe and healthy, allowing them to operate from a state of "happy golucky" instead of fight-or-flight avoidance.

Detailed Walkthrough

Millan begins by establishing that when a dog misbehaves, the goal is for the owner to guide the dog back to a good state of mind, such as patience or happiness, rather than inducing fight-or-flight responses [0:00]. He introduces five key internal states: silence (which grants patience), calmness (which builds trust through breathing), confidence (derived from knowledge), love (given at the correct time), and joy (the celebration after effort) [5:00]. These elements form a formula for creating a strong connection with a dog, connecting spirit, instinct, heart, and mind.

He emphasizes that what is trained is the mind, but what is connected is the spirit and instinct [5:00]. Millan then discusses the concept of energy exchange, noting that animals are highly sensitive to human emotional states; they sense shifts in energy before humans do [1:00:00]. He shares his personal experience walking large packs of dogs off-leash, which he attributes to transmitting this specific energy—silence, calmness, and confidence—through the leash, reframing the leash as a symbol of trust and respect in the animal world [15:00].

The discussion shifts to dog positioning during walks. To eliminate 90% of behavioral problems, one must understand that dogs naturally learn to follow their parents through play and exploration [20:00]. A dog at the front of the pack is not stressed; conversely, a dog in the middle or back can become fearful if handled improperly [20:00]. For a dog to be in the front of the pack, the owner needs significant knowledge.

Millan outlines three critical rituals for positive interaction with dogs: 1) The meeting (where no touch, no talking, and no eye contact are used initially to establish safety, peace, nose eyes ears, trust, and respect), 2) Walking the dog next to you while following, and 3) Feeding [30:00]. He stresses that initial greetings must be devoid of excitement; instead, they should reinforce the owner as a source of safety.

For dogs exhibiting anxiety or running around, Millan suggests assessing physical needs first, noting that insufficient exercise (e.g., only walking for 20-30 minutes when the dog has been confined for 20+ hours) can border on abuse [35:00]. He recommends structured walks of about two hours a day to support physical and mental health, challenging the notion that this is too much for a puppy [40:00].

Regarding discipline, Millan advocates for clear boundaries. For example, if a dog attempts to eat food before being allowed, he uses a simple "yelp" rather than arguing, which teaches the dog instantly without creating conflict [40:00]. He notes that destructive behaviors like excessive barking or house destruction are often manifestations of unreleased physical and mental energy due to lack of proper structure [2:25:00].

Finally, he extends these principles to human relationships. True love is rooted in safety and security first; one must honor who the other person *is*, not what they project onto others [1:15:00]. He concludes that by mastering self-discipline—cultivating patience, calmness, confidence, etc.—one can create a state of being that fosters positive interactions with dogs, family, and partners alike.

Key Takeaways

  • The core principle in dog training is managing the owner's internal energy rather than solely correcting the dog's behavior [0:00].
  • A dog reacts directly to the energy sent by its owner; good states include patience, calmness, confidence, love, and joy [5:00].
  • The initial interaction with a dog must be ritualized (no touch, no talking, no eye contact) to establish trust and safety before any other training can occur [30:00].
  • A structured walk of approximately two hours daily is recommended for dogs to support both physical health and mental stimulation [40:00].
  • Destructive behaviors like excessive barking or house destruction are often signs that the dog's physical or mental energy needs are not being met through proper structure [2:25:00].
  • In relationships, true love is predicated on providing safety and security first; one must respect who another person fundamentally is [1:15:00].
  • To be an effective leader (whether with dogs or in life), one must first master the qualities of a good follower, specifically calm surrender and patience [2:20:00].
  • The ultimate goal for humans is to exude positive energy—silence, calmness, confidence, love, joy—which benefits all interactions [2:00:00].

Practical Notes

  • Initial Greeting Protocol: When meeting a dog, maintain no touch, no talking, and no eye contact. This reinforces the owner as a source of safety, peace, trust, respect, nose eyes ears [30:00].
  • Energy Management: To lead effectively with animals, one must bring perfect energy—patient, calm, confident, loving—as you cannot lie to an animal about your state [2:15:00].
  • Discipline Technique Example: When addressing unwanted behavior (e.g., eating before permission), use a minimal correction like a "yelp" rather than engaging in prolonged conflict [40:00].
  • Exercise Prescription: For dogs, aim for structured walks that challenge both the mind and body, moving beyond short, casual stroll durations [40:00].
  • Commitment to Self: Before attempting to lead or influence others (dogs or people), one must work on mastering their own internal state—their spirit, instinct, and mind [2:10:00].
