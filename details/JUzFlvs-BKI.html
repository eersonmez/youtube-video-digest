Dr. Chinasa T. Okolo discusses AI governance, equity, and the challenges facing the global majority.

Scott Hanselman · 2026-07-16T16:32:56+00:00 · Who is left behind when AI moves fast? with Dr. Chinasa T. Okolo

What This Video Is About

This video features a discussion with Dr. Chinasa T. Okolo, an internationally recognized researcher and policy advisor on AI governance and safety for the global majority. The central theme revolves around addressing the societal anxieties surrounding Artificial Intelligence while providing a nuanced perspective that moves beyond simplistic narratives of AI taking over or being a universal panacea. Dr. Okolo emphasizes that humans remain in control of AI development, but stresses that its deployment requires careful consideration of foundational issues like infrastructure and equitable access before technological solutions can be expected to solve deep-seated problems in the global majority.

The discussion critically examines how AI is currently perceived—often reduced to large language models like chatbots by the general public and policymakers—while highlighting more sophisticated machine learning systems already used in government services. A major concern raised is the limited understanding of these complex systems among decision-makers, leading to risks such as community healthcare workers deferring to flawed AI decisions without critical evaluation. Dr. Okolo advocates for a holistic approach that integrates technical expertise with social sciences, focusing on equity and autonomy rather than just technological advancement.

Detailed Walkthrough

The conversation begins by addressing public anxiety regarding AI, where people fear it will replace them or take over the world [1:08]. Dr. Okolo asserts that humans are in control of how AI is developed, shaped, and deployed, stressing the need for responsible implementation [2:10]. She notes a common misconception that "AI" only refers to large language models (LLMs) like ChatGPT; however, governmental entities have utilized predictive models and basic machine learning systems for decades, particularly in delivering government services [3:22].

A key point raised is the need for explainable AI. Dr. Okolo's PhD work focused on this concept, which involves explaining a system to its user. She raises a critical question: should an AI have been imposed on the user at all? In her field research with community healthcare workers in India [8:06], she observed that when these workers were pressured to adopt AI tools without understanding them, they risked deferring to false decisions made by the system rather than leveraging their own domain expertise [8:38]. Explainability helps maintain autonomy by allowing users to understand how a decision was reached [9:10].

The discussion then shifts to the risks associated with relying heavily on LLMs. Dr. Okolo warns that outsourcing fundamental tasks, such as searching and reading literature for research, can cause researchers to lose critical thinking skills because they rely solely on what the chatbot provides [11:51]. Furthermore, she points out issues with AI-generated content, citing a friend who developed "Ref Checker" to detect fabricated references and citation errors, noting that fabrication is increasing [13:57].

Regarding development in low-resource settings, Dr. Okolo cautions against "technosolutionist theater"—the act of deploying technology without addressing underlying issues like infrastructure or funding [23:28]. She notes that many deployed solutions are abandoned because there is no sufficient technology transfer or resource allocation to support long-term use [24:39]. For AI solutions in low-resource areas, she suggests considering cloud support and having personnel available for debugging and adaptation [25:10].

The concept of "sovereign AI" involves autonomy over local data, models, and infrastructure. While some efforts exist globally (e.g., Germany's cloud or South African initiatives), Dr. Okolo argues that true sovereignty is complex because the entire value chain—from chips to models—is still heavily reliant on major global players like Chinese or American companies [28:43]. She suggests that achieving sovereignty requires developing alliances based on shared values and focusing on local capacity building rather than just adopting foreign systems.

Finally, when addressing big tech companies claiming support for responsible AI in the global majority, Dr. Okolo demands true independence in governance framework development. She cites her work with Nigeria's national strategy as an example where funding from major tech firms influenced the process, advocating for neutral external stakeholders to prevent undue influence [31:08].

Key Takeaways

  • Humans retain control over AI development, but its deployment must be responsible and cautious [2:17].
  • The public often equates "AI" solely with large language models (LLMs), overlooking decades of use of basic machine learning in government services [3:01].
  • Explainable AI is vital to prevent users from blindly deferring to flawed automated decisions, especially in critical fields like healthcare [8:55].
  • Over-reliance on LLMs can diminish the critical thinking and research skills of academics by outsourcing fundamental cognitive tasks [12:07].
  • "Technosolutionist theater" occurs when technology is deployed without addressing foundational problems like connectivity, electricity, or funding in developing regions [23:28].
  • True AI sovereignty requires more than just local compute; it demands autonomy over data curation, model development, and equitable compensation for labor [27:49].
  • For successful long-term deployment of tech solutions in low-resource areas, infrastructure support (like cloud services) and community engagement are necessary [25:01].
  • Policy frameworks must maintain independence from the influence of major technology companies to ensure genuine governance [31:16].

Practical Notes

  • AI Types: Differentiate between Large Language Models (LLMs), predictive models, machine learning, and deep learning. LLMs are often the public face of AI interaction [2:34].
  • Explainable AI Goal: The goal is not just to explain *how* a system works, but also to question *if* it should be imposed on the user in the first place [7:14].
  • Low-Resource Deployment Requirements: For long-term viability of tech solutions, consider setting up cloud support and having personnel available for debugging and adaptation [25:01].
  • Sovereignty Components: True sovereignty involves local compute, local models (e.g., in local languages), data curation control, and the ability to independently develop AI clusters [27:49].
  • Policy Demand: When engaging with big tech on responsible AI, demand true independence in governance framework development and advocate for neutral external advisors [31:08].
