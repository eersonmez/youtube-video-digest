The recent regulatory actions against Anthropic's Fable and Mythos models highlight the complex cyber risks associated with advanced AI.

Alex Kantrowitz · 2026-06-28T15:05:21+00:00 · AI’s True Cyber Risk: Fable, Mythos, China, and The Rest — With Alex Stamos

What This Video Is About

This video discusses the recent regulatory actions taken against Anthropic's frontier models, specifically Fable and Mythos, which led to their removal from public and private access. The core of the discussion revolves around whether these advanced AI capabilities represent a genuine step-change in artificial intelligence or if they are largely coordinated marketing efforts. A significant portion of the conversation focuses on the cybersecurity implications: while some view these models as hype, others argue that their ability to find software vulnerabilities is crucial for defense against cyber threats.

The central conflict pits government intervention and export controls against the practical needs of security professionals who rely on such advanced tools to discover and fix flaws in complex software. The speakers debate whether restricting access to powerful bug-finding AI models inadvertently harms cybersecurity defenses by limiting the ability to address existing vulnerabilities, especially given the proliferation of insecure codebases written in memory-unsafe languages like C and C++.

Detailed Walkthrough

The discussion begins with the event where the White House became aware of security testing conducted by Amazon within AWS Bedrock on Anthropic's models [0:16]. This testing reportedly raised concerns regarding Fable's protections compared to Mythos. After disagreements between Anthropic and Amazon over the seriousness of these findings, Andy Jassy mentioned this to the White House, leading to an order from the Commerce Department designating both Fable and Mythos as export controlled [1:09-1:36]. This resulted in Anthropic pulling down both models, despite their stated willingness to fix the issues collaboratively [2:15-2:28].

The conversation then shifts to debating whether these powerful models constitute a real capability increase or marketing hype. One perspective suggests that Mythos is arguably the best bug-finding model available for known security evaluations [4:11], but questions if it represents a true breakthrough compared to previous advancements with Opus 4 and GPT-5 series, which allowed models to match top human bug finders [5:01-5:28].

A key distinction is made between Fable and Mythos. Fable uses the same model weights as Mythos but has specific protections implemented by Anthropic to prevent it from performing "really nasty stuff" that Mythos can do [6:32-6:39]. However, speakers argue that even with these restrictions, jailbreaks are possible, meaning users could still trick Fable into certain actions, though not the most powerful exploits achievable with Mythos (like grinding on a Linux kernel for extended periods) [7:05-7:31].

The discussion highlights that bugs themselves are cheap; the real danger lies in the sheer volume of bad software and how these models can find vulnerabilities at scale [7:49-8:01]. The analogy used is comparing Mythos to an F-150 or a McLaren, suggesting that while one model might be restricted (the McLaren), restricting it harms defenders who need full coverage to find all bugs [8:38-8:50].

Furthermore, the speakers address the geopolitical aspect. While Fable was pulled down by US regulatory action, open-weight Chinese models like GLM 5.2 have been shipped and are competitive in coding tasks, suggesting that relying solely on US labs for defense is a flawed strategy [13:07-13:43]. The conclusion drawn is that the industry needs to accelerate its own capabilities rather than playing purely defensive games against adversaries.

Key Takeaways

  • Government pressure led to Anthropic pulling Fable and Mythos due to security concerns raised during testing by Amazon in AWS Bedrock [0:16-1:36].
  • Mythos is considered one of the best bug-finding models known for open evaluations, though its status as a true breakthrough is debated against previous model generations like Opus 4 and GPT-5 [4:11-5:28].
  • Fable shares weights with Mythos but has Anthropic-imposed protections to limit dangerous outputs; however, these protections are not absolute against jailbreaking [6:32-7:02].
  • The primary cybersecurity risk identified is the massive volume of insecure software that superhuman bug finders can uncover at scale [7:49-8:01].
  • Restricting access to powerful AI tools like Mythos risks harming defenders who need them for comprehensive vulnerability discovery and patching [8:38-8:50].
  • Open-weight models from Chinese labs, such as GLM 5.2, are demonstrating competitive capabilities in coding tasks, challenging the narrative that US defense is solely reliant on closed American labs [13:07-13:43].
  • A critical concern raised is that if regulatory definitions of "jailbreak" become too broad (e.g., including any vulnerability finding), it could force reliance on foreign models for security work [17:18-17:26].
  • The industry needs to shift focus from merely *finding* bugs to developing robust methods for *creating and fixing exploits* and other offensive operations, which should be the true off-limits areas [11:30-11:29].

Practical Notes

  • Model Status: Fable and Mythos were pulled due to export control orders from the Commerce Department following security testing concerns raised by Amazon in AWS Bedrock [1:54-2:28].
  • Alternative Tools: For immediate use, speakers suggest using Opus for AI tasks as a viable alternative while waiting for regulatory clarity on Fable's return [15:50-16:03].
  • Security Research Principle: It is noted that it is theoretically impossible to create a model that is completely immune to jailbreaking, referencing a paper similar to Gödel's completeness theorem [16:27-16:34].
  • Mitigation Strategy (Defense): Companies are encouraged to use AI tools for security scanning; if modern AI security tools were applied to existing codebases, they could find up to 80% of current bugs that companies might otherwise miss [15:20-15:43].
