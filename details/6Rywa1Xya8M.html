Leonid Kim lays out an individualized blood-pressure target framework for older adults.

Leonid Kim MD · 2026-06-15T22:00:11+00:00 · What's the Best Blood Pressure for Older Adults?

What This Video Is About

This video addresses the significant disagreement among medical bodies regarding optimal blood pressure targets for older adults, emphasizing that a single universal number is insufficient due to individual physiological differences. The central argument presented by Dr. Leonid Kim is that blood pressure management must be highly individualized, weighing potential cardiovascular benefits against risks associated with overly aggressive lowering in vulnerable populations.

The discussion centers on the evidence from major studies like the SPRINT trial, which showed benefits of lower targets for certain resilient individuals but also highlighted limitations regarding study inclusion criteria and measurement methodologies. Dr. Kim outlines a comprehensive framework that moves beyond just systolic pressure to incorporate functional status, frailty, medication burden, and specific symptoms to determine appropriate starting points for blood pressure goals in older adults.

Ultimately, the video serves as an educational guide to help viewers understand the nuances of hypertension management in aging, stressing that while aggressive lowering can be beneficial for some healthy seniors, it carries risks like falls, kidney injury, and cognitive slowing in others. The key message is to initiate a detailed conversation with a physician using this framework rather than self-adjusting medication based on general guidelines.

Detailed Walkthrough

The discussion begins by noting the divergence between recent American Heart Association/American College of Cardiology guidelines (suggestive targets around 130 mmHg or lower) and more conservative recommendations from primary care bodies (suggestive ranges of 135–150 mmHg) [0:54]. This discrepancy is attributed to the complexity of the underlying evidence, specifically referencing the SPRINT trial.

The SPRINT trial involved over 9,000 adults in their late 60s, comparing a group treated to a systolic target under 120 (intensive treatment) against one treated to under 140 (standard treatment). The intensive group showed fewer heart attacks, strokes, cardiovascular deaths, and a 27% reduction in all-cause mortality [2:19]. However, the analysis quickly pivots to limitations of this study. SPRINT excluded individuals with diabetes, prior strokes, those in nursing homes, or anyone with significant frailty, meaning it only tested aggressive lowering in resilient populations [3:15–3:36]. A follow-up Accord trial examining intensive lowering in diabetic patients did not show the same clear benefit, questioning the broad applicability of SPRINT's findings [3:44–3:57].

A second major complication noted is the measurement technique. SPRINT utilized unattended automated office blood pressure readings, which were found to read approximately 7 to 10 mmHg lower than typical clinic measurements. Consequently, AHA/ACC later adjusted their interpretation of the <120 target to a clinical target closer to 130 to account for this discrepancy [4:18–4:54]. Furthermore, concerns were raised that SPRINT was stopped early due to strong benefits, which could potentially exaggerate treatment effects in meta-analyses [4:56–5:17].

The fundamental tension between medical groups is framed as a difference in weighting: primary care groups weigh the harms of intensive treatments more heavily in older adults, while cardiology societies prioritize cardiovascular benefits from lower targets [5:35–5:49]. Therefore, the focus shifts to individual patient targeting. Aggressively lowering blood pressure carries risks such as falls leading to hip fractures (with a 1-year mortality rate up to 25%), kidney injury, syncope, drug interactions, and cognitive slowing due to reduced cerebral blood flow [6:07–7:03].

Dr. Kim's clinical framework involves assessing several factors before setting targets. First is functional status (e.g., ability to walk a quarter mile or climb stairs), as fitter individuals can tolerate more aggressive lowering [8:21–8:42]. Second is the overall medication burden, considering the risk of adding another drug [8:43–8:50]. Third, kidney function must be tracked over time (past year, 6 months, present) [8:52–9:07]. Crucially, the diastolic number requires close monitoring; if it drops too low (below 70 or 60), the heart's own coronary arteries may not receive sufficient blood supply during diastole [9:13–9:45]. The relationship between systolic and diastolic pressure is also related to pulse pressure (the gap between top and bottom numbers); a wide pulse pressure can indicate stiff vessels, increasing cardiovascular risk [9:46–10:08].

Based on this framework, Dr. Kim provides starting points for discussion:

  • For healthy/active adults aged 65-80 with no red flags, the initial target discussed is systolic BP of around 130 mmHg, potentially moving to 120 or slightly below if tolerated [10:37–10:48].
  • For frailer adults in that age range (with mobility issues, dizziness upon standing, kidney disease, or multiple medications), the initial target discussed is closer to 140/90 mmHg [10:50–11:06].
  • For those over 80 years old, the approach must be very cautious, with a typical starting range between 135-145 systolic, adjusted based on symptoms and tolerance [11:09–11:22].

Key Takeaways

  • Blood pressure targets for older adults are highly personal and cannot be dictated by a single universal number due to individual physiological variability.
  • The SPRINT trial supported lower targets (<120 systolic) in selected, resilient older adults but its findings do not apply universally.
  • Measurement methods matter; automated office readings can read 7-10 mmHg lower than typical clinic measurements, leading some guidelines to adjust their clinical interpretation of aggressive targets.
  • Aggressive blood pressure lowering carries risks beyond cardiovascular events, including falls (leading to hip fractures), kidney injury, syncope, and cognitive slowing.
  • A comprehensive assessment must include functional status (activity level/mobility), medication burden, and the trend of kidney function over time.

Practical Notes

  • Consultation: Always discuss any changes to your health regimen or medication with a doctor; this content is educational, not medical advice [0:42–0:47].
  • Assessment Factors: When discussing targets with a physician, be prepared to discuss functional status (e.g., ability to walk a quarter mile), total medication count, and recent kidney function trends.
  • Diastolic Threshold Warning: Be aware that diastolic pressures dropping too low (below 70 or 60) can potentially increase the risk of coronary heart disease by reducing blood flow to the heart muscle during diastole [9:13–9:45].
  • Medication Safety: Do not stop taking prescribed blood pressure medications on your own; consult your medical team first [11:48–11:52].
