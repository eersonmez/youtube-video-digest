Andrew Huberman interviews Dr. Alan Castel about memory, learning, aging, curiosity, and cognitive function across life.

Andrew Huberman · 2026-07-13T12:00:32+00:00 · How to Improve Your Memory & Cognitive Function at Any Age | Dr. Alan Castel

What This Video Is About

Andrew Huberman interviews Dr. Alan Castel, a psychology professor at UCLA who studies human memory and cognitive aging. The discussion focuses on how people remember names, events, skills, safety-relevant information, and life experiences, and why memory is often less like a recording device than people assume. A recurring theme is that effective learning usually requires active effort: making mistakes, noticing what matters, testing oneself, changing context, and retrieving information rather than merely seeing or repeating it.

The conversation also challenges a simple “aging equals decline” model. Castel argues that aging brings wide variability: some older adults experience serious cognitive impairment, while others remain highly functional into their 80s, 90s, and beyond. He emphasizes that older adults may become better at remembering what matters to them, regulating attention, filtering irrelevant information, and drawing on experience or wisdom. At the same time, aging can increase vulnerability to falls, scams, social isolation, and certain forms of memory failure.

The practical importance is broad: memory affects everyday safety, relationships, health behaviors, learning, planning, and identity. The episode connects laboratory ideas such as retrieval practice, prospective memory, curiosity, eyewitness distortion, and metacognition to common experiences: forgetting names, failing to notice emergency exits, repeating stories, misremembering arguments, leaving a child in a car, balancing on one leg, maintaining relationships, and choosing goals across the lifespan.

Detailed Walkthrough

The episode opens with Castel’s argument that “good learning happens through making mistakes” [0:00]. Simply seeing something repeatedly is not enough to remember it accurately. He uses the Apple logo as an example: people have seen it countless times but often cannot draw it correctly or answer basic questions such as whether the bite is on the left or right and whether there is a stem or leaf. The learning benefit comes when a person first tries to draw it from memory, struggles, makes errors, and then looks again. That “errorful” attempt makes the later exposure more effective because the person now knows what to attend to.

Huberman frames Castel as an expert on human memory and cognitive aging and says the episode will cover why people remember names, events, and procedures; how memory can be improved at any age; and how imagining the future depends on memories of the past [0:00]. This establishes one of the deeper claims of the conversation: memory is not just about preserving old information, but also about planning, creativity, future-oriented action, and life direction.

Early in the discussion, Huberman asks about mnemonic strategies for remembering batches of information, such as pairing people’s names with fruits or other cues [5:00]. Castel calls this partly a workaround. Names are difficult because they are often arbitrary. Historically, some names were descriptive, such as “Baker,” but modern names often do not carry useful meaning. Castel argues that forgetting a name can be natural and may be less important than remembering whether a conversation was meaningful or whether a person seemed trustworthy. Still, names sometimes need to be remembered, and people may “trick” memory by building associations. He uses his own name: Castel can be linked to “pastel,” perhaps with a story about his grandfather using pastels. The tradeoff is that these techniques require effort, take attention away from the actual interaction, and can create predictable errors: someone may remember “Pastel” rather than “Castel” [5:00].

The conversation then moves into the distinction between seeing and noticing. Castel gives the example of emergency exits: people may walk past an exit many times but not know where it is when they need it [10:00]. In studies, rather than simply telling people where the exit is, researchers have people get up and find it. That active engagement reveals that the information had been visible all along but not consciously noticed. Huberman connects this to vision: much information reaches the retina, but only some reaches conscious awareness. Castel uses this to show that memory depends heavily on attention and goal-directed noticing, not just exposure.

A major learning theme emerges around discomfort, frustration, and beginner’s mind [15:00]. Huberman asks whether people who are good learners experience frustration differently, perhaps interpreting autonomic arousal not as “I’m bad at this” but as “this is the opportunity to learn.” Castel agrees that mindset and goals can matter. He connects this to curiosity: learning often involves a knowledge gap, a state where a person knows where they are and senses where they want to be. Curiosity motivates the effort to close that gap. This is different from studying only because something will be on a test; throughout life, learning often occurs through observation, conversation, hobbies, and self-directed interest in areas such as bird watching, music, or skateboarding [15:00].

Context and perspective are then discussed as tools for memory. Castel notes that changing one’s physical and mental perspective can make memories more distinctive [20:00]. If he teaches a class in a different classroom each year, he can mentally revisit those rooms to recall what he learned or taught. If everything is learned in the same spot, interference can occur. Huberman gives an example from scientific meetings: early in his career, he changed seats after breaks to stay awake and learn differently. Later, he gave himself permission to leave unhelpful talks and take walks. Castel interprets this as metacognitive insight: recognizing how one learns and then using that knowledge to adjust behavior [20:00].

Huberman then raises the issue of intentionally “taking a picture” with the mind during ordinary moments [25:00]. He describes deliberately selecting certain non-dramatic visual scenes, such as a street scene with scaffolding and a person walking by, and later still remembering them. He asks whether he remembers the visual content itself or the decision to remember it. Castel responds that selection is crucial: making a conscious decision that “this is a special moment” changes encoding. But he warns that even strong visual memory is not truly photographic. Research on photographic memory shows that people with exceptional visual memory still make errors [25:00].

This leads into memory distortion and eyewitness testimony. Huberman asks whether people remember events as they happened or confabulate them, using everyday disagreement as a context [30:00]. Castel says people do not simply “muck it all up,” nor do they usually distort intentionally. Rather, memory is constantly interpreted through existing knowledge and current state. Eyewitness memory is a powerful example. A person may feel they remember a traumatic event vividly — shirt, smell, weather — but research shows that such memories can be inaccurate. In some cases, DNA evidence later shows that an eyewitness identified the wrong person. Castel explains a mechanism: once a witness identifies someone in a lineup, that identified person can replace the original memory. Memory, like physical evidence, can be contaminated; later recollection may replay the chosen suspect rather than the person actually seen [30:00].

Huberman next introduces what he calls “medium-term memory,” while acknowledging that the standard terms are short-term memory, working memory, and long-term memory [35:00]. He gives a concrete safety example: when checking into hotels, he studies the fire escape map, identifies exits, sometimes walks the hallway, and mentally rehearses how to leave if there is smoke. This habit came from a prior hotel fire alarm that turned out to be a real fire and revealed confusion about exits. Castel treats this as important everyday memory. While people often complain about names, some memories have much higher stakes: what medication one is taking, how one reacted to a person previously, or how to leave a building safely [40:00].

The episode then discusses routines and prospective memory — remembering to do something in the future. Castel and Huberman address tragic cases where a parent accidentally leaves an infant in a car [45:00]. Castel emphasizes that this is not limited to obviously careless or distracted people. A routine can become lethal when a person drives to work on autopilot, while on one unusual day they were supposed to drop the child at daycare. Because the familiar routine dominates, the future intention fails. Some cars now provide reminders to check the back seat. Castel frames this as prospective memory: much of memory is not just recalling the past but remembering future obligations. Phones and reminders are common ways of offloading this, but people remain vulnerable, especially under habit, distraction, or arousal [45:00].

Aging enters more centrally around the question of repetition, dementia, and whether memory loss is inevitable [50:00]. Castel notes that some repeated storytelling can be related to nostalgia and familiarity, but pathological aging may involve severe short-term repetition, such as asking “What’s for dinner?” every five minutes. Huberman asks whether Alzheimer’s is simply an accelerated version of inevitable memory decline or whether some people remain sharp into their 90s and past 100. Castel emphasizes diversity in aging: a 100-year-old may function very well while a 60-year-old may not. Biological age alone is insufficient. He cites a study of nuns in which some had the brain plaques and tangles associated with dementia after death but had remained behaviorally high functioning [50:00]. This supports the idea that brain pathology and outward cognitive function do not always map perfectly.

The discussion then shifts to physical aging, balance, and brain health. Castel describes balance as an underappreciated issue [55:00]. People often think their balance is fine because they have not fallen, but a simple test — standing on one leg for at least 10 seconds — can reveal vulnerability. Closing the eyes makes the task much harder because visual cues support balance. Castel notes that older adults may struggle more, though even younger people begin tipping when visual input is removed. Balance involves the cerebellum, visual information, and the inner ear, and it is trainable through simple practice, yoga, or tai chi [55:00].

Exercise is presented as important not just for the body but for brain function and aging [1:00:00]. Castel and Huberman discuss how movement may improve oxygen delivery to the brain and how walking itself can be viewed as repeatedly losing and regaining balance. Hiking adds unpredictable surfaces and recruits muscles that may not be used in ordinary movement. Castel also challenges age-based fatalism: if one knee hurts, saying “you’re just getting older” is incomplete because the other knee is the same age and may feel fine. There may be trauma, weakness, or something modifiable. He favors physical therapy and varied movement as ways to maintain function [1:00:00].

A broader psychological view of aging follows. Castel notes that the title of his book, “Better with Age,” reflects the idea that although aches and pains increase, some older adults who remain active and healthy do very well [1:05:00]. He and Huberman contrast this with youth and early adulthood, which are often romanticized but can be confusing and stressful. Castel says he feels more content at 50 than at 40 and that his internal landscape was not more peaceful at 20 or 30 despite greater physical resilience [1:05:00]. This reframes aging as a mixed process: physical capacity may decline in some ways, but emotional regulation, contentment, priorities, and perspective may improve.

Castel then discusses his interview with legendary UCLA basketball coach John Wooden [1:10:00]. When asked about success and teamwork, Wooden said the most important thing was love: being around the people you love and doing the things you love. He had lost his wife years earlier, would not sleep on her side of the bed, and wrote her a letter every month. His walls included grandchildren’s report cards next to letters from presidents, showing what he valued. The second important word was balance, both psychological and physical. Wooden had a fall while wearing a life alert device but did not press it, apparently because he did not want to bother anyone. Castel uses this to show that technology alone is insufficient; psychological factors such as pride can prevent people from using available safety tools [1:10:00]. Later, Castel clarifies that the fall did not directly cause Wooden’s death; Wooden recovered and lived several more years, nearly to 99 [1:45:00].

The conversation turns to longevity messaging and whether it is helpful or burdensome [1:15:00]. Huberman says he does not primarily identify as a longevity person and is more interested in a vibrant life than merely living longer. He asks whether messaging about extending health and function is net positive or whether it pressures people not to accept the reality of aging. Castel answers that people are generally living longer, but the key is whether added years are healthy years. The longer someone lives, the greater the chance of developing dementia. From a psychological perspective, subjective age — how old a person feels — may differ from biological age [1:15:00].

Planning and goals become the next major theme. Huberman says academics often operate on three- to five-year plans because grants commonly run on that horizon [1:20:00]. Castel says plans can motivate, but they need not be rigid. When he moved to UCLA from Toronto, he thought he would see in three to five years whether it worked out. He also had broader aspirations, such as writing an accessible book that captured lessons from his work, without necessarily setting a strict timeline [1:20:00]. Huberman connects goal pursuit to biological activation, referencing anterior cingulate activity and the idea that striving toward a future may activate a “will to live” at a cellular or neural level [1:25:00]. The transcript presents this as conjectural discussion rather than established prescription.

Perspective across age is illustrated through a clinical anecdote about young people experiencing suicidal crisis after breakups [1:30:00]. Huberman recounts a clinician’s realization that telling such patients “you have your whole life ahead of you” was not effective because, to them, the breakup felt like the loss of the entire future. They had not yet lived enough future to know otherwise. Castel and Huberman use this to show how age and experience change the way losses, goals, and possibilities are understood. Older adults may have more lived examples of recovery and change, which can alter how they interpret adversity.

Intergenerational learning appears in Castel’s description of older adults participating in his classes [1:35:00]. In one class on aging, four older adults joined the classroom, several of them therapists. Castel found it strange that they were instructed not to ask questions, because he wanted their participation. He tells students that although there are two tests, the “final exam is the test of life”: what they learn about aging may matter later for themselves or their parents. Students often report learning the most from the older adults rather than from the professor. Castel argues that more intergenerational interaction is valuable, and that some cultures build it into community life more than Western culture does [1:35:00].

Purpose, life satisfaction, and the “meaning of life” enter the discussion around [1:40:00]. Huberman and Castel consider how people should not simply live by default and wait until old age to think about purpose, pain, and memory. Castel notes that many older adults report good life satisfaction, whereas midlife people may be more likely to feel lacking. They discuss bucket-list-type experiences such as travel to Sedona, Yosemite, Big Sur, and national parks, but the deeper point is that goals and meaningful experiences help organize life. Huberman recommends Yosemite’s Clouds Rest hike with practical cautions: start early because descent may otherwise happen in the dark, and bring water because there is no water along the way [1:45:00].

Social relationships reappear as a central element of aging well [1:50:00]. Castel notes that people may have many acquaintances or social media friends, but far fewer people they would call weekly to ask how they are doing. As people age, social circles tend to shrink because people move away or die. However, research suggests that the quality of remaining relationships can improve. This is another nonlinear aspect of aging: fewer relationships may be deeper and more meaningful [1:50:00].

Castel reflects on why aging became interesting to him and how observing older relatives shaped his curiosity [1:55:00]. He remembers grandparents confusing his name with his brother’s while also remembering detailed information such as the price of bananas. He interprets this as a matter of focus, era, and priorities rather than simply “old versus young.” Aging is progressive change: some traits become more pronounced, while people may also become better at emotional regulation and at doing what matters to them [1:55:00].

Curiosity is presented as a mechanism that remains powerful later in life [2:00:00]. Castel describes research showing that older adults remember what they are most curious about and are also good at forgetting what they did not care about in the first place. Huberman and Castel treat this as potentially beneficial: people may want to remember meaningful people and topics while ignoring much of the low-value information that arrives through phones or email. Castel notes that he may take longer than his daughter to learn a piano piece, but he can be more selective about what he tries to learn, what is in his wheelhouse, and what is just beyond it [2:00:00].

The conversation also touches on how people think about lifespan and whether they live as if they have a long or short arc ahead [2:05:00]. Huberman speculates that people may have some intuitive sense, based on parents, grandparents, and personal health, of how long they are likely to live, and that this may shape whether they “pack more in” or take a slower arc. Castel calls this an interesting conjecture but notes that accidents and injuries can always change the trajectory [2:05:00]. The transcript does not establish this as evidence-based biology; it is a reflective discussion.

Curiosity has a darker side in the context of scams and fraud [2:10:00]. Castel says his lab studies curiosity using controlled trivia questions, but he sees implications for lifelong learning in hobbies such as hiking, bird watching, or skateboarding even at 70 or 80. The flip side is that curiosity can open “Pandora’s box” when an offer seems too good to be true, limited-time, or emotionally urgent. He gives examples of scams involving fake offers or kidnapping claims, including a supposed grandchild’s voice on the line asking for help. He notes that voice capture from online posts can be used in these scams [2:10:00]. This links memory, emotion, attention, technology, and aging-related vulnerability.

The episode then returns to wisdom and experience [2:15:00]. Castel gives examples of advice from teachers and rabbis that stayed with him. He also uses the case of pilot Chesley “Sully” Sullenberger, who landed a plane on water after both engines failed after takeoff from New York City. Castel frames it as a wisdom dilemma: would one prefer a younger pilot with faster reaction times and perhaps better raw memory, or an older pilot with extensive experience? The point is that wisdom can have practical value when novel crises require judgment built from experience [2:15:00].

Near the end, Castel and Huberman discuss what information people need to remember versus what they can look up [2:20:00]. Castel suggests people do not need every detail in memory, but they need general knowledge, trusted aids, and trusted sources. General understanding often comes from once having learned many details, even if the details are later forgotten. Huberman gives the example of rereading a neurobiology textbook and recognizing material from years of experience while thinking about it differently now. This suggests that revisiting familiar material can be enriching, though Huberman also questions whether he might be defaulting to what is easiest rather than challenging himself with something unfamiliar [2:20:00].

The episode closes with Huberman emphasizing that Castel’s work is immediately applicable and focuses on a population — older adults — that exists everywhere but does not always receive enough research attention [2:25:00]. He notes that beyond longevity and preservation, Castel studies what is unique and valuable about people in later decades of life and what everyone can learn from that, assuming they are fortunate enough to reach those ages.

Key Takeaways

  • Learning improves when people actively try, fail, and correct. Castel’s Apple logo example shows that repeated exposure is weak compared with retrieval attempts. Drawing the logo from memory, noticing uncertainty, and then checking the real logo creates better learning than passive viewing [0:00].
  • Names are hard because they are often arbitrary. Mnemonics can help by creating meaningful links, such as “Castel” with “pastel,” but they add cognitive load and can produce errors, such as remembering the cue instead of the correct name [5:00].
  • Seeing is not the same as noticing. People may pass emergency exits daily without knowing where they are. Active exploration — getting up and finding the exit — makes the information more likely to be encoded [10:00].
  • Curiosity helps close knowledge gaps. Castel frames curiosity as the motivation that arises when a person knows where they are and senses where they want to be. This supports learning in hobbies, conversation, observation, and lifelong interests, not just test preparation [15:00].
  • Changing context can reduce interference and improve recall. Different rooms, seats, or physical perspectives can make memories more distinct. Metacognitive insight means noticing what helps you learn and adjusting your environment accordingly [20:00].
  • Memory is not a perfect recording, even when vivid. Visual memory and eyewitness memory can feel highly detailed but still be wrong. A lineup identification can contaminate memory so that the identified person replaces the original perception [25:00], [30:00].
  • Everyday memory can be safety-critical. Remembering a hotel fire escape route, medication, or a future obligation can matter more than remembering a name. Castel treats these practical memories as central rather than trivial [35:00], [40:00].
  • Routines can create dangerous blind spots. Habits allow efficient behavior, but they can override unusual intentions, as in tragic cases where a parent forgets a child in the back seat while driving a familiar route to work [45:00].
  • Aging is highly variable and not just decline. Some older adults function well despite age or even brain pathology, while some younger older adults struggle. Biological age alone does not predict cognitive function [50:00].
  • Balance, movement, and exercise are part of cognitive aging. Falls can drastically affect independence and health. Balance is trainable through simple one-leg practice, yoga, tai chi, and varied movement such as hiking [55:00], [1:00:00].
  • Older adults may become more selective learners. Castel’s curiosity research suggests older adults remember what they care about and may be good at forgetting what they do not value. Selectivity can be an advantage, especially in a noisy information environment [2:00:00].
  • Wisdom can compensate for slower raw speed. The Sully Sullenberger example illustrates that experience-based judgment can matter more than youthful reaction time in complex, high-stakes situations [2:15:00].

Practical Notes

To learn something more effectively, do not only reread or rewatch it. Try to retrieve it first, even if you expect to be wrong. Castel’s Apple logo exercise suggests a simple workflow: attempt from memory, identify uncertainty, check the source, and then encode the corrected details [0:00]. The same principle can apply to names, routes, concepts, and procedures.

For names, use associations only when the name matters enough to justify the effort. A rhyme, story, occupation link, or distinctive feature can help, but the transcript emphasizes the tradeoff: mnemonic links consume attention and can introduce errors [5:00]. In social contexts, it may be more important to remember the substance of a conversation or whether the person seemed trustworthy.

For safety-related memory, engage physically with the environment. If an emergency exit matters, do not merely glance at a sign or map. Find the exit, walk the route, and mentally rehearse conditions such as smoke or darkness. Huberman describes doing this in hotels by checking the “you are here” map, locating the nearest exit, sometimes walking the hall, and rehearsing turns before sleeping [35:00].

Use external reminders for prospective memory. The transcript supports phone reminders, car reminders, and other offloading tools for future intentions [45:00]. This is especially important when an unusual task interrupts a strong routine, such as needing to stop at daycare on a normal commute route. The risk is not only poor memory; it is autopilot.

Change context when learning feels stale or memories blur together. Sitting in different places, studying in different rooms, or changing physical perspective can reduce interference and increase distinctiveness [20:00]. The tradeoff is that change can be uncomfortable at first.

Test balance before assuming it is fine. Castel describes a simple self-check: stand on one leg for at least 10 seconds, with something nearby to hold for safety [55:00]. Closing the eyes makes it harder, but the transcript indicates Castel avoids that version with older groups because of fall risk. Balance training can include standing on one leg, yoga, tai chi, and varied walking or hiking surfaces [55:00], [1:00:00].

Treat physical pain or limitation as potentially modifiable rather than automatically age-determined. Castel’s example is that if one knee hurts and the other does not, “age” alone is not a sufficient explanation because both knees are the same age [1:00:00]. He mentions physical therapy and varied exercise as useful ways to address function.

For older adults or anyone vulnerable to scams, be cautious when curiosity is triggered by urgency, scarcity, emotional threat, or an offer that seems too good to be true. Castel specifically mentions scams involving limited-time offers, fake kidnappings, and captured voices of relatives [2:10:00]. A practical implication supported by the discussion is to pause and verify through trusted sources before acting.

For lifelong learning, choose targets based on curiosity and selectivity. Castel notes that he may learn piano more slowly than his daughter, but he can be deliberate about what is in his wheelhouse and what is just beyond it [2:00:00]. This suggests a practical balance: pursue topics that matter enough to sustain effort, while still challenging yourself.

For aging well, the transcript repeatedly supports attention to social connection, love, purpose, balance, movement, and meaningful goals. Wooden’s advice emphasized love and balance [1:10:00]. Castel notes that social circles may shrink with age but relationship quality can improve [1:50:00]. Planning does not need to be rigid, but goals can motivate action and orient a person toward the future [1:20:00], [1:25:00].
