The functionality of Zigbee devices in Home Assistant depends heavily on manufacturer, community, and maintainer support.

BeardedTinker · 2026-07-08T15:00:32+00:00 · Why Some Zigbee Devices Work So Much Better

What This Video Is About

This video addresses the common frustration users experience when integrating various Zigbee devices into Home Assistant, where many devices fail to perform as expected despite using the same underlying protocol. The central argument is that poor device performance or limited usability within Home Assistant is rarely an inherent flaw in the Zigbee protocol itself. Instead, the issue stems from a lack of robust support across three interconnected layers: the manufacturer, the community, and the software maintainers.

The discussion contrasts devices from different manufacturers, such as Tuya and Sonoff, to illustrate how ecosystem support dictates functionality. The speaker emphasizes that device support is not a simple binary state (supported or unsupported); rather, it exists on various levels of actual useful functionality. Understanding these layers allows users to evaluate the long-term viability and practical utility of Zigbee hardware beyond just its initial pairing success.

Detailed Walkthrough

The video begins by establishing that two devices using the same protocol can offer vastly different experiences [0:01]. One functions perfectly with all features exposed and behaving as expected, while the other offers minimal functionality, such as only reporting battery percentage [0:08]. The speaker immediately dismisses blaming Zigbee or Home Assistant itself, noting that this video is not a comparison between ZHA versus Zigbee to MQTT, though it acknowledges that Zigbee to MQTT can sometimes offer broader support for unusual devices [0:49].

The core thesis is introduced: the functionality of Zigbee devices in Home Assistant depends on three pillars—the manufacturer, the community, and the maintainers [2:10]. If all three layers are strong, the user experience is positive. However, when any layer fails, problems arise quickly. Manufacturers who genuinely care about interoperability provide documentation and expose functionality correctly; conversely, others ship hardware and disappear [2:32]. The community acts as a vital resource, building custom quirks and reverse-engineering devices to share fixes [2:51]. Layer three involves the software maintainers, where contributors build these necessary custom quirks. A significant problem noted is when these pull requests sit unaddressed for months, leading contributors to give up, which hinders ecosystem momentum [3:04].

The speaker then dives into Tuya devices as a case study in inconsistency. While some are fine, buying Tuya can feel like gambling because two nearly identical devices might have vastly different levels of exposed functionality; one may work beautifully while the other reports nonsense and exposes only basic entities [4:12]. A specific example is an infrared blaster that pairs successfully with ZHA, showing battery percentage and a switch entity, but fails to expose its primary functions—learning and sending infrared commands—making it practically useless [4:37-5:09]. This leads to the concept of device support existing on levels rather than being binary; pairing success does not equate to useful functionality [5:12].

In contrast, Sonoff devices are shown to be better because they provide official custom quirks that actively bridge the gap between the hardware and Home Assistant. The speaker demonstrates how installing an official Sonoff custom quirk can transform a device's experience. For instance, applying an official custom quirk to a Sonoff temperature and humidity sensor results in more entities, better functionality, and a more complete device support profile compared to its limited state [6:14-7:07]. This improvement is not theoretical; the speaker uses these devices in real-world applications like irrigation systems where proper support makes them genuinely valuable within Home Assistant [7:31-7:52].

The conclusion reinforces that users should stop basing purchasing decisions solely on price or marketing promises and instead evaluate the ecosystem support across all three layers: manufacturer, ZHA/Zigbee2MQTT support, and community backing [8:14-8:47]. The hidden problem is not Zigbee itself, but everything surrounding it.

Key Takeaways

  • The usability of a Zigbee device in Home Assistant is determined by the strength of its ecosystem support, not just the protocol or hardware specifications.
  • Device functionality exists on levels; successful pairing does not guarantee that all useful features are exposed to Home Assistant.
  • Manufacturer commitment matters: some vendors actively support interoperability and provide documentation, while others do not.
  • The community plays a crucial role by creating custom quirks and reverse-engineering devices when official support is lacking.
  • Software maintainer momentum is critical; delays in integrating community-built fixes can stall ecosystem growth.
  • Official vendor custom quirks (like those from Sonoff) significantly enhance device capabilities beyond basic pairing, transforming partially useful hardware into valuable components.
  • Users should evaluate the manufacturer's support level and the availability of ZHA/Zigbee2MQTT support before purchasing Zigbee gear.

Practical Notes

To implement official custom quirks for a device like a Sonoff sensor:

  • Add the ZHA line to your configuration.yaml file.
  • Set enable_quirks equal to true.
  • Specify the path using custom_quirks_path, pointing to a directory (e.g., /config/zha_quirks).
  • Paste the official quirk file obtained from the manufacturer's documentation or website into that specified subfolder.
