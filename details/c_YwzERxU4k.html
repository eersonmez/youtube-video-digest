Charity Majors discusses the evolution of software observability and its relationship with modern engineering practices.

Scott Hanselman · 2026-06-23T05:23:38+00:00 · "Observabilitying" the Future of Software with Charity Majors

What This Video Is About

This video features an interview with Charity Majors, CEO and co-founder of Honeycomb, discussing the evolution of software observability in the context of modern engineering practices, particularly the rise of AI code generation. Majors argues that true observability transcends simply collecting fragmented metrics, logs, and traces; it requires structured, connective data—specifically traces with spans. She posits that telemetry for software a team controls is fundamentally a product problem, not just an infrastructure concern. The discussion also touches upon how AI accelerates code creation but necessitates robust feedback loops to manage the resulting complexity, emphasizing the enduring value of human judgment in engineering teams.

Detailed Walkthrough

Charity Majors begins by addressing the current state of software development where code generation is outpacing human comprehension [0:02]. She cautions against letting code "wash over you" and stresses that one cannot simply "vibe into production" without proper oversight [0:15-0:27].

A key realization she highlights is that modern observability requires structured, connective data, which she refers to as traces with spans [2:30-2:38]. She argues that while metrics and logs are useful—describing the "exhaust pipe of your infrastructure" (telemetry you don't control)—the telemetry for software *you* control is a product problem [2:51-3:09].

Majors uses the analogy of debugging. Previously, engineers relied on simple print statements like console.writeline ("Got here number two") to locate issues—a process she describes as "got here debugging at scale" [3:48-3:57]. She contrasts this with modern observability, citing an example where she used observability across an entire system via an MCP server and agent loop to solve a problem in minutes [4:14-4:22].

She emphasizes that the most critical concept yet to be grasped is that observability is a product problem requiring deep contextual understanding. Separating data bits describing the same thing means they can never be knitted back together; only when knit together can they be analyzed across different dashboards [5:37-5:48]. She notes that with tools like OpenTelemetry (OTel) and auto instrumentation, it is now genuinely possible to build faster *with* telemetry than without it [6:03-6:19].

The conversation then shifts to the historical difficulty of creating feedback loops. In pre-AI software engineering, instrumentng code required deep knowledge of data types (metric counter vs. gauge, etc.), and once deployed, finding and visualizing that data was a massive cognitive load [6:47-7:33]. AI is presented as a tool to help close this loop by providing understanding during the writing process [7:54-8:03].

Majors discusses her second edition of "Observability Engineering," noting it has expanded significantly from its first 250-page version, now exceeding 600 pages, and is an entirely rewritten book [9:15-10:27]. The book covers six sections, moving beyond the difference between monitoring (knowing if a system is up or down) and observability (reasoning from first principles about code) [11:08-11:23].

A major theme is the necessity of feedback loops for AI agents. She draws an analogy to training monkeys; you cannot just generate output, you must observe it and evaluate it against a desired outcome like Shakespeare to achieve reinforcement learning [12:59-13:18]. Therefore, implementing AI agents on brownfield systems lacking observability is described as being "deeply screwed" [13:31-13:35].

Finally, Majors addresses organizational change. She asserts that successful technical transformations require trust and credibility within the organization—people must believe what you say will happen [16:27-16:40]. Furthermore, she stresses the importance of investing in junior engineers because human judgment is irreplaceable; if companies only hire seniors due to AI handling "easy stuff," they are fundamentally flawed [27:05-28:13]. She concludes that clarity and communication—not just a specific programming language—is the programming language of the future [19:01-19:20].

Key Takeaways

  • True observability demands structured, connective data (traces with spans), moving beyond fragmented metrics or logs.
  • Telemetry for software you control is fundamentally a product problem requiring deep contextual understanding.
  • AI accelerates code generation but necessitates stronger feedback loops to prevent system failures and manage complexity.
  • The unit of work in modern development has shifted from the traditional transaction to the trace, which is structured data.
  • Observability enables "got here debugging at scale," replacing manual bisect methods with automated systemic analysis.
  • Successful technical transformations depend on building trust and credibility within the organization, not just implementing tools.
  • Investing in junior engineers is crucial because human judgment remains irreplaceable despite advancements in AI.
  • Production deployments should be treated as experiments, utilizing tooling like feature flags to safely test in production environments.

Practical Notes

  • Data Structure: Focus on making data "connectable" and "united," rather than fragmented across different signal types (metrics, logs, traces).
  • Tooling/Concepts: OpenTelemetry (OTel) is mentioned as a tool aiding this process. Timelines are a feature that knits together disparate events from various agents over time [20:31-21:06].
  • AI Implementation Risk: Implementing AI agents or agentic loops on brownfield systems without existing observability infrastructure is highly risky and likely to fail.
  • Organizational Strategy: For senior engineers driving change, success hinges on establishing trust with peers and vendors; for internal teams, maintaining credibility by delivering on promises cuts through bureaucracy [16:32-16:40].
  • Development Practice: Treat every deployment as a production experiment, leveraging techniques like feature flags to control exposure incrementally.
