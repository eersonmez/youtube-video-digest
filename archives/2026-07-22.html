Boosting Software Efficiency : A Case Study of 100% Performance Improvement in ... - Gili Kamma

Gili Kamma describes taking over a legacy embedded water-metering concentrator in 2017, a 32 MB RAM Linux/C++/Qt system that suffered unexplained resets, missing data, and poor scalability. He instrumented C++ new/delete to measure allocation rates, peaks, sizes, leaks, and fragmentation, then added an internal simulator that multiplied real meter messages to reproduce production load; 6,000 simulated meters exposed a crash. Two threads were simultaneously building roughly 5 MB messages, so serializing them halved peak demand, while fixed-size or static buffers and smaller messages further improved capacity. To diagnose and contain failures, he forwarded the last 100 log lines after resets, queued measurements in nonvolatile memory until acknowledged, and reinitialized the UART when an adjacent RF receiver reset and caused silence. Proactive summaries, automated monitoring, and long-running tests helped deliver eight months without resets, support for 10,000 meters, and releases every two to three months compared with the previous three-engineer, six-month effort.

  • Allocation instrumentation exposed memory leaks, fragmentation, and peak-memory behavior.
  • A 10-line internal simulator multiplied real meter traffic by up to 100 times and reproduced the 6,000-meter crash.
  • Serializing large message creation and using fixed-size or static buffers reduced peak memory from about 10 MB to 5 MB and more than doubled capacity.
  • Nonvolatile acknowledgment-based queuing limited data loss to the collection interval and survived prolonged network outages.
  • Reset-log forwarding, UART reinitialization, proactive summaries, and 24/7 tests produced eight months without resets and support for 10,000 meters.
