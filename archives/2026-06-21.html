Scott Hanselman interviews Zed founder Nathan Sobo about DeltaDB and rethinking version control for collaborative, agent-assisted programming.

Scott Hanselman · 2026-06-21T23:17:37+00:00

The space between the Commits with Zed and DeltaDB's Nathan Sobo

Scott Hanselman talks with Nathan Sobo, founder of Zed, about editor monocultures, the history from Atom and Electron to Visual Studio Code, and why Zed was built by reinventing the stack for a faster, more intentional developer experience. The discussion then focuses on DeltaDB, Zed’s new version-control layer that builds on Git rather than replacing its core snapshot model. Sobo explains that Git captures commits, while DeltaDB records and indexes the work that happens between commits: edits, agent conversations, work trees, references, and collaboration context. The goal is to make work trees shareable, trace code back to the conversations that produced it, jump to exact historical code moments through “portals,” and follow code as it moves across files. They also discuss privacy concerns, possible summarization or selective sharing, and early access at zed.dev/deltadb.

  • Nathan Sobo created Atom at GitHub and later founded Zed to build a faster editor stack.
  • Zed was motivated by dissatisfaction with Electron-based editor performance and VS Code dominance.
  • DeltaDB is presented as a layer on top of Git that records fine-grained deltas between commits.
  • It can preserve agent conversations, edits, and work-tree state as collaborative artifacts.
  • DeltaDB aims to let developers trace code to its originating conversation and follow it through history.
  • Sobo emphasizes privacy controls and selective sharing to avoid workplace-surveillance dynamics.

Ed Rogers discusses Bristol Braille's work making digital braille accessible through open source technology.

Scott Hanselman · 2026-06-21T22:35:57+00:00

Braille Is Freedom with Bristol Braille's Ed Rogers

In this discussion, Ed Rogers, founder of Bristol Braille, addresses the historical decline in Braille literacy and how modern technology can bridge accessibility gaps. He explains that while screen readers are fast, they don't always provide a tactile experience, which is why devices like the Canute Console Premium were developed. These devices use innovative, low-cost mechanical designs—avoiding expensive pins—to present information across multiple lines, unlike traditional single-line displays. Rogers emphasizes that Braille is not just for the visually impaired but represents a structured way to interact with complex data like tables and poetry. The company promotes an open-source approach, allowing users to modify hardware and software, fostering a community of makers who can build accessible tools.

  • Braille technology utilizes six dots per character representing nearly every alphabet globally.
  • The Canute Console Premium is an open source product designed for computer interaction using Braille.
  • Bristol Braille employs innovative mechanical designs with octagons instead of traditional pins to reduce cost.
  • The company advocates for an open-source philosophy to ensure accessibility tools are not locked down by proprietary systems.
  • Braille allows users to interact with structured data like tables and poetry in ways linear text cannot.

Charity Majors discusses the evolution of observability in software engineering.

Scott Hanselman · 2026-06-21T22:07:58+00:00

"Observabilitying" the Future of Software with Charity Majors

Charity Majors, CEO and co-founder of Honeycomb, explains that modern observability requires more than just metrics, logs, and traces; it demands structured, connectable data, which she views as a product problem for controlled software. She contrasts this with traditional methods like grepping log files from decades ago. The discussion highlights how AI is changing the landscape, but emphasizes the need for better feedback loops between code and production telemetry. Majors also discusses her new book on observability engineering, stressing that first principles are crucial in an era of rapid content generation. Furthermore, she advocates for investing in junior engineers to develop judgment, as AI cannot replace human experience and iterative learning.

  • Observability is a product problem for controlled software.
  • Structured, connectable data (traces) is key to modern observability.
  • AI requires better feedback loops, not just faster code generation.
  • Junior engineers are vital for developing necessary judgment.
  • Trust and reciprocity are essential skills in the age of AI.

Mike Krieger discusses why breakthrough consumer AI startups may be difficult to build in today’s consolidated app market.

Alex Kantrowitz · 2026-06-21T16:23:19+00:00

Mike Krieger on Why Most AI Startups Will Struggle

In this excerpt, Mike Krieger compares the environment for launching consumer apps during Instagram’s early days with the current landscape for AI startups. He says that when Instagram was released, the app ecosystem still felt like a “wild west,” where two relatively unknown founders could launch an app and reach number one in photos and video within about three months. He argues that this kind of breakout is much harder now because user attention is concentrated among dominant apps, especially platforms like TikTok and Reels. Krieger suggests that creating a truly breakthrough consumer AI experience is very difficult, and incremental improvements may not be enough to make users switch. For example, even a document editor that is twice as good because of AI may not persuade people to move all their existing work.

  • Instagram launched in a less consolidated app market.
  • Consumer attention is now concentrated in major apps like TikTok and Reels.
  • Krieger says breakout consumer AI products are hard to achieve.
  • A modestly better AI tool may not overcome switching costs.
  • The discussion focuses on challenges facing AI startups.
