How I’m Starting a New Smart Home from Scratch!

The video is a practical rebuild of a smart home from first principles. He starts with the network because unreliable connectivity makes every smart device worse: the ISP all-in-one box goes into bridge mode, a stronger router/Wi-Fi setup takes over, as many devices as possible are wired through Ethernet and switches, and smart home hubs/bridges sit on the wired backbone. For the automation platform, he favors Home Assistant because it can combine Matter, Z-Wave, and other systems beyond the limits of Apple/Amazon/Google-only setups. His device-selection rule is to automate the most annoying problems in high-traffic areas first. In the living room, that means solving ceiling-fan/light shadow issues and pendant-light inconvenience with smart switches, scenes, and button actions. He recommends building scenes first so dashboards, button presses, and automations all point to one reusable source of truth. A key implementation lesson is Home Assistant automation mode: the first family test failed when rapid button presses were ignored, and changing the automation mode fixed responsiveness. He also adds desk-light automation based on whether the computer is on and connected to a second screen, while warning against over-automating low-value annoyances.

  • A reliable network is the foundation: bridge the ISP box, use stronger routing/Wi-Fi, and wire high-value devices where possible.
  • Home Assistant is chosen for flexibility, especially when Z-Wave or other non-Matter devices are needed.
  • Pick first automations by combining annoyance level with how often the area is used.
  • Create reusable scenes before wiring up buttons, dashboards, and automations; update the scene once instead of every trigger.
  • Automation mode matters in Home Assistant: single-mode automations can drop rapid button presses, so responsiveness must be tested with real users.
  • The best smart-home setup preserves dumb-home reliability while removing repeated annoyances.
