Improve Flexibility with Research-Supported Stretching Protocols | Huberman Lab Essentials

Huberman explains flexibility as a neural, muscular, and connective-tissue problem rather than just a muscle-length problem. He walks through muscle spindles, Golgi tendon organs, spinal reflexes, the insula, and von Economo neurons to show how the nervous system protects range of motion and can also be trained to tolerate stretching. He distinguishes dynamic, ballistic, static, and PNF stretching, then says long-term range-of-motion gains are best supported by static stretching, including PNF-style approaches. The practical protocol is simple: warm up first, use static holds around 30 seconds, do roughly three sets per muscle group, repeat about five days per week, and keep the intensity low. He stresses that low-intensity static stretching around 30-40% of the pain threshold may be more effective and safer than forcing painful ranges. Yoga is discussed as a broader practice that may improve flexibility, pain tolerance, and nervous-system control.

  • Flexibility depends on nervous-system reflexes and safety mechanisms, not just muscle tissue.
  • Static stretching appears best for durable range-of-motion gains; dynamic and ballistic stretching have different roles around movement preparation.
  • A concrete protocol is three 30-second static holds per target muscle group, about five days per week, after an adequate warm-up.
  • The stretch should feel mild and controllable, not painful; very low intensity may outperform moderate-intensity stretching for range-of-motion gains.
  • He recommends adapting the endpoint day by day based on warmth, stress, and body state rather than chasing a fixed distance.
