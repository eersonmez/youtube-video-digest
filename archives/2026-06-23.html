Why Italians Saw the Pope Very Differently - Ada Palmer

Ada Palmer argues that in this period the pope was not only a spiritual leader but also a temporal ruler who fought wars, even against other Catholics. She emphasizes that geography shaped how Catholics perceived him. For people in distant places like Denmark or Iceland, the pope appeared remote, majestic, and sacred, encountered mainly through impressive papal envoys, ceremonies, and public honors. For Italians, however, the pope was a familiar local political figure: someone whose youth, family, vices, rivalries, and personal history were widely known. Italians also directly experienced the patronage and disruption that followed a new pope’s rise, as his relatives and allies gained offices while others were displaced. This proximity made it easier for Italians to see the pope as a flawed, ambitious political actor rather than only as an exalted religious authority.

  • The pope held both spiritual and temporal power.
  • Catholics could find themselves fighting wars against the pope.
  • Distant Europeans often encountered the pope through ceremony and envoys.
  • Italians knew the pope as a local elite with personal flaws and family ties.
  • Papal elections reshaped Italian patronage networks and offices.

Who Actually Needs Magnesium? Which Type Is Right For You (Doctor Explains)

This educational video, not personal medical advice, explains that severe magnesium deficiency is uncommon but many people may fall into a “gray zone” of suboptimal magnesium that standard blood tests can miss because most magnesium is stored in cells, muscle, and bone. Dr. Leonid Kim links low or suboptimal magnesium to fatigue, poor sleep, stress sensitivity, cramps, blood pressure, insulin resistance, migraines, and long-term bone concerns. He says modern diets low in leafy greens, legumes, nuts, seeds, and whole grains, plus stress, alcohol, proton pump inhibitors, diuretics, diabetes, and gastrointestinal issues can increase risk. He reviews forms: glycinate for sleep or stress, citrate or oxide for constipation, oxide also for migraines, malate for possible muscle fatigue or cramps, taurate for theoretical cardiovascular and metabolic support, and L-threonate, including Magtein, for possible brain effects but with limited evidence.

  • Severe deficiency is different from suboptimal magnesium status.
  • Blood magnesium can look normal while tissue stores are low.
  • Diet, stress, alcohol, PPIs, diuretics, and diabetes can increase risk.
  • Glycinate is often favored for sleep, stress, and tolerability.
  • Citrate and oxide are more useful when constipation is a goal.
  • Evidence for malate, taurate, and L-threonate is promising but limited.

Scott Hanselman · 2026-06-23T07:30:27+00:00

The space between the Commits with Zed and DeltaDB's Nathan Sobo

Scott Hanselman interviews Nathan Sobo, founder of Zed and creator of Atom, about building developer tools outside current monocultures such as Chromium-based apps, VS Code, and Git-only workflows. Sobo explains why Zed was built as a faster, more native-feeling editor rather than another Electron-based environment. The discussion then focuses on DeltaDB, which aims to extend version control beyond Git commits by recording the process between snapshots: edits, abandoned work, agent prompts, conversations, work trees, and collaboration context. Sobo describes DeltaDB as a Git-compatible superset that tracks fine-grained deltas, enabling developers and agents to revisit exact moments in code, follow code as it moves, and share agent conversations across teammates and time zones. They also discuss privacy concerns, possible controls for sharing or summarizing conversation history, and early access through zed.dev/deltadb.

  • Zed was created to challenge Electron-based editor performance and UX limits.
  • DeltaDB is presented as a Git-compatible superset, not a total replacement for Git.
  • It versions the activity between commits, including edits, prompts, and agent conversations.
  • Work trees and agent sessions become collaborative artifacts that teammates can join later.
  • Fine-grained deltas allow code to be traced across time, movement, deletion, and refactoring.
  • Sobo acknowledges privacy risks and discusses controls for sharing only selected history.
