The biological clock that doomed the Neanderthals - David Reich

Dwarkesh Patel · 2026-05-23T22:31:02+00:00
Evolutionary Genetics & Neanderthal Incompatibility
Geneticist David Reich discusses a model where the evolutionary distance between human lineages acts as a biological clock. Interactions 300,000 years ago (with a shorter separation of ~400,000 years) allowed for successful interbreeding, whereas interactions 70,000 years ago (with a separation of ~1.2 million years) resulted in severe biological incompatibilities. Offspring produced at this stage had low fitness, contributing to the ultimate doom and extinction of the Neanderthals.
  • The 'biological clock' refers to the accumulation of biological incompatibilities as human lineages diverge over evolutionary time.
  • Around 300,000 years ago, when the evolutionary separation between modern humans and Neanderthal ancestors was shorter (about 400,000 years), interbreeding was biologically viable and resulted in successful cultural diffusion.
  • Around 70,000 years ago, the evolutionary separation had grown to approximately 1.2 million years.
  • At this wider separation, biological incompatibilities were severe enough that offspring from interbreeding had very low fitness, preventing successful integration and contributing to the doom of the Neanderthals.
  • This theory is a model-based expectation and prediction rather than a direct empirical observation from archaeological or genetic records.
